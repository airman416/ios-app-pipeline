## Core Thesis

**Traction trumps everything.** Traction is quantitative evidence of customer demand — growing downloads, growing revenue, growing transactions. The single biggest reason startups fail is not bad product but bad distribution. Most failed startups have a product; they don't have customers. As Marc Andreessen says: *"The number one reason that we pass on entrepreneurs we'd otherwise like to back is they're focusing on product to the exclusion of everything else. Many entrepreneurs who build great products simply don't have a good distribution strategy. Even worse is when they insist that they don't need one, or call \[their\] no distribution strategy a 'viral marketing strategy.'"*

Paul Graham's definition is the operating one: *"A startup is a company designed to grow fast. … The only essential thing is growth. Everything else we associate with startups follows from growth."*

### The 50 Percent Rule

**Spend 50% of your time on product and 50% on traction, in parallel, from day one.** Not after launch. The pull to spend everything on product is strong because that's why you got into the startup. Resist it.

Why the rule works:

- Traction development pushes a stream of cold customers through the product. Early "warm" customers tell you what you want to hear. Cold customers tell you what's actually broken. The book uses a leaky bucket metaphor: your product is a bucket with holes, you pour traction water in, the leaks are where the holes are. Plug them, repeat.
- Traction development uncovers messaging, niches, channels, and roadblocks you can't learn any other way.
- A head start on the right traction channel means you can grow rapidly on day one of launch — not after another six-month product-feedback cycle.

**Four common ways a "people-want-it" product still fails:**

1. No viable business model (no one will pay, ads can't cover bills).
2. Market too small (and no way to expand).
3. Reaching customers costs more than they're worth (mismatch between price and required sales motion).
4. Hypercompetitive market.

Following the 50% rule catches all four traps early enough to pivot.

**Example: Dropbox.** Tested SEM during product development. Acquisition cost was $230 against a $99 product. Killed it. Switched to viral marketing. Built the referral program in-product. Biggest growth driver ever since.

**Example: Marketo.** Set up SEO and a blog *before* product development. By launch they had a 14,000-buyer pipeline. *"Instead of beta testing a product, we beta tested an idea."*

### Three Phases

- **Phase I — make something people want.** Bucket is leakiest. Send small amounts of traction water through it to find the holes. Do unscalable things — guest posts, manual outreach, conferences.
- **Phase II — market something people want.** Bucket is mostly sealed (product/market fit). Scale up traction efforts. Fine-tune positioning.
- **Phase III — scale.** Established business model. Focus on dominating market and increasing earnings.

Moving the needle means different things in each phase. In Phase I, the first 100 customers count. In Phase III, you need to reach millions to add 100k.

### Moving the Needle

Set an explicit traction goal in hard numbers. Then *only* work on activities that could plausibly move the needle for that goal. Weinberg's own DuckDuckGo mistake: he ranked #1 for "new search engine" — but only 50 people searched it per day. He needed 5,000 visitors/day. The channel worked technically but couldn't move the needle.

### How Much Traction Is Enough for Investors

A moving target. Naval Ravikant: *"It is a moving target. The entire ecosystem is getting far more efficient. … The definition of traction keeps changing as the environment gets competitive. That's why it is actually useful to look at AngelList and look at companies who just got funded; that will give you an idea of where the bar is right now."*

Practical rule: contact investors who already understand your space — they'll extrapolate from less. Friends and family invest in you, not the traction. Sustainable engagement growth (e.g., "100 customers but 10% monthly growth for 6 months") is hard for any investor to ignore.

### To Pivot Or Not To Pivot

Most startups give up too early. DuckDuckGo took 4 years before its core differentiator (privacy) became a mainstream issue (the 2013 NSA leaks). Founders are usually too early, not too late. Before pivoting, look for **bright spots** — pockets of customers who are deeply engaged. If they exist, dig into why and try to expand from that base. If they don't, pivot.

## The Bullseye Framework

Three-step framework for finding the one traction channel that will get you traction. Peter Thiel quote that opens the chapter: *"It is very likely that one channel is optimal. Most businesses actually get zero distribution channels to work. Poor distribution—not product—is the number one cause of failure. If you can get even a single distribution channel to work, you have great business. If you try for several but don't nail one, you're finished."*

### Step 1 — Outer Ring: What's Possible (Brainstorm)

Brainstorm one credible channel strategy for **every** one of the 19 channels. The outer ring exists specifically to counter your biases. If you skip channels you don't know, you'll never find the underutilized one that beats your competitors. The most underutilized channel in an industry is often the most promising.

Distinction: a *traction channel* is one of the 19 (e.g., Social and Display Ads). A *channel strategy* is a specific play within a channel (e.g., running ads on reddit specifically). At the outer-ring stage you generate one strategy per channel.

Research input: study how every successful company in your space got customers; study how failed ones wasted marketing dollars.

### Step 2 — Middle Ring: What's Probable (Test)

Promote 2–3 most promising outer-ring strategies into the middle ring. Stop when there's an obvious drop-off in excitement (usually around the third channel).

For each middle-ring channel, design a **cheap test** that answers three questions:

1. How much will it cost to acquire customers through this channel?
2. How many customers are available through this channel?
3. Are the customers you're getting through this channel the kind you want right now?

**Test economics in Phase I: under $1,000 and under one month per channel test.** Often much cheaper. Run them in parallel, not sequentially. Mistake to avoid: prematurely scaling. You're not trying to win the channel yet — you're trying to determine if it *could* move the needle. Run 4 Facebook ads, not 40.

### Step 3 — Inner Ring: What's Working (Focus)

Pick the single most promising channel from middle-ring tests. Pour all your traction effort into it. Find every tactic, optimization, and sub-strategy within that one channel. Wring everything out of it.

**Mistake to avoid: distracting marketing efforts in other channels.** If SEM is your inner-ring channel and you also got some traction from trade shows and publicity in middle-ring testing, drop the trade shows and publicity. They're a distraction. Optimizing inside the dominant channel will produce more than secondary channels can.

Confusion to manage: focusing on one channel often means using *other* channels to feed into it. SEO needs publicity to get links. Viral marketing rides on email or Facebook. Content marketing uses guest posts on other blogs. One channel is dominant; others can support it.

If no middle-ring channel works, repeat Bullseye with the data you just learned. If multiple Bullseye rounds yield nothing, the product is still too leaky — go back to product.

### Ranking Criteria (across all rings)

When deciding which channels to promote, the spreadsheet columns the book recommends:

- Number of customers available through this strategy
- Conversion rate
- Cost to acquire a customer (CPA)
- Lifetime value of a customer (LTV)

Even rough guesstimates are valuable. Use them to rank strategies and verify they could plausibly move the needle for your traction goal.

### Why Bullseye Works

- Forces you to take all 19 channels seriously, including ones you'd dismiss.
- Casts a wide net but converges quickly via cheap parallel tests.
- Compatible with Lean: Lean is for product development, Bullseye is for traction. They run in parallel.

**Mint case study (the prototype Bullseye run).** Noah Kagan's traction goal: 100,000 users in 6 months. He built the quant spreadsheet (which is the figure in the book — see note above). Tested targeting blogs, publicity, and SEM in parallel. Targeting blogs was the winner. Strategies that worked inside that channel: VIP access (embed an "I Want Mint" badge → priority access → 50,000 signups from 600 bloggers), direct sponsoring of small personal blogs ($500/blog, "Can I send you $500?" subject line — 10,000+ preregistrations), and content partnerships with bigger sites like The Motley Fool. Result: 1M users within 6 months of launch. When targeting blogs maxed out, Bullseye was rerun and publicity became the new core channel.

## The CPA × LTV Math (Evaluating a Channel)

Universal numeric framework for comparing channels. The book formalizes this most clearly in the SEM chapter, but it applies to every channel.

**Core formula:**

CPA = CPC / conversion percentage

Where CPC is the per-click cost (or, more generally, cost per qualified visitor) and conversion percentage is the rate at which visitors become customers.

Example: clicks cost $1, 10% of click-throughs convert. CPA = $1 / 0.10 = $10.

**The rule:** a channel is viable when **CPA < LTV** (lifetime value of a customer). When you can pay $10 to acquire a customer worth $50, the channel is a money-printing machine. Until you hit that ratio, you're either burning cash or breakeven.

**The Inflection / Archives.com benchmark.** Matt Monahan's early SEM campaign broke even after a few weeks (CPA ≈ revenue per customer) even before optimizing landing pages and funnels. They concluded SEM would work, scaled to six figures/month, and built Archives.com on it — sold to Ancestry for $100M. The lesson: *if you can get close to breakeven after a few weeks of cheap testing, the channel will likely work after optimization.* Don't expect profitability from your first test.

**The compounding logic of small CPA gains.** Monahan: *"If keywords cost 15 cents, and you're running a Web site and for every click you're able to generate 13 cents. Well, if you scale that, that's a losing business. If you improve … and you get that 13 cents to 16 cents, now all of a sudden you have something that's more sustainable. And if you go from 16 to 20 cents, you're looking at 25 percent profit margins."* A 50% gain in conversion is the difference between losing money and a scalable business.

**Move-the-needle test.** Before running a channel test, calculate whether the channel could conceivably deliver enough customers at acceptable CPA to hit your traction goal. If 100% of available channel volume wouldn't move the needle, don't bother testing it. (DuckDuckGo's later example: tech news sites can't convert enough users to move the search market.)

## Traction Thinking Principles

### The 50% Rule (already covered above)
Half product, half traction. From day one. In parallel.

### Don't ignore a channel because it's not popular
The most *underutilized* channel in an industry is often the most promising. Jason Cohen: *"I'll bet you a lot of your competition will refuse to even try these channels. And if that's true, that's even more reason to go try those channels! It can almost be a competitive advantage (at least a temporary one) if you can acquire customers in channels that others cannot, or refuse to try. That's more interesting than duking it out with AdWords competitors in positions one to three."*

### The channel that works for your competitors may not work for you
Weinberg's DuckDuckGo mistake: he assumed SEO would work because his prior startup grew that way. Wrong. Just because your last company grew through channel X doesn't mean this one will. Just because every competitor uses Facebook doesn't mean you should. Run Bullseye fresh every time.

### Critical Path

The path to your traction goal with the fewest necessary steps. Process:

1. **Define your traction goal in hard numbers.** Examples: 1,000 paying customers, 100 daily new customers, 10% of market, 100M searches/month. Choose a goal where hitting it changes things significantly — fundability, profitability, market leadership.
2. **Enumerate the milestones absolutely necessary** to hit that goal. Could be product, hiring, or marketing milestones. *Only the necessary ones.*
3. **Assess every activity against the Critical Path.** If it's not on the path, don't do it.
4. **Reassess after each milestone.** Your original enumeration is often wrong — building A might reveal you can skip B.

Critical Path is a framework to help you decide what *not* to do. Founders waste limited resources on off-path work all the time. DuckDuckGo example: at the 100M-searches-per-month goal, images and auto-suggest weren't necessary (users were forgiving of missing features). At the 1% market share goal, they were necessary (mainstream users aren't).

### Three Sources of Channel Bias

The book names exactly three reasons founders ignore profitable channels:

1. **Out of sight, out of mind.** Speaking engagements, trade shows, offline ads — never come up because they're not in your peer group's vocabulary.
2. **Negative bias.** "I hate phone calls" → no sales. "Affiliate marketing is sleazy" → no affiliates. Just because *you* hate it doesn't mean your customers do.
3. **Bias against schlep.** Schleppy channels (BD, trade shows, community building) are slow and time-consuming. So founders skip them. Which is why they remain underutilized and lucrative.

### Be ready for a 10-year haul
A startup is potentially a decade of your life. Pick an idea you can sustain that long. Most founders feel in retrospect they picked too quickly.

### Law of Shitty Click-Throughs (Andrew Chen)
*"Over time, all marketing strategies result in shitty click-through rates."* When banner ads debuted, CTR was 75%. Today: under 1%. Every channel decays as it saturates. Constantly run small tests on new channel strategies to stay ahead. The next cutting-edge tactic is your competitive advantage; it expires.

### 1. Viral Marketing

**Definition.** Growing your customer base by getting existing customers to refer new ones. True virality means each user brings in at least one additional user. (K > 1 = exponential growth.)

**Who it works for.** Consumer products. Communication products. Products with natural social/collaborative usage. Products that can be embedded or shared. Generally works best when product itself is the carrier. Facebook, Twitter, WhatsApp, Dropbox, Instagram, Snapchat, Pinterest all grew this way.

**Core math:**

K = i × conversion percentage

- *i* = number of invites sent per user
- conversion percentage = % of recipients who become customers
- K > 1 → exponential viral growth
- K > 0.5 → still very helpful even without exponential

You can split conversion percentage into click-through % × signup % to find the weakest link.

**Viral cycle time** = how long it takes one user to send an invite that converts. YouTube's is minutes. Shortening cycle time matters more than people realize: same K + shorter cycle = vastly faster growth. To shorten: create urgency, simplify every step, reduce signup friction.

**Seven types of viral loops:**

1. **Pure word of mouth.** Product so good people tell others. Early Facebook on college campuses.
2. **Inherent virality.** Product is worthless without others (Skype, WhatsApp, Snapchat). Network effects.
3. **Collaborative.** Product better with others (Google Docs).
4. **Communicative / embedded branding.** Every outgoing message carries an ad. "Get a free email account with Hotmail." "Sent from my iPhone." Free-tier branding on MailChimp, Weebly, UserVoice, Desk.com.
5. **Incentivized.** Dropbox extra storage for referrals. Airbnb, Uber, PayPal, Gilt account credits.
6. **Embedded buttons/widgets.** YouTube embed codes. Facebook/Twitter share buttons. reddit.
7. **Social broadcast.** Spotify on Facebook, Pinterest pins broadcast to social graphs.

Combine multiple types when possible. Uber example mixes inherent (rides are often with others), collaborative (split fare), and incentivized (referral credits).

**Tactics.**
- Measure K and cycle time from day one as your baseline.
- Map the entire viral loop (every step). Cut unnecessary steps.
- A/B test relentlessly — buttons vs. links, button location/color/size, headlines, page speed, social proof, signup field count, allowing pre-signup product use, Facebook/Twitter login.
- Focus first on changes that *could* yield 5–10x improvements (whole new signup flow, new onboarding) before micro-optimizing.
- **Conversion pages must match invitation messaging** ("Mark referred you" in the invite → same on the landing page).
- **Viral pockets:** segment K by country, age, or other trait. Find subgroups already going viral and double down there. *"You may be taking off in Indonesia while not doing as well in Australia."*
- Seed the loop continually — viral loops aren't self-sustaining. Use SEO and ads to push fresh customers into the top.
- Andrew Chen's blunt advice: *"The easiest way, for a beginner—copy someone else's viral loop until yours starts to work in a similar way. Copying someone else's loop down to the detail is important, including text copy, etc."*

**Common mistakes (Andrew Chen):**
- Bolting "viral features" onto a product that isn't inherently viral.
- Bad products trying to go viral.
- Not running enough A/B tests (assume 1–3 of 10 yield positive results).
- Adding Facebook "like" buttons as if that's a strategy.
- Treating virality as a tactic instead of a deep part of product strategy.

**Case: myZamana** (Indian dating). Ashish Kundra — sends targeted emails based on user actions ("Mark liked you!"). The more people use the site, the more notifications go out. Built every viral mechanic into the product. Grew from 100k to 4M users in under a year.

**Even teams with expert engineers take 2–3 months to implement and optimize a single new viral channel** to the point where it grows without ad spend.

### 2. Public Relations (Publicity)

**Definition.** Coverage in traditional media — newspapers, magazines, TV. Distinct from unconventional PR (stunts) and content marketing.

**Who it works for.** Almost everyone in Phase I/II; press confers credibility, drives fund-raising, helps recruiting, attracts partners. Less of a needle-mover in Phase III for big consumer products (DuckDuckGo can't move the needle on tech press anymore).

**Key strategic shift (Ryan Holiday).** Stories now filter *up*, not down. *"Blogs have an enormous influence over other blogs… The smaller sites legitimize the newsworthiness of the story for the sites with bigger audiences."* Don't pitch The New York Times directly. Pitch the blog that TechCrunch reads. TechCrunch picks it up. The NYT picks it up from TechCrunch.

**Case: DonorsChoose.org.** Local NYC reports → Newsweek → Oprah names it one of her favorite things → Gates Foundation sponsorship.

**What grabs reporters:** milestones — funding raised, product launch, usage barrier broken, PR stunt, big partnership, special industry report. Bundle smaller announcements into bigger ones. New version *and* usage milestone hits harder than either alone.

**Pitch tactics.**
- Keep emails short. Reporters at TechCrunch get 50+ pitches/day. Don't send walls of text.
- Always create a story angle. *"Satisfaction is a nonviral emotion."* A good angle elicits emotion strong enough to share — ideally one that polarizes (some nod, some shake their heads). Example: a stock market book pitched as "the market is rigged" rather than "technical details of high-frequency trading."
- Ryan Holiday's template:

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey [name],

I wanted to shoot you a note because I loved your post on [similar topic that did a lot of traffic]. I was going to give the following to our publicist, but I thought I would go to you with the exclusive because I read and really enjoy your stuff. My [company built a user base of 25,000 paying customers in two months without advertising / book blows the lid off an enormous XYZ scandal]. And I did it completely off the radar. This means you would be the first to have it. I can write up any details you'd need to make it great. Do you think this might be a good fit?

If so, should I draft something around [their average] words and send it to you, or do you prefer a different process? If not, I totally understand, and thanks for reading this much.
```

**Starting tactics.**
- HARO (Help A Reporter Out) — reporters request sources, you respond, get a mention.
- Offer commentary on industry stories you're not the subject of.
- Twitter — reporters often have surprisingly few followers; engaging there gets you noticed. DuckDuckGo got into Time's top-50 sites because Gabriel built a Twitter relationship first.

**Amplification once you have a story:** submit to link-sharing sites, social ads on the post, email influencers for comment, ping space-relevant blogs that haven't covered you. Once it's hot, drag it out with "How We Did This" follow-up pieces.

**PR firms:** print reporters often ignore PR firm pitches but read founder pitches. Wait to hire one until you're really focused on publicity as a core channel.

### 3. Unconventional PR

**Definition.** Two sub-types. (a) Publicity stunts — anything engineered to get coverage. (b) Customer appreciation — small, scalable acts of being awesome to customers that generate goodwill and word of mouth.

**Who it works for.** Anyone willing to be creative and bold. Underutilized because most companies stick with conventional PR.

**Publicity stunts — examples in the book.**
- **Half.com.** Renamed the town of Halfway, Oregon to Half.com for one year. Launched on the Today show with the mayor. NYT, PBS, WSJ, hundreds of outlets. 40M+ impressions. Acquired by eBay for $300M six months later.
- **WePay.** At PayPal's developer conference, placed a 600-lb block of ice (a dig at PayPal's frozen-account complaints) at the entrance. Thousands of signups.
- **DuckDuckGo billboard** in Google's SoMa neighborhood about Google tracking. $7,000 for a month. National press from USA Today, Wired. User base doubled that month.
- **Blendtec "Will It Blend?"** Series blending rakes, golf balls, iPhones. iPhone video 8M+ views. Top-100 most viewed on YouTube. Blender company.
- **Dollar Shave Club** launch video ("Our Blades Are F\*\*king Great"). 12,000 customers in 2 days. 31,000 Facebook shares, 16,000 tweets. 1,500+ sites linked. Ranked #3 for Google "shave."

**Customer appreciation — examples in the book.**
- **Hipmunk.** Sent luggage tags + handwritten notes to the first several hundred Twitter mentioners. Mother's Day Giveaway: asked customers to say why they love mom more than Hipmunk; sent flowers/chocolates to winners' moms; $500 spend → coverage, followers, lasting fans. "Chipmunked" Facebook profile cartoons: 500 requests in an hour. Flying customers home for Thanksgiving. Facebook fans +350%.
- **reddit.** Alexis Ohanian sent T-shirts, personally emailed early users to thank them. Stories became central to early press.
- **Grasshopper.com.** David Hauser's rebrand: chocolate-covered grasshoppers to 5,000 influencers + video about entrepreneurs. $65,000 spend → Fox News, Guy Kawasaki and Kevin Rose tweets, 150+ blog posts, 200,000+ YouTube views, +3,000% social traffic. Two full-time employees dedicated to delighting customers.
- **Zappos.** Customer service classified as marketing investment. Long calls are good (they mean better service). Returns, ordering pizza for callers, swapping workout clothes for a deep-fat fryer (real example).
- **Shopify Build a Business** contest: 1,900 new customers, $3.5M in platform sales.
- **Dropbox Dropquest** online scavenger hunt: 500,000 participants in year one.

**Other Hauser stunts (more):**
- **"The New Dork"** parody of Jay-Z's "Empire State of Mind." 1M+ views. Mentioned by Ashton Kutcher, Mashable, TechCrunch. Trick: referenced specific publications in the video so they'd share it.
- **Chargify green bull** at SXSW. $3,000 stuntman instead of $10–15K sponsorship. Bull did backflips, high-fives, drove a Corvette, got kicked out. Hundreds of customers.

**Reality check.** Hauser's team has had many flops too — March Madness promos, ticket giveaways, dancing grasshopper videos that bombed. The wins more than pay for the misses, but you must accept that success in this channel is unpredictable.

### 4. Search Engine Marketing (SEM)

**Definition.** Paid ads on search engines — Google AdWords, Bing Ads. Pay-per-click.

**Who it works for.** Companies selling directly to people actively searching for solutions. Works at any phase. Especially good in Phase I as a cheap testing tool — even pre-product.

**Terminology.**
- CTR (Click-Through Rate)
- CPC (Cost Per Click)
- CPA = CPC / conversion percentage
- Quality Score — Google's reward for relevant, high-CTR ads. High quality score = better placement + cheaper clicks. CTR is the dominant factor.

**Case: Inflection / Archives.com.** Matt Monahan used SEM as a *product-research* tool *before* building. Ran AdWords ads to landing pages that tested different value props ("get access to census data" vs. "get access to your family genealogy"). Measured CTR + landing-page conversion. Decided product direction (deep ancestor count vs. "trace back to 1200s" vs. "related to celebrities"). When they finally built the product, they knew what the market wanted. Initial SEM campaign broke even in weeks. Scaled to six figures/month. Sold to Ancestry for $100M.

**Strategy.**
1. Find keywords (Google Keyword Planner, KeywordSpy, SEMrush, SpyFu).
2. Group into ad groups (e.g., one per product type).
3. Write ads with keyword in body + strong CTA.
4. Use Google Analytics URL Builder for unique tracking URLs.
5. Start with 4 ads — enough to test messaging, demos, landing pages without optimization-overhead.
6. Iterate on keywords, ad copy, demographics, landing pages, CPC.

**Long-tail keywords** (e.g., "1990 Philadelphia census data") are cheaper and convert better because less competition. Aggregate many to hit volume.

**Tactics.**
- **Content network** (non-Google sites that show Google ads) — bigger audience but harder for beginners. Expand here only after profitable on search.
- **Retargeting** (AdRoll, Perfect Audience, AdWords retargeting). Visitors see your ad elsewhere. 3–10x higher CTRs. Caveat: feels creepy with sensitive data.
- **Conversion Optimizer.** Google auto-adjusts ads to perform better after enough data.
- **Negative keywords.** Exclude irrelevant search terms ("wine glasses" if you sell eyeglasses).
- **Scripts** for automating ad management at scale.
- **Quality score management.** CTR is the dominant factor. Tailor ads to keywords (manually or via Dynamic Keyword Insertion). Average AdWords CTR is ~2%; below 1.5% Google penalizes you.

### 5. Social and Display Ads

**Definition.** Banner ads on the web + ads on social platforms (LinkedIn, Twitter, Facebook, reddit, Tumblr, YouTube, StumbleUpon, Foursquare, etc.). $15B+/year industry.

**Who it works for.** Larger consumer plays and brand building. Works at any phase. Display ads tend toward branding; social ads work especially well for audience-building → eventual conversion.

**Display ad networks.** Google Display Network (4B daily page views, 700M monthly visitors, 80%+ of online audience), Advertising.com (AOL), Tribal Fusion, Conversant, Adblade. Niche networks like The Deck (web creatives). BuySellAds = self-service direct buying on smaller sites — easy to test with small budgets.

Underutilized phase I tactic: contact small site owners directly and place a fixed-price ad. Cheap and targeted.

**Research competitor ads with MixRank, Adbeat. Audience data with Alexa, Quantcast.**

**Social ads — indirect response (Nikhil Sethi, Adaptly).** *"Instead of looking at every click and how it converts, indirect response says, 'Let's create an environment within the social context that's geared toward the specific product or service you're trying to offer, build affinity there, build loyalty there, and then migrate that audience toward some conversion element we want to occur at a later point in time.'"*

CareOne (debt relief): social-acquired customers were 179% more likely to fill out the lead form, 217% more likely to make a first payment, 680% more likely to complete a partially-filled form, 732% better first-payment rate vs. non-social leads.

**Content-driven social ads.** Nikhil's principle: *"Paid is fundamentally only as good as the content you put behind it. You should only employ social advertising dollars when you've understood that a fire is starting around your message and you want to put more oil on it."* If a piece has organic traction, paid amplification multiplies it. Otherwise paid ads waste money.

**Airbrake example.** $15 on Twitter ads on an interview-with-Stripe-CTO post → hundreds of organic retweets, tens of Facebook likes, two reddit/HN submissions, tens of thousands of visits.

**Native content distribution networks** — Outbrain, Sharethrough. Promote content on Forbes, Thought Catalog, Vice, Gothamist.

**Major social sites covered.**
- **LinkedIn.** 250M+ business professionals. Target by job title, company, industry — uniquely hard targeting elsewhere.
- **Twitter.** 250M users. Sponsored tweets in feed. Strongest tactic: paid promotion around real-time events your audience cares about (sportswear during games).
- **Facebook.** 1B+ users. Interest-, page-like-, and friend-of-friend targeting. Granular enough that Gabriel once targeted only his wife. *"When you buy a Facebook ad, you're buying more than just a targeted fan; you're buying the opportunity to access that fan's social graph."*
- **StumbleUpon.** 25M+ stumblers. Ads *are* the content — paid stumbles. Users are quick to bounce; needs immediate engagement (infographics, video).
- **Foursquare.** 45M users, location-based. Hypertargeted by location or by people who visited similar places.
- **Tumblr.** 100M+ users. Sponsored posts, reblogs.
- **reddit.** 5B monthly page views, 175M monthly uniques. Sponsored links at top of pages or sidebar. Best ads are controversial or funny — they get treated like content (upvoted, commented). Target specific subreddits (500k+ communities — r/bacon, r/gaymers).
- **YouTube.** 1B+ monthly uniques, 4B+ hours of video. Pre-roll ads, banners.
- **Others.** BuzzFeed, Scribd, SlideShare, Pinterest — newer platforms offer windows of opportunity.

### 6. Offline Ads

**Definition.** TV, radio, magazines, newspapers, yellow pages, billboards, direct mail, transit ads, infomercials. Bigger total spend than online.

**Who it works for.** Anyone reaching demographics hard to target online (seniors, less tech-savvy consumers, commuters). Underutilized by startups, so less competition. Mass-market consumer products especially. Jason Cohen used it at Smart Bear Software.

**Demographics over price.** The most important factor in an offline buy is matching the medium's demographic to your customer. Ask for the **audience prospectus (ad kit)** from any seller. Beyond demographics, choose by *sensibility* — local arts magazine readers ≠ top-40 listeners.

**Cost scales with reach.** Times Square billboard > Ohio billboard. Local radio $500–1,500/week, big-market $4–8K/week. Local TV $5–50 per 1,000 viewers for 30 seconds. National TV airtime averages $350K. Infomercials cost $50–500K to produce.

**Remnant ad inventory.** Unsold space sold cheap before deadlines. Up to 90% off rate card via Manhattan Media, Novus Media. Tim Ferriss quote in the book. Less control over timing/location.

**Tracking offline.** Unique URLs (tractionbook.com/flyer), promo codes, "How did you hear about us?" signup field. Jason Cohen at Smart Bear offered a free book in every ad — book orders signaled which ad worked.

**Print categories.** Consumer publications (~7,000 US magazines), trade publications (very targeted), local magazines (free, near grocery stores). Strong magazine ads have an attention-grabbing header, eye-catching graphic, tagline of benefits, strong CTA.

**Newspapers** slant heavily over-30 (young people don't buy them offline). Best for time-sensitive offers, awareness campaigns, product launches.

**Direct mail.** Buy lists (~$100/1,000 consumer names; more for businesses). Be wary of looking spammy. Services will buy, print, and mail for you for high volumes. Tactics: self-addressed envelopes, handwritten envelopes, clear CTA, bulk-mail discounts.

**Local print** (church bulletins, community newsletters, coupon booklets, yellow pages) — cheap, targeted, surprisingly effective. InstaCab hired cyclists to hand business cards to taxi-hailers in SF.

**Outdoor.** Billboards via Lamar, Clear Channel, or Outfront Media. $300–2,500/month for non-prime locations; $7,000 for DuckDuckGo's SoMa stunt; $30–100K/month for Times Square. GRP (Gross Ratings Points) measures reach. Hard to drive immediate action (you can't visit a URL while driving) — best for event/awareness. Transit ads (buses, taxis, benches, shelters) work for direct response — captive audience. Blue Line Media handles transit campaigns. Bonus: billboards/transit often stay up past their paid date if nobody else booked them.

**Radio.** Priced cost-per-point (CPP). $500–1,500/week local, $4–8K/week Chicago-tier. SiriusXM: 50M+ subscribers, one buy.

**TV.** Branding mechanism. 90% of consumers watch TV; 26 hrs/week avg adult. Production: tens of thousands typical, up to $200K for premium. Cheaper options: animation, film students, simplicity. 1,300+ US stations — local TV often $5–50 per 1,000 viewers. Buying is opaque, no rate cards — for big buys hire a media buyer.

**Infomercials.** $50–500K. Two-minute shorts or 28-min episodes. Almost always direct response. Categories that work: workout equipment/programs, body care, household, vacuums, health (juicers), work-from-home. P90X grew to $400M via infomercials. Test messaging via radio ads first.

**The Jason Cohen wisdom:** *"One thing I learned at Smart Bear is that I have zero ability to predict what's going to work. There'd be a magazine where I thought, 'This is just some piddly magazine, surely no one reads this,' and sure enough it was cheap and it'd do terrifically! … It was unpredictable and decayed over time: so the only thing we were left with was trying everything and measuring what worked."*

### 7. Search Engine Optimization (SEO)

**Definition.** Improving organic search ranking to drive free traffic. Two high-level strategies: **fat-head** and **long-tail**.

**Who it works for.** Anyone where customers search for a known need. Less useful when the product category is so new there's no search demand (Rand Fishkin's Uber example: nobody searches "alternatives to taxicabs that I can hire via my phone").

**Core principle.** SEO = **content + links.** More high-quality links to a page → higher ranking. Use keywords in titles and headers. About 10% of clicks go beyond the first 10 links — you need to rank top of page 1.

**Search distribution.** ~30% of searches are short fat-head terms ("Facebook", "Braves"). ~70% are long-tail. Long-tail searches in aggregate are the majority.

**Fat-head strategy.**
1. Research terms describing your industry with Google Keyword Planner.
2. Find ones with enough volume that capturing 10% would matter.
3. Check ranking difficulty (Open Site Explorer — count competitor links).
4. Validate with Google Trends + SEM (buy ads against the terms first).
5. Orient site around chosen terms (page titles, home page).
6. Get links — ideally with exact-match anchor text ("[small business accounting software]" as the link text).

**Long-tail strategy.**
1. Google Keyword Planner for niche search volumes.
2. Check competitor sites — many landing pages (site:domain.com), high % search traffic on Alexa.
3. Produce significant amounts of content per long-tail keyword.

**Patrick McKenzie / Bingo Card Creator.** *"You build a machine that takes money on one end and spits rankings out the other."* Pays freelancers $10–20 per article to create niche landing pages (e.g., "Owls of East Asia bingo"). One page = $3.50 content cost, generates $60 over 3 years. Hundreds of these compound. Subpages by category — "plants and animals" with "dog breeds bingo," "cat breeds bingo," "owls of East Asia bingo."

**Auto-generated long-tail content.** Yelp, TripAdvisor, Wikipedia, Names Database (Gabriel's prior startup) — all gained traffic via auto-generated pages from product data. *Expose data behind a login. Generate pages by geography ("foreclosed homes in Queens").*

**Link-building tactics.**
- **Publicity** — articles link back.
- **Product** — pages people naturally share (LinkedIn profiles).
- **Content marketing** — infographics, slideshows, original research drive shares.
- **Widgets** — embeddable tools with link back.

**Big don'ts.** Black-hat tactics (buying links, tricking search engines) work short-term but get penalized. Stay white-hat.

**HubSpot inbound benchmark.** 30 marketers + 120 sales (all in Cambridge, no outside sales). 45–50k new leads/month. 60–80% inbound (not paid). Inbound leads close 100% more than paid and cost 50% less. *"Most other SaaS companies get more like 10% of their leads from inbound."*

### 8. Content Marketing

**Definition.** Using a blog (or similar owned content) to attract, engage, and convert customers. Distinct from but complements SEO.

**Who it works for.** Anyone with expertise to share. Especially: Software/SaaS (Moz, Unbounce, HubSpot), free consumer products that can't pay for acquisition (OkCupid), thought-leadership-driven categories. Long ramp — six months minimum.

**Case: Unbounce.** Founder Rick Perreault started blogging *a year before product launch.* First hire: a full-time blogger. By launch had 20k monthly visitors and 5,000-strong email list. *"If we had not started blogging at the beginning the way we did, Unbounce would not be here today. … Content still drives customers. Something we wrote in January 2010 still drives customers today."* Took 6 months to see meaningful traffic; biweekly posts.

**Case: OkCupid.** Started blog 5 years into the business. Less frequent (~monthly), longer, data-driven, intentionally controversial ("How Your Race Affects the Messages You Get"). CNN, Rachael Ray, NYT picked it up. Within a year of launching the blog, OkCupid was #1 search result for "online dating." Acquired by Match.com for $50M.

**Tactics.**
- Write about problems your target customers face — you know more than them.
- **Infographics get shared ~20x more often than typical blog posts.** Unbounce's "Noob Guide to Online Marketing" infographic — tens of thousands of downloads, thousands of customers, shared once an hour a year later.
- The "showing them they have a problem they didn't know about" secret — better than just offering a solution.
- Guest posts on bigger blogs early on (Unbounce started after 3 months).
- Influencer outreach: ping Twitter influencers for feedback on every post.
- Engage in industry forums and Quora.
- It's hard to predict which post will hit — keep a regular schedule and analyze winners with social mention monitoring and analytics.
- Email capture at the bottom of every post / behind premium content (videos, white papers).

**Compounding nature.** *"If you invest in content, it gets picked up by Google. People find it, they share it, and it refers customers almost indefinitely."* Compare to ads: stop paying, traffic stops.

**Halo effect.** A strong blog can positively impact at least 8 other channels — SEO, publicity, email marketing, targeting blogs, community building, offline events, existing platforms, business development. Unbounce's biggest BD wins (Salesforce integration etc.) came *after* the blog raised their profile.

### 9. Email Marketing

**Definition.** Acquiring, engaging, retaining, monetizing, and getting referrals via email.

**Who it works for.** Almost everyone. Groupon, JackThreads, Thrillist, Zappos use it as core. Personal channel — emails sit next to family/friends emails. *"It really can't be beat"* for reaching expressed interest.

**Four uses (Colin Nederkoorn, Customer.io):**

**1. Acquisition.**
- Build the list via every other channel (blog opt-in, premium content gating). Don't buy lists — that's spam.
- Free email mini-courses (5–7 day sequences) — educate → CTA at end.
- Advertise on complementary newsletters.

**2. Engagement / Activation.**
- Define what "activation" means for your product. Twitter: 5 follows + 1 tweet. Dropbox: install + upload 1 file.
- Build the ideal signup journey, then build emails to catch users who fall off each step.
- *"You then create all of the paths they can go down when they fail to go through the ideal experience. And you have emails in place to catch them and help them get back on that path."*
- Dropbox: emails users who created an account but never uploaded.
- Colin's onboarding email at Customer.io (17% reply rate):

```
Subject: Help getting started?

Hey {{ customer.first_name }},

I'm Colin, CEO of Customer.io. I wanted to reach out to see if you need any help getting started.

Cheers,
Colin
```

**3. Retention.**
- Twitter mention notifications, weekly digests.
- Dating sites: profile suggestions, unread messages.
- Mint: weekly financial summary.
- BillGuard: monthly suspicious-charge report.
- Brennan Dunn / Planscope: "this week you made $X" email. Patrick McKenzie calls these "you are so awesome" emails.
- Photo apps: "you took this picture a year ago today" — emotional + brings users back.

**4. Revenue.**
- Sales-via-sequence: WP Engine drives ads → free speed test tool → email capture → 7-email mini-course on WordPress speed → pitch hosting. Higher conversion than direct sales page.
- **Email retargeting**: abandoned-cart emails 1–2 days later with special offer.
- Freemium upgrade prompts: explain the premium feature they're missing.
- Subscription upsells: annual billing, plan-limit warnings (Skype credits running out).
- Patrick: email subscribers were 70x more likely to buy courses than other-channel visitors.

**5. Referrals.**
- Groupon's "deal unlocks at N buyers" — built-in email-the-friends incentive.
- Dropbox: refer friends for extra space. Biggest growth mechanism.
- Asana, Hotmail, Facebook, LinkedIn — all email-driven viral.

**Tactics.**
- **Deliverability** — use MailChimp, Constant Contact (technical reasons emails get blocked).
- A/B test subjects, formats, images, timing. Best timing: 9 a.m. – 12 p.m. in the user's time zone, or the time they originally signed up.
- **Never send from noreply@.** Use a personal address; let people reply. Drives support, feature requests, upsells.
- Email copy is an art — Copy Hackers as a starting resource.
- Tools: Vero, Customer.io for automated triggers.

### 10. Engineering as Marketing

**Definition.** Using engineering resources to build free tools, calculators, widgets, microsites that attract leads. Underutilized.

**Who it works for.** Especially software/SaaS companies where free apps can be subsets of the main product. The free tool serves the same audience as the paid product.

**Marketing assets, not ads.** Dharmesh Shah (HubSpot): *"I think of free tools as content (albeit interactive content). … We think of each piece of content (blog article, app, video, whatever) as a marketing asset. This asset creates a return—often indefinitely."* Once built, free tools generate leads forever. Ads stop when payment stops.

**Case: HubSpot Marketing Grader.** Dharmesh was manually checking site marketing for sales calls — got tired of it after a few days, automated it for himself. Made it public. 3M+ sites used it. Source of a large portion of HubSpot's 50,000+ leads/month.

**Case: Moz.** Followerwonk (Twitter analytics) and Open Site Explorer (competitor backlinks) — tens of thousands of leads.

**Case: WP Engine speed tester.** Email required → free report → 7-email mini-course → premium hosting pitch.

**Case: Codecademy Code Year microsite.** Email signup for weekly programming lessons throughout 2012. 450,000 signups — nearly doubled the user base.

**Case: Patrick McKenzie holiday-themed microsites** (Halloween/Christmas bingo) — reusable annually.

**Case: DuckDuckGo's DontTrack.us.** Microsite explaining Google's tracking + how DuckDuckGo differs. Initial press wave then sustained traffic forever as privacy news breaks (NSA leaks, Data Privacy Day). Now several DDG microsites.

**Case: Delicious bookmark widget.** Tripled product adoption.

**Case: RJMetrics.** Founder Robert Moore — uses own analytics on Twitter/Tumblr/Instagram/Pinterest data for blog posts ("BuzzFeed Posts: What's the Magic Number for 'Best Of' Lists?"). Builds microsites at keyword-rich domains: cohortanalysis.com, querymongo.com (SQL→MongoDB translator). Querymongo is highest-trafficked microsite, hundreds of leads/month.

**Strategy guide (Zack Linford / Optimozo):**
- Provide something of true value for free, no strings.
- Make it extremely relevant to your core business.
- Demonstrate value as quickly as possible.

**Tactics.**
- Put microsites on their own domain (easier to share, SEO via keyword domain).
- Look internally for tool ideas — what you already automated for yourself, or a popular blog post you could turn into a microsite.
- Single-purpose, simple tools beat multi-purpose ones.
- Cyclical/seasonal microsites compound year over year.

### 11. Targeting Blogs

**Definition.** Getting prospect-relevant blogs to write about, review, or feature your product. Highly effective Phase I; doesn't scale well into Phase II/III due to limited high-traffic blogs.

**Who it works for.** Almost any early-stage startup. *"One of the best ways to get your first wave of customers."*

**Case: Mint.** Already detailed above under Bullseye. Targeting blogs was its core early channel. Three sub-strategies:
- Standard guest posts and articles.
- VIP access embed badges — 600 blogs, 50,000 signups.
- Direct sponsoring — "Can I send you $500?" emails, $500 to personal bloggers with strong readerships but no monetization, 10,000+ preregistrations.
- Content partnerships with bigger sites (Motley Fool — combines BD + targeting blogs).

**Case: AppSumo / Lifehacker.** Noah built a productivity-app bundle *specifically for Lifehacker's audience* before pitching them. Strong early traction.

**Case: Dropbox.** Posted a video on Hacker News → 10,000+ signups → trending on Digg → even more signups. Quora, Codecademy, Gumroad similar. Filepicker.io: Hacker News top spot for ~3 hours, 10,000 visits, 460 concurrent users, 500+ developer signups, 5,000+ files managed.

**Finding the right blogs.**
- Search engines: "top blogs for X," "best X blogs"
- YouTube + Vimeo + Dailymotion search for influencers
- Delicious for tag-saved links
- Twitter search; Followerwonk + Klout for top accounts
- Social Mention for keyword mention frequency
- Ask your customers directly

**Link-sharing communities.** reddit, Product Hunt, Hacker News, Inbound.org + hundreds of niche subreddits/forums. Submit relevant posts; sharing is the heart of these communities.

**Tactics.**
- Don't ask blogs to write about you generically. Build something tailored to *their* audience first (AppSumo's Lifehacker bundle).
- Offer early-access VIP perks in exchange for sharing.
- Sponsor small personal blogs who have audiences but no monetization.
- Use Twitter / YouTube / Delicious / Social Mention to find smaller niche blogs.

### 12. Business Development (BD)

**Definition.** Strategic partnerships that benefit both parties. Differs from sales: BD exchanges value (often product/integration/distribution for product/integration/distribution); sales exchanges product for money.

**Who it works for.** Anyone — but especially companies that can offer something specific a big partner needs. Even Google got initial traction primarily through BD: Netscape default search deal (1999) + Yahoo! search-power deal.

**Five major BD deal types:**

1. **Standard partnerships.** Two companies combining capabilities — Apple/Nike → Nike+ shoe ↔ iPod.
2. **Joint ventures.** Entirely new product, often equity-involved. Starbucks/Pepsi bottled Frappuccino.
3. **Licensing.** Strong brand lent to a new product. Starbucks ice cream. Music licensing for Spotify/Grooveshark.
4. **Distribution deals.** One provides product/service, the other provides access to customers. Groupon's whole business. Kayak/AOL — Kayak's tech powered AOL's travel search → Kayak's initial traction.
5. **Supply partnerships.** Securing key inputs. Half.com securing 1M books for launch.

**Discipline.** Most-tempting partnerships are often off your Critical Path. Don't waste resources on deals that don't directly move your traction metric. Chris Fralic example: at Half.com they pre-defined exactly what they needed before launch (working tech, 1M-item inventory, distribution).

**Understand partners' incentives.** Chris flew to Atlanta and worked in a used bookstore for a day to understand how books flowed from publishers to Borders to remainder piles. *"It's research and learning and understanding your partner's business before you start picking up the phone."*

**Build a pipeline (Charlie O'Donnell, Brooklyn Bridge Ventures).** *"Don't ever list Condé Nast without listing every single other publisher you can think of."* Simple spreadsheet: Company / Partner Type / Contact / Size / Relevance / Ease of Use / Priority Score. Aim for 50–100 names. Most deals don't close.

**Categorize by attribute, not name (Chris Fralic).** *"So rather than saying 'I want to go after XYZ brand,' say 'We want to go after Internet retailers that are between 50 and 250 on the IR 500.'"* Then pick 10–20 to focus on.

**Case: Delicious.** Chris's deals: The Washington Post (built them a custom Delicious bookmark interface — easy integration so the Post's IT wasn't pulled in), Mozilla (Firefox 2.0 promoted Delicious extension during upgrade — more than tripled Delicious's user base), tried Wikipedia (failed). The Post deal legitimized Delicious enough to land Mozilla.

**Tactics.**
- Find the *right* contact — the person who owns the metric you'd help.
- Warm intros + short forwardable proposal.
- Same-day meeting/call if possible.
- Term sheet: ideally one page. Key terms — lifetime, exclusivity, payments, commitment, guarantees, revenue share.
- Make engineering integrations easy for partners; do as much work as possible on your side (Delicious built the custom Post UI).
- Document each closed deal: "How the deal was done" memo (timeline, contacts, sticking points, what hooked the partner).

**Low-touch BD.** APIs, feeds, embeds — scale BD past traditional 1-1 deals. SlideShare embeds, Disqus, SoundCloud library. But land traditional deals first to prove value before going API-public.

### 13. Sales

**Definition.** Generating, qualifying, and converting leads through interpersonal interaction. Especially critical for enterprise and expensive products requiring hand-holding.

**Who it works for.** B2B, enterprise, high-ticket. Consumer products generally find first customers via other channels.

**Getting first customers.**
- Sean Murphy: list every project you've worked on, every contact. *"They reach out and say, 'Here is what we are doing: do you know somebody we should talk to that makes sense?'"*
- The **lunch pitch**: one page, 5–10 bullets, optional visual. Focuses the conversation on the prospect's problem.
- John Raguin (Guidewire): offered free 7–10 day consulting studies to insurance companies in exchange for feedback on the ideal system → worked with 40+ insurance companies that way.
- Steve Barsh: *"You get your first customers by picking up the phone."*

**SPIN Selling framework (Neil Rackham).** Four question types in order:

1. **Situation questions** — clarify the prospect's buying situation. (How many employees? How is the org structured?) Use sparingly — too many feel one-sided.
2. **Problem questions** — clarify pain points. (Are you happy with your current solution? What problems do you face?)
3. **Implication questions** — make them feel the problem is bigger than they thought. (Does this hurt productivity? Who's affected? How does it impact turnover?)
4. **Need-payoff questions** — focus on your solution. (How would this help? What impact in 3 months? Whose life improves?)

**Who to cold call.** Sean Murphy: not the top, not the bottom. One or two levels up — enough perspective on the problem and organization, enough authority to bring change. Only go straight to CEO/exec at very small companies.

**Five qualification areas (Todd Vollmer).**
- **Process** — how does the company buy?
- **Need** — how badly do they need this?
- **Authority** — who can decide?
- **Money** — do they have funds? What does *not* solving cost them?
- **Estimated Timing** — budget cycle, decision timeline?

After a first call, send a follow-up email documenting what was discussed + a direct close question ("Will you agree to this closing timeline?").

**Pitfalls.**
- "Free consulting" prospects who never intend to buy.
- "Change agent" prospects who have never actually brought new tech into their company.

**The sales funnel (David Skok, Matrix Partners).**
- Top: drive leads via other channels. *"It's very, very expensive to use cold calling, and really not that effective by comparison with using marketing to get some kind of qualified prospect."*
- Middle: qualify leads. Email + company info gates on white papers/e-books. HubSpot deprioritizes Etsy/eBay small-shop leads.
- **Mark Suster's A/B/C bucketing:** A = realistic close in 3 months (66–75% of sales rep time), B = 3–12 months, C = 12+ months (no sales time; owned by marketing).
- Marketing's two jobs: **arm** (collateral, ROI calculators, competitive analyses) and **aim** (target focus, weed out non-serious).
- Bottom: convert. Lay out exactly what you'll do, set timetable, get yes/no commitment.

**Designing from the buyer's perspective.** David: *"A lot of companies design their sales cycles around how they think things should work. I believe very strongly in the notion that you have to design it from the customer standpoint inwards."* List every question/objection a buyer has, answer as many as possible on your website itself.

**Blockages = points where prospects drop off.** Ways to reduce:
- SaaS (no IT install)
- Free trials, open source
- Channel partners (resellers)
- Demo videos, FAQs, reference customers
- Email nurture campaigns
- Webinars / personal demos
- Easy install/use
- Low intro pricing (under $250/mo SMB, $10K enterprise)
- Eliminate committee decisions

**Case: JBoss.** $65M revenue 2 years after founding (sold to Red Hat for $350M). 5M+ SourceForge downloads but no contact info → gave away documentation in exchange for contact info → 10k+ leads/month. Used Eloqua to qualify leads by behavior (time spent on support pages = good for paid support product). Marketing called promising leads → handed to inside sales → 25% close rate (industry 7–10%). Unqualified leads went to nurture campaigns (newsletter, webinar invites). When they engaged enough (clicks, attendance), back to sales pipeline. Average deal: $10K+.

### 14. Affiliate Programs

**Definition.** Paying third parties for actions — sale, qualified lead, etc. Amazon, Zappos, eBay, Orbitz, Netflix all use them.

**Who it works for.** Retail, info products, lead gen. Less natural for free consumer apps unless tied to incentivized referral (which is really viral marketing). High-margin or high-LTV products that can afford payouts.

**Three categories of programs.**

**1. Retail affiliate programs.** $2B+ annually. Amazon pays 4–8.5% of each sale. Affiliate types:
- Coupon/deal sites — RetailMeNot, CouponCabin, Brad's Deals, Slickdeals. Rank for "[brand] coupon" searches.
- Loyalty programs — Upromise, Ebates pay members a cut of cash back.
- Aggregators — Nextag, PriceGrabber compile and compare.
- Email lists — recommend products to subscribers.
- Vertical sites — niche blogs in parenting, sports, electronics, etc.

**2. Information products.** E-books, software, music, courses. ClickBank dominates — commissions often 75%, 100k+ affiliates, millions of products.

**3. Lead generation.** $26B industry. Insurance, law, mortgage. Insurance AdWords clicks $50–100 each — leads correspondingly valuable. Networks: Affiliate.com, Clickbooth, Neverblue, Adknowledge.

**Building your own program (vs. using a network).**
- Network = instant access to affiliates, upfront cost ($500–2,000), faster start.
- Own program = recruit from your own customer base + bloggers + influencers; pay in product features instead of cash (Dropbox storage, QuiBids free bids); harder to launch.

**Tactics.**
- Start payouts simple: flat fee per conversion or fixed % of conversion. Sophisticated tiered/segmented programs come later.
- First place to recruit: existing customers (already love you, easy).
- Next: content creators (bloggers, social influencers, email list curators).
- Maneesh Sethi case: was a happy customer of an SEO program, contacted them, negotiated commission per referral, made $30,000 over 2 years from one email to his list.
- Build relationships first — write guest posts for the influencer, give free product access, *then* ask.

**Top affiliate networks (book lists all of these).**
- **Commission Junction (CJ)** — biggest retailers, $2,000+ to join, curated quality.
- **ClickBank** — digital products, $50 to list.
- **Affiliate.com** — strict affiliate approval.
- **Pepperjam** — multi-channel (mobile, social, offline, print), $1,000 to join, started by Kris Jones.
- **ShareASale** — 2,500+ merchants, flexible commissions, ~$500 start.
- **Adknowledge** — affiliate + traditional + mobile + display.
- **LinkShare** — Macy's, Avon, Champion.
- **MobAff** — mobile (SMS, push, click-to-call, mobile display/search).
- **Neverblue** — $20K+/month advertisers, full-service. Groupon, eHarmony, Vistaprint.
- **Clickbooth** — DirecTV, Dish, QuiBids.
- **RetailMeNot, Inc. (formerly WhaleShark Media)** — owns coupon sites, partners with brands.

**Kris Jones's pitch for the channel:** *"With affiliate marketing, you get to define what the transaction or the conversion is, and you also have tools available to mitigate low quality. … You don't assume the risk."* Unlike AdWords, you don't pay for low-quality clicks — you pay per validated conversion.

### 15. Existing Platforms

**Definition.** Websites, apps, or networks with hundreds of millions of users you can leverage. App Stores (iOS, Android), browser extensions (Chrome, Firefox), social platforms (Facebook, Twitter, Pinterest, Snapchat, Tumblr), large marketplaces (Craigslist, eBay, Amazon).

**Who it works for.** Almost everyone. *Especially* mobile apps in iOS/Android stores. Plays on new/emerging platforms get outsized returns because of low competition.

**Sub-strategy A: App stores.**

- **Top rankings and featured listings are the discovery engine.** Once in, the flywheel spins: ads → into charts → organic downloads → higher in charts → more downloads → word of mouth → higher → repeat.
- **Mark Johnson's app promotion cycle:**
  1. Ads get you into the charts.
  2. Now you're in the charts, more people see you.
  3. So you get organic downloads.
  4. Which moves you higher in the charts.
  5. More people see you, more organic downloads.
  6. People tell friends.
  7. You climb higher.
  8. Loop.
- Tactics for getting on the charts: ad networks (AdMob), paid installs (Tapjoy), cross-promotion, paid-to-top services (FreeAppADay).
- Other channels feed in too — publicity, targeting blogs.
- **For sustainability, ratings matter.** Editors choose features based on ratings. Press references them. Apps consistently nag for ratings because of this. Ask for ratings *right after delivering value* in-app. But base experience must be excellent — most apps aren't, even today.
- Iconic examples — Instagram, Path, Google Maps, Pandora, Spotify — all have excellent UX + high ratings.

**Case: Trainyard (Matt Rix, paid iOS game).** Released free version (Trainyard Express). Italian blog wrote a glowing piece → #1 free app in Italy → 22,000 downloads day one → UK #1 → 450,000 downloads in a week. Seven days later Apple featured it → 50x daily downloads, millions total. Even after feature ended, downloads stayed elevated.

**Sub-strategy B: Browser extensions.** Adblock Plus, YouTube downloaders, password managers. Lets users get value without consistent return visits to your site. Evernote's extensions drove a 205% increase in web usage in 2010 (already 6M users).

**Sub-strategy C: Social sites.**

- Focus on new/emerging platforms that aren't yet saturated. Brands are slow to adapt; you can win windows.
- **Fill gaps in the platform.** YouTube grew because Myspace had no native video — YouTube provided an upload+embed solution, and Myspace embeds linked *back* to YouTube. Bitly = shortened links on Twitter. Imgur = image hosting for reddit. PayPal = payments for eBay (they bought goods on eBay and required PayPal payment until sellers adopted).
- Airbnb / Craigslist: built a "Post to Craigslist" feature that cross-listed bed listings → tens of thousands of Craigslist users came back to Airbnb to book.

**Case: Evernote.** Core traction channel since founding (now $1B+ valuation). Strategy: be on every new platform on day one. iPhone (one of the first apps, featured/promoted). iPad (designed a new iPad version before launch, never having seen the device — stood in line for one on launch day). Android (one of the first apps, featured 6 weeks straight when store was less crowded → hundreds of thousands of new customers). Verizon's Android launch piggybacked Evernote nationwide.

Phil Libin (Evernote CEO): *"We really killed ourselves in the first couple of years to always be in all of the App Store launches on day one. Whenever a new device or platform would come out, we would work day and night for months before that to make sure Evernote was there."*

Alex Pachikov (Evernote founding team): *"A lot of people have cool apps and could do really, really well if they were to get this initial push, and that initial push is free if you do it early. But you risk that all that effort is a waste."* — Evernote tried this on Nokia, Windows, BlackBerry too; those flopped. The Android/iOS wins more than paid for those losses.

**Stand-out feature strategy.** Evernote Peek used iPad Smart Cover for flashcard study. Felt magical, leveraged Apple's new tech. Apple put it in a commercial. Top educational app for over a month. 500,000+ new Evernote users.

**Multi-app strategy.** Evernote later split into many apps (Evernote Food, Evernote Hello) to get featured in more categories where the main app didn't appear.

**Chris Dixon:** *"Some of the most successful startups grew by making bets on emerging platforms that were not yet saturated and where barriers to discovery were low. Betting on new platforms means you'll likely fail if the platform fails, but it also dramatically lowers the distribution risks."*

### 16. Trade Shows

**Definition.** Industry-specific in-person events for showcasing products to insiders. Early stage: build interest, do customer discovery, find partners. Later: make announcements, close big customers, seal partnerships.

**Who it works for.** Especially B2B/enterprise. Companies whose customers and partners are geographically dispersed enough that meeting individually would be cost-prohibitive.

**Case: SureStop bike brakes.** Used trade shows at every phase. Pre-product: brought prototypes, gathered feature/price requirements, no plans to sell. Post-product: bigger booths. Met Jamis (top bike manufacturer) early with a prototype, learned specs, built to them, formed manufacturing partnership → biggest source of traction.

**Choosing shows (Brad Feld).**
- Set your goals (press, investors, customers, partnerships).
- List all events in your industry.
- Evaluate each against your goals (do these interactions happen here?).
- Set annual budget allocated by quarter.
- Backward-calc whether each event makes sense given your budget + economics. (Example: 10,000 attendees, 30% target-fit = 3,000 prospects. $10K to attend. $5K product → profitable at 3 sales, do-able. $50 product → won't pay off.)

**Visit first.** Best way to evaluate: go as a guest the year before. Or ask previous exhibitors: How crowded? Quality of attendees? Would you go again?

**Preparation tactics.**
- List key attendees you want to meet; schedule meetings beforehand. Brian Riley sent researched emails + one-pager.
- Jason Cohen: aim for 5+ meetings/day with editors, bloggers, customers, prospects, vendors, competition, partners. Email title: "At [Trade Show X]: Can we chat for 5 minutes?"
- Reach out to journalists who'll attend — give them something to write about.
- **Host dinners (Mark Suster).** Book one "brand name" person first; their name draws everyone else. Mix customers + prospects + employees + interesting guests. Split costs with two other startups for affordability.

**Booth tactics.**
- Decide on location based on goal: high traffic for many attendees, strategic placement (e.g., next to a known company) for partner-focused.
- Standard kit: big banner, nice materials, business cards, compelling demo.
- **Send discount cards pre-conference** that must be redeemed at your booth (Jason Cohen at Smart Bear).
- Giveaways: pun-based or value-prop-based (DuckDuckGo could give duck keychains). Bags with your name get free advertising as attendees fill them.
- Engaging in-booth demos (SureStop's funny brake demo video).
- Outbound on the floor — RJMetrics found only 28% of conversations were walk-ups; outbound got 3–4x value.
- Every handout has a unique tracking URL + clear CTA + mobile-optimized landing page.
- Throw a party near the show center; cosponsor with other startups.

### 17. Offline Events

**Definition.** Hosting or sponsoring conferences, meetups, parties.

**Who it works for.** Customers who don't respond well to online ads or have no natural online congregation point. Especially good for enterprise with long sales cycles. Companies whose customers share interests and need community.

**Conference tactic at someone else's event — Twitter at SXSW 2007.** Twitter was 9 months old, thousands of users. Two moves: (1) paid $11K to install flat-panel screens in hallways showing a Twitter visualizer; (2) created an event-specific feature where texting "join sxsw" to 40404 got users on screen and auto-following SXSW ambassadors. Tweets jumped from 20k → 60k/day during the conference. Won SXSW Web Award. *Only money Twitter ever spent on marketing.*

**Case: Eric Ries's Startup Lessons Learned conference.** Tested demand by asking blog readers if they'd attend. Resounding yes. Sold tickets via his site and partner startup blogs. One-day in SF (low cost/coordination, attractive to time-poor attendees). Live-streamed to meetups across the country — those meetups still meet years later as "Lean Startup Circle" meetups in 20+ cities — and helped make his book a bestseller.

**Case: Enservio.** Sold expensive insurance software. Couldn't reach top executives. Solution: Claims Innovation Summit at the Ritz-Carlton in Dove Mountain, Arizona. Booked prominent consulting/insurance/startup figures as speakers; execs came to network + vacation. Established Enservio as industry leader overnight. Now annual.

**Case: MicroConf (Rob Walling).** First year: struggled to sell tickets — Facebook ads and AdWords flopped because the conference was unknown. Once first year proved itself, sells out in days. *"People had no issue with the price once it proved itself."* Format: small, attendees can meet everyone, speakers sit with attendees at lunch, participate in roundtables. Premium ticket price keeps quality high (filters out non-serious attendees).

**Who benefits from offline events (Rob Walling).** Companies whose customers have shared interests/community/need-for-community. HitTail (Rob's other company, broad customer base) wouldn't benefit. Niche markets with online-reachable customers and recognizable names that can be invited as speakers.

**Meetups.**
- Cheap to start. Nick Pinkston started a Hardware Startup Meetup at TechShop SF — 60 attendees, $70 for pizza, now 2,600+ members.
- Seth Godin organized 10,000+ attendee Linchpin meetups via his blog across many cities.

**Parties.**
- Evite threw a huge SF party for internet celebrity Mahir Çağri — Evite handled all invitations. Exposed Evite to its target audience in a memorable way.
- Yelp parties for "Yelp Elites" with free food, free merchandise, VIP treatment. When other users heard, it incentivized them to be more active.

**Tactics.**
- Test with a one-day mini-conference at a local university lecture hall. Invite 3 local founders to give talks or sit on a panel. Or "unconference" format with attendee-voted roundtables. Under $500.
- Sponsors can offset costs for larger events.
- Keep attendee quality high via premium ticket pricing.
- Make sure speakers actually mingle with attendees — small format wins.
- Rob's macro point: *"People need to think about doing things that don't scale. Early on when you're trying to get those first one thousand customers, you have to do things that don't scale. You have to take more risks. … You can still build a business without being creative. If you don't have creativity, you need money. You need one or the other."*

### 18. Speaking Engagements

**Definition.** Giving talks at trade shows, conferences, and meetups. Distinct from running the events — this is being the speaker.

**Who it works for.** Especially B2B/enterprise where the room is the target customer. Dan Martell (Clarity, consumer-focused) also makes it work. Anyone who can teach.

**Mark Zuckerberg quote**: improving at public speaking has vastly improved his management ability. Even if not a primary channel, give at least one talk.

**Strategy.**
- Event organizers *need* to fill time — you're doing them a favor if your talk is good.
- Don't pitch your talk topic first. **Ask the organizers what topics they want covered** (Steve Barsh's tactic), then craft a pitch to fit.
- **Event types and lead times:**
  - **Premier** events (national/international, few per year): 6–12 months proposal lead.
  - **Regional** events (one-day-drive radius): 2–4 months.
  - **Local** events (city-level): 1–3 months.
- Organizers look at timing, topic, and credibility. Establish your expertise (blog, prior speaking).

**Building speaker credibility.** Start small — coworking spaces, nonprofits, tiny conferences. Use these to refine talks and build reputation. *"To become a speaker you have to speak once. … I've never marketed myself as a speaker; it's not in my bio. … You speak at a conference, people see it or talk about it, and you get invited to other ones."* (Dan Martell)

**Tactics.**
- The audience's two opening questions: *Why are you important enough to be giving this talk? What value can you offer me?* — answer both immediately. Dan does his own intro highlighting his exits (Flowtown, Spheric).
- **Tell a story:** *what* you're doing, *why*, and specifically *how* you got here or *where* you're going.
- **Reuse the same one or two core talks**, lightly modified per audience. More practice = better, easier to spot what isn't clicking, easier to adapt 60 min → 20 min by removing slides.
- *"The best talks I've ever seen are where each slide is essentially a seven-minute story with a beginning, middle, and end."*
- **Record speeches.** A 250-person talk is 250 people; the video is unlimited.
- **Leverage social media:** Rand Fishkin tweets slides before each talk so followers know what's coming and there's buzz when video posts.
- Dan asks audience for "divided attention" — tweet/share during the talk. Twitter handle on every slide.
- **End with a CTA** — sign up for a list, check a link. Tells you whether the talk was engaging enough to act on.
- **Schedule a speakers' dinner** — if there isn't one, create it.
- Get the attendee list from organizers in advance and pre-arrange meetings for after your talk (much more receptive after they've heard you).

### 19. Community Building

**Definition.** Investing in connections among your customers — fostering relationships, helping them bring others in. Yelp's restaurant evangelists, Stack Overflow's reputation-building Q&A community, Wikipedia's contributor pool.

**Who it works for (Chris McCann, Startup Digest).** *"Companies whose core function is the connecting of people are best set up to take advantage of community. … When a company's underlying value is in bringing people together, and where people matter in the system, that's where this community stuff can really take off."* Doesn't fit, e.g., advertising/retargeting companies.

**Bootstrapping the audience.**
- **Wikipedia** started with the small Nupedia user group.
- **Stack Overflow** — Jeff Atwood (codinghorror.com) and Joel Spolsky (Fog Creek/Joel on Software) both already had massive blog audiences. They polled the community on Stack Overflow's *name* (~7,000 submissions before launch). Hundreds of customers in days, thousands in the first month.
- **Startup Digest (Chris McCann).** Started by emailing 22 friends. Grew via 20-second pitches at startup events. Now 250,000+ members.

**Mission matters.** You need a mission worth bonding around. Jeff Atwood: *"We had a manifesto, and an idea of what we wanted to accomplish. And people bought into the vision because it was about them being awesome. … It is about creating something that helps everyone in material and specific ways."*

**Be open.** Stack Overflow solicited feedback throughout development; launch audience was already invested.

**Foster cross-connections (Atwood's biggest mistake).** Stack Overflow initially had no "Meta" — no place for community to discuss the community. *"In a nutshell, I was telling the people who loved Stack Overflow the most of all to basically … f\*\*k off and go away."* Always allow members to talk to each other about the community itself.

**Engage personally.** Alexis Ohanian emailed everyone who wrote about reddit to thank them. Sent shirts, stickers, gifts. Coordinated an open bar tour for redditors. Personal interaction beats emails and gifts.

**Quality maintenance.**
- Stack Overflow's strict question guidelines, decided in tandem with the community, placed on the FAQ. Users police more aggressively than founders would.
- Wikipedia: strict article and conflict-of-interest guidelines.
- Startup Digest: content selected by community members per city.
- Quibb: invite-only.
- reddit: karma voting determines visibility.
- **Common decline pattern:** quality dilutes as evangelists leave / get drowned out → more leave → spiral. Prevent by setting strict standards early.

**Community as asset.**
- Wikipedia's contributor pool.
- Yelp's reviewers.
- Codecademy's user-generated programming lessons.
- GitHub — free for open source, drove early adoption (Tom Preston-Werner notes the free advertising / goodwill from open-sourcing code).

**Community as hiring pipeline.** Every DuckDuckGo employee was a community member first. They've already bought into the mission.

## How to Apply to iOS Apps (Synthesis)

This is the founder's section — mapping Traction to the iOS app pipeline. The app will grow primarily via TikTok and Instagram organic, but Bullseye is still essential and several other channels are surprise candidates.

### Stage 1 — Ideation: traction thinking as a constraint

1. **The 50% Rule must be baked into the schedule from day one.** Half the founder's hours go to traction (TikTok hooks, content, micro-tests), half to product. Most app founders violate this and grind on the product until launch — that's a guaranteed flop. Block calendar time explicitly.

2. **Set the traction goal in numbers before building.** "10K MAU at $5 ARPU within 6 months" or "1,000 paying subscribers by month 4" or "30% week-2 retention." Without it, you can't run Bullseye — you can't tell whether a channel could move the needle.

3. **Define the Critical Path early.** What product milestones, content milestones, and channel milestones are absolutely necessary to hit the traction goal? Everything else gets cut. App-specific examples: paywall A/B testing infrastructure is on path; settings screen with 12 toggles is off.

4. **Don't pick an idea you can't sustain for years.** Privacy was DuckDuckGo's wedge for 4 years before NSA leaks blew it up. App categories saturate fast; the founder needs to be willing to do this for 3+ years if needed.

### Stage 2 — Viral Growth: TikTok/Instagram as primary, but run all 19 channels through Bullseye

5. **Run Bullseye properly — don't skip to "TikTok is the answer."** The brainstorm must include all 19, even ones that sound irrelevant. The book's whole thesis is that the unexpected channel often wins. Spend a half-day forcing one credible idea per channel.

6. **Default core channel for a TikTok-driven iOS app: a hybrid of Viral Marketing + Existing Platforms (TikTok/Instagram) + Unconventional PR.** TikTok-native creators making organic content *is* unconventional PR + viral mechanics riding on an existing platform. Bullseye terminology helps — TikTok is the existing platform, viral mechanics are how content propagates inside it, and what you film is unconventional PR (stunts, transformation videos, behind-the-scenes, controversy).

7. **Engineering as Marketing is the surprise wild card.** Build a free companion microtool tangentially relevant to the app, on its own keyword-rich domain, with email capture → drip → app install. Examples: a sleep app could build a "What's the ideal bedtime for your chronotype" tool; a finance app could build "How long until you can quit your job" calculator. Dharmesh's principle holds: tools are *assets*, not ads. They generate forever. App pipeline founders almost never do this — which is exactly why it's underutilized.

8. **Targeting Blogs still works, but rebranded as "Targeting TikTok creators and Instagram accounts."** Same playbook as Mint: identify creators in your niche with engaged audiences but no monetization. "Can I send you $500?" emails (or product VIP access in exchange for an authentic video). Mint sponsored 600 bloggers and got 50K signups — the analogous play today is 600 sub-50K-follower TikTokers, each posting one organic video.

9. **App Stores ARE one of the 19 channels (Existing Platforms).** Evernote's "ship on day one of every new Apple platform" strategy still applies — visionOS, watchOS new versions, Apple Intelligence integrations, iPad redesigns. Featured = 50x downloads (Trainyard). Ratings are the ranking lever; ask for ratings *right after a value moment*, never before.

10. **Public Relations (traditional PR) is weak for early iOS apps.** Filter-up still works in theory but the leverage on consumer apps is low unless there's a stunt or controversy. Skip in Phase I, revisit after a viral moment.

11. **Unconventional PR is a serious candidate.** Founder stunts on TikTok ARE unconventional PR. Dollar Shave Club ($5K video → 12K customers in 2 days) is the prototype. Cheap, founder-led, with a strong angle. Prepare for failure — many flops per win. Hauser's team admits the misses.

12. **SEM (Apple Search Ads + Google App Campaigns) is the canonical CPA-vs-LTV test channel.** Spend $500–1,000 on Apple Search Ads on 4–8 keywords. Measure CPA. If CPA < LTV within weeks of testing, it's a viable channel even if TikTok is the primary. If not, kill it; don't optimize a losing channel.

13. **Social and Display Ads → Meta Ads as the natural test.** Even if not the core channel, test Meta as a way to retarget existing users (lookalike audiences from current paying subs).

14. **Offline Ads and Offline Events are unlikely fits** for a TikTok-first app — but check the demographic. If targeting seniors, fitness, or local audiences, offline could be a surprise.

15. **Trade Shows and Speaking Engagements: skip in Phase I** unless the app is in a vertical with major conferences (health, education, professional tools). For most consumer apps, the audience isn't there.

16. **Affiliate Programs are a weak fit for low-priced subscription apps** (commissions can't cover affiliate motivation). Better as a built-in viral referral program (Dropbox-style) — which is technically Viral Marketing, not Affiliates.

17. **Community Building takes years but compounds.** Discord, subreddit, Skool, or in-app community. Best for apps where users connect around shared goals (fitness, learning, habits). Set quality standards early — Stack Overflow's strict guidelines prevented dilution. Bootstrap off existing community (Stack Overflow had two famous founder blogs).

18. **Business Development becomes viable in Phase II/III.** Pre-product-market-fit, BD is mostly distraction. Once growth is repeatable, look at integrations, distribution partners (other apps, influencers with audiences), licensing.

### Stage 5 — Onboarding and Stage 11 — Optimization

19. **Email Marketing is underused in iOS apps.** Most apps rely on push notifications and skip email. But email is the most personal channel for retention. Collect emails at signup. Send a weekly "you are so awesome" recap (Patrick McKenzie's pattern: tell users how much progress they made). Reactivation emails when users drop off — Dropbox's "you haven't uploaded a file" template.

20. **A/B test viral cycle time, not just K.** Same K but shorter cycle = faster growth. For app referrals, shortening cycle time means: simpler share flows, deep linking, pre-filled invite messages, in-app friction-removal.

21. **Look for viral pockets.** Calculate K per country, per age, per acquisition source. If TikTok-acquired users invite more than Apple Search Ads users, that's a pocket — double down on TikTok. If a specific country lights up, localize for it. Mint's "viral pockets" framing fits perfectly to mobile.

22. **Map the entire onboarding loop and cut steps.** Andrew Chen's viral-loop map applies to onboarding. Every screen between download and value-moment is friction. Cut anything not strictly necessary.

23. **Set up CPA × LTV tracking before any paid spend.** Sean Ellis: *"Don't start testing until your tracking/reporting system has been implemented."* The single biggest beginner mistake is buying ads before knowing what a customer is worth.

### Cross-cutting traction principles for the pipeline

24. **Most of the 19 channels die from the Law of Shitty Click-Throughs.** What worked for the founder's last app may not work for this one. What worked for the competitor may not work either. Run Bullseye fresh on every product. Run it again every 6 months as channels decay.

25. **Surprise winners for an iOS app — rank-ordered guesses:**
    - **(a) Viral Marketing + TikTok = the assumed core.** Confirmed by every modern viral consumer app.
    - **(b) Engineering as Marketing.** Free companion microtool on its own domain. Almost no app founders do it. Compounds forever. Could become a Phase III growth engine.
    - **(c) Targeting Blogs/Creators.** "Can I send you $500?" still works. Mint's playbook, ported.
    - **(d) Unconventional PR.** Cheap, founder-led, occasional huge wins.
    - **(e) Email Marketing for retention.** Not for acquisition, but for resurrecting dormant users and driving upgrades.

26. **Weak fits for a TikTok-driven iOS app (be honest):**
    - Offline Ads (unless niche demographic).
    - Trade Shows (unless vertical has them).
    - Speaking Engagements (unless founder is already a known voice).
    - Sales (no enterprise motion).
    - Affiliate Programs as a third-party network (commissions don't pencil for $9.99/mo subs).
    - Business Development in Phase I (distraction; revisit at Phase II).

27. **The Mint spreadsheet is the template for Stage 11 optimization.** SOURCE / TRAFFIC / CTR / CONVERSION % / TOTAL USERS / STATUS / CONFIRMED / CONFIRMED USERS. Adapt for app channels: SOURCE / IMPRESSIONS / INSTALL RATE / TRIAL START % / PAID CONVERT % / LTV / CPA / STATUS. Update weekly.

28. **The "Critical Path" filter is the founder's anti-distraction shield.** When a feature request comes in, when a partnership offer arrives, when a shiny new channel emerges — ask: does this serve the current traction goal? If not, log it for later and move on.

29. **Bullseye must be re-run between growth stages.** What got DuckDuckGo from 100 to 1M searches a day was not what got it from 1M to 10M. Channels saturate. Mint's first channel (targeting blogs) maxed out and was replaced by publicity. Plan for at least one channel re-pick every 10x growth step.

30. **Last principle, the most important: traction trumps everything.** Funding, hiring, press, partnerships, product credibility — all become easier with growth, harder without it. The founder should be reading their own traction numbers every day, the way other founders read their code.
