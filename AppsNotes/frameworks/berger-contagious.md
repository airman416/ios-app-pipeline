# Contagious — Jonah Berger (Key Extracts for iOS App System)

Source: Full book read. Apply across all stages — especially Stage 1 (Ideation), Stage 2 (Viral Growth — TikTok/Instagram), Stage 4 (UI/UX design of shareable moments), and Stage 6 (Retention Loops / referral mechanics).

## Core Thesis — Why Word of Mouth Beats Advertising

Berger's central claim: products, ideas, and behaviors don't catch on because they are inherently better, cheaper, or more heavily advertised. They catch on because people talk about them, and people talk about them for specific, engineerable reasons. Quality, price, and advertising matter — but they do not explain why Olivia became a vastly more popular baby name than Rosalie (17,000 Olivias born in 2010 vs. 492 Rosalies), or why one YouTube video gets 300 million views while 50 percent of YouTube videos get fewer than 500 views and only one-third of 1 percent ever cross 1 million.

**Word of mouth dominates advertising. Specifically:**

- People share 16,000 words per day. There are more than 100 million conversations about brands every hour.
- Word of mouth is the primary factor behind 20–50 percent of all purchasing decisions.
- A word-of-mouth conversation by a new customer leads to roughly a $200 increase in restaurant sales. A five-star review on Amazon leads to about 20 more book sales than a one-star review.
- Doctors are more likely to prescribe a drug if other doctors they know prescribe it. People are more likely to quit smoking if their friends quit, and more likely to become obese if their friends become obese.
- Word of mouth from "everyday Joes and Janes" is at least ten times more effective than traditional advertising.

**Why WOM beats ads:**
1. **More persuasive.** Ads always claim the product is best, so they aren't credible. Friends "tell it to us straight" — they'll mention the downsides too.
2. **More targeted.** WOM is naturally directed toward an interested audience. We don't tell skiing friends about diapers. Customers referred by their friends spend more, shop faster, and are more profitable.

### The Keller Fay Study — The 7 Percent Number

When Berger asks executives and students what percent of word of mouth happens online, the average guess is around 50 percent. **The actual number from the Keller Fay Group: 7 percent.** 93 percent of word of mouth is offline (face-to-face, phone). Online is overrepresented in our perception only because it leaves a visible record. People spend close to two hours a day online but more than eight times as much time offline. Offline discussions are more prevalent and potentially more impactful.

The first implication: **don't confuse a Twitter or Facebook strategy with a word-of-mouth strategy.** Facebook and Twitter are technologies, not strategies. WOM marketing works only if people actually talk — putting up a Facebook page guarantees nothing. 50 percent of YouTube videos have fewer than 500 views; only one-third of 1 percent get more than 1 million.

### The Myth of Influentials, the Myth of Randomness, the Myth of "Born Viral"

Berger spends the introduction destroying three popular but wrong intuitions:

1. **"Special influential people make things go viral."** Malcolm Gladwell's "mavens, connectors, salesmen" theory. Berger's research finds little empirical evidence that more-connected or more-persuasive people drive epidemics. Anything those people touch does *not* turn to gold. The driver is the message, not the messenger. Like a great joke, contagious content spreads regardless of who tells it.
2. **"Virality is random."** Berger argues the opposite — there is a predictable recipe. Looking only at viral hits (e.g., funny cat videos) and concluding "be funny and cute" is like noting that Bill Clinton, Bill Gates, and Bill Cosby are all famous and concluding you should change your name to Bill. To understand virality you have to look at *successes and failures together* and find what consistently distinguishes them.
3. **"Some things are just born contagious."** The Blendtec story refutes this. Tom Dickson's blender was as unsexy a product as exists. With a $50 marketing budget, George Wright produced *Will It Blend?* videos that got 300 million views and increased retail blender sales 700 percent within two years. **Virality isn't born, it's made.**

### Berger's Method

Across a decade at Wharton, Berger and collaborators analyzed thousands of New York Times articles, hundreds of years of baby names, millions of car purchases, and the full BzzAgent campaign dataset. The output is the STEPPS framework — six properties that consistently predict whether a thing spreads.

### Immediate vs. Ongoing Word of Mouth — A Key Distinction

This distinction (which Berger develops most explicitly in the Triggers chapter, but applies throughout) is worth surfacing early because it changes what you optimize for.

- **Immediate WOM** = passing on info soon after the experience. The next day at work, the same night to a spouse. Driven mostly by *interest* — was the experience surprising, entertaining, novel, remarkable?
- **Ongoing WOM** = the conversations weeks and months later. Driven mostly by *triggers* — does something in daily life remind the audience of your product or idea?

Different products depend on different types. Movies depend on immediate WOM (theaters drop films that don't open well). New food products on shelves depend on immediate WOM (grocery stores have limited shelf time). But most consumer apps, subscription products, public-health campaigns, ballot initiatives, and brand-building efforts require **ongoing** WOM. The implication: if you optimize purely for "interesting first impression" (the typical advertiser's default) without engineering triggers, the buzz dies in a week. The Berger/Schwartz BzzAgent analysis showed this empirically — interesting products got more immediate WOM but no sustained advantage; frequently triggered products got 15 percent more buzz *and* sustained it.

This is the single most underappreciated finding in the book for app builders.

## The STEPPS Framework

Six principles. Not a recipe (you don't need all six). More like Cobb salad toppings — the more, the better. Each principle is independent; pick what fits.

- **S — Social Currency** — We share things that make us look good.
- **T — Triggers** — Top of mind, tip of tongue.
- **E — Emotion** — When we care, we share.
- **P — Public** — Built to show, built to grow.
- **P — Practical Value** — News you can use.
- **S — Stories** — Information travels under the guise of idle chatter.

## 1. SOCIAL CURRENCY — We Share Things That Make Us Look Good

**Definition.** Just as people use money to buy products, they use social currency to achieve desired positive impressions among their families, friends, and colleagues. What you talk about influences how others see you. People prefer to share things that make them seem entertaining rather than boring, clever rather than dumb, hip rather than dull.

### Psychology / studies behind it

- **Self-disclosure is biologically rewarding.** Harvard neuroscientists Jason Mitchell and Diana Tamir hooked subjects to brain scanners while they shared their own opinions vs. others' opinions. Self-disclosure activated the same reward circuits as food and money. In a follow-up, people were willing to forgo about 25 percent of monetary pay to share their opinions. "Maybe instead of giving people a penny for their thoughts, we should get paid a penny for listening."
- **Talking about ourselves dominates conversation.** More than 40 percent of what people talk about is their personal experiences or relationships. About half of tweets are "me-focused."
- **Choices signal identity.** The minivan / Mohawk thought experiment: tell people Carla drives a minivan, and 100 percent infer kids; most guess soccer mom. Tell them Todd has a Mohawk; most peg him as 15-30, into edgy music, shops at surf/skate stores. We make educated guesses about others — and they make guesses about us — based on outward signals. What we *talk about* is one of those signals.
- **The cockroach exaggeration study (University of Illinois).** Students were told they'd cook together; researchers planted cockroaches in the food. When students later retold the story to peers (vs. to an experimenter), the cockroaches were larger, more numerous, the experience more disgusting. We exaggerate to make stories more remarkable — to look better in the telling. Stories drift toward extremity through retelling.
- **Iyengar/Berger 6,500-brand study.** Remarkable brands (Facebook, Hollywood movies) got nearly 2x the word of mouth of less-remarkable brands (Wells Fargo, Tylenol). More interesting tweets and more surprising articles were shared more.

### The Three Engines of Social Currency

#### A. Find the Inner Remarkability

Remarkable = unusual, extraordinary, novel, surprising, extreme — but above all, *worthy of remark*. Sharing remarkable things makes the sharer seem more remarkable.

- **Snapple Real Facts.** Marke Rubenstein's team filled bottle caps with "out of the ordinary [facts] Snapple drinkers wouldn't know and wouldn't even know they'd want to know." Fact #12: kangaroos can't walk backward. Fact #73: the average person spends two weeks waiting for traffic lights. The pattern: trivia so surprising you can't help but tell someone. (A ball of glass bounces higher than a ball of rubber. Frowning burns more calories than smiling. An ant can lift 50 times its own weight.)
- **JetBlue breaks the low-cost-carrier schema.** You expect tiny seats, no movies, limited snacks. You get a big seat, Terra Blues chips, free DIRECTV. The deviation from expectation is what's remarkable.
- **The Blair Witch Project.** Mystery + controversy. Was the "rediscovered footage" real? People argued about it; argument is remarkable; budget ~$35,000, gross ~$248 million.
- **Black toilet paper.** Berger himself demonstrated that you can find remarkability anywhere. Black toilet paper at a party made toilet paper the conversation topic of the night.
- **The hundred-dollar cheesesteak.** Howard Wein at Barclay Prime turned the lowly Philly cheesesteak (Kobe beef, lobster tail, truffles, brioche, champagne) into a remarkable conversation piece. The point wasn't the sandwich — it was the story. Reviewed in USA Today, WSJ, Discovery, David Letterman.
- **Blendtec's *Will It Blend?*.** Marbles, golf balls, an iPhone, a Justin Bieber CD — pulverized on camera. People shared because the demonstration was unbelievable. The product's "inner remarkability" was its destructive power; the videos surfaced it.

Tactic: ask **what makes this thing surprising, novel, or extreme** — then build the message around that, not around feature lists.

#### B. Leverage Game Mechanics

Game mechanics = elements (rules, levels, points, feedback loops) that make activities fun and compelling. They motivate internally (achievement) and externally (social comparison).

- **Frequent flier programs.** First created by American Airlines in 1981. Today more than 180 million people accumulate miles, but less than 10 percent of miles are redeemed annually — there are an estimated 10 trillion unredeemed miles. People aren't chasing the rewards; they're chasing the *status*. Berger himself paid more for a connecting flight to clear the 222-mile gap to Premier Executive status — perks were minor; the game was the draw.
- **Status is inherently relational.** Harvard study: students were asked whether they'd prefer $50,000/year (others get $25,000) or $100,000/year (others get $200,000). Majority chose option A — they preferred doing better than others, even at lower absolute pay.
- **Foursquare badges and the Mayor.** Check in five different airports, get a Jetsetter badge. Check in to a venue more than anyone else in 60 days, become Mayor. Users post badges to Facebook because the badges give them social currency. Along the way they spread the Foursquare brand for free.
- **Coffee punch cards.** "Buy 10, get 1 free." Discrete progress markers increase motivation — people buy coffee more frequently as they approach the reward.
- **The Delta Platinum Medallion friend.** Berger's friend brags constantly about his status on Facebook. Every brag spreads the Delta brand.

**Building a good game:**
- **Quantify performance.** If a domain doesn't have metrics, create them. Airlines invented mileage tiers; Foursquare invented Mayors; Burberry's "Art of the Trench" let customers submit photos and tagged the chosen ones publicly (drove site traffic from 100+ countries; helped lift sales 50 percent).
- **Help people publicize their achievements.** A tangible, visible symbol they can display.
- **Make the status system easy to understand.** Olympic gold/silver/bronze beats credit-card platinum/sapphire/diamond/jade chaos. British supermarkets' red/yellow/green nutrition circles. Anyone gets it instantly.
- **Even non-experts should be able to decode rank.** If they can't, the system creates confusion, not status.

#### C. Make People Feel Like Insiders (Scarcity & Exclusivity)

**Scarcity** = limited supply. **Exclusivity** = limited access (gated by knowledge or invitation, not money).

- **Please Don't Tell (PDT).** Manhattan bar entered through a vintage phone booth inside Crif Dogs hot dog shop. No advertising. 45 seats. Reservations open at 3:00 p.m. sharp the day of; by 3:30 they're gone. Founder Brian Shebairo and bartender Jim Meehan deliberately designed the experience as a "discovery brand" — "Nothing is more viral or infectious than one of your friends going to a place and giving it his full recommendation." The phone booth itself is a remarkable artifact people *have* to tell others about.
- **Rue La La.** Ben Fischman's flash-sale site. Same products as his struggling SmartBargains.com. The difference was scarcity (deals available only 24 hours; 40–50 percent sold out within the first hour, traffic spikes at 11 a.m.) and exclusivity (invitation-only). Sold both sites for $350 million in 2009.
- **The McRib effect.** McDonald's launched the McRib in 1981. Nationwide rollout — sales were disappointing. Decade later, McDonald's made it scarce: regional rotation, limited-time returns. Demand exploded. Fans built McRib Locator websites. Twitter and Facebook groups begged for its return. The product never changed; only its availability.
- **Disney Vault.** Pulls classic animated films (Snow White, Pinocchio) off the market for years before reissue. Limited availability raises perceived value.
- **Research evidence.** People rate cookbooks higher when supply is limited; cookies taste better when scarce; pantyhose are perceived as higher-end when less available.

**Why this works:** If something is hard to get, people assume it must be worth the effort. If it's gone, others must want it — so it must be good. And insider knowledge is itself currency. "Look at *me* and what *I* was able to get."

**Practical wrinkle.** Restriction must be different from impossibility. PDT lets persistent callers through. Manage disappointment — train staff to say "no, but" ("No, we're booked at 8:30, but how about 11?").

### A Note on Motivation: Don't Pay People to Share

People talk about brands they like for free. As soon as you pay them, you crowd out intrinsic motivation. Decisions to share become proportional to the payment, not to product love. Social incentives (status, currency) outperform monetary incentives long-term. Foursquare doesn't pay users to check in. Airlines don't give discounts to frequent flier members.

Fantasy football illustrates this: Berger spent hours scouting, with no money on the line, just for bragging rights.

The underlying research: Lepper, Greene, and Nisbett's classic 1973 "overjustification effect" study showed that children who'd freely chosen to draw with markers stopped wanting to do so once external rewards were introduced and then withdrawn. The intrinsic motivation was crowded out by the extrinsic one. For founders: if you pay influencers or pay users to refer, you can never go back to letting the social currency carry itself — you've trained the audience to expect compensation. Berger's own BzzAgents weren't paid. They got free product samples and the chance to be early. Status, not cash.

## 2. TRIGGERS — Top of Mind, Tip of Tongue

**Definition.** Triggers are stimuli in the environment that prompt people to think about related concepts. Peanut butter triggers jelly. The word "dog" triggers "cat." Triggers determine *what comes to mind* — and what comes to mind determines what people talk about.

### The Cheerios vs. Disney World Paradox

People talk about Cheerios *more often* than Disney World. Disney has higher social currency and emotion; Cheerios is far more frequently triggered (a box on every breakfast table, an aisle in every supermarket). Triggers beat interest for *ongoing* word of mouth.

### Psychology / studies behind it

- **Mars bar / NASA Pathfinder spike (1997).** Mars Inc. saw an unexplained sales bump. They hadn't changed anything. NASA's Pathfinder mission to Mars had hit news cycles for weeks. Media coverage of the planet acted as an environmental trigger for the candy bar. (Berger jokes that Sunny Delight should sponsor a mission to the sun.)
- **The North/Hargreaves/McKendrick supermarket music study.** Researchers swapped Muzak in a UK supermarket with French or German music. French music → most customers bought French wine. German music → most bought German wine. Music made country-associated ideas accessible; accessibility spilled into behavior.
- **The Berger/Fitzsimons fruit-and-veggie tray study.** Students rated two slogans: "Live the healthy way, eat five fruits and veggies a day" (generic) and "Each and every dining-hall tray needs five fruits and veggies a day" (triggered by trays). Students hated the tray slogan, rated it less than half as attractive, said it wouldn't influence them. But students who saw the tray slogan and ate from trays ate **25 percent more fruits and vegetables**. The generic slogan changed nothing. Triggers > cleverness.
- **The polling-place voting study (Meredith, Wheeler, Berger).** Arizona 2000 general election. 40 percent of voters cast ballots in churches; 26 percent in schools. On a ballot initiative to raise sales tax to fund schools, more than 10,000 additional people voted Yes when their polling place was a school. The building was an environmental trigger. The initiative passed. (Bush-Gore 2000 was decided by under 1,000 votes — 10,000 is more than enough.)
- **Rebecca Black's "Friday."** Released March 2011. The image (00002.jpeg) shows YouTube search volume for "Rebecca Black" through March-April 2011: searches spike every Friday, then drop. Same song every day of the week — but Friday triggered searches. The title was a built-in environmental trigger.
- **BzzAgent buzz analysis (Berger / Schwartz / Balter).** Hundreds of BzzCampaigns. The team initially tested whether interesting products got more word of mouth. They didn't — at least not for *ongoing* WOM. Interesting products got more immediate buzz; frequently triggered products got 15 percent more buzz overall *and* sustained it. Mundane products (Ziploc bags, moisturizer) outperformed inherently interesting ones because they're cued by daily activities.
- **Cheerios on Twitter (00008.jpeg).** Tweet volume spikes at 5–11 a.m., peaks at 7:30–8 a.m., aligning with breakfast. On weekends the spike shifts later. Real-time evidence that environmental cues (the breakfast hour) drive talking.
- **Immediate vs. Ongoing WOM.** Some businesses (movies, new food products on retail shelves) depend on immediate WOM. Most products and ideas (antibullying campaigns, policy initiatives, retention-based apps) live or die on ongoing WOM. Triggers are the engine of ongoing WOM.

### Building Triggers Into Your Thing

#### A. Consider the context.

What does the audience's environment contain? What cues fire during their day? Where will the desired behavior take place? A bath mat PSA shown on television is memorable but useless — you can't buy a bath mat *in* a bathroom. NYC DOH's "Man Drinks Fat" PSA (00003.jpeg — a man chugs a glass of liquid fat) works because it triggers the message at exactly the right moment: when someone reaches for a soda.

#### B. Grow the habitat.

Most products have a "habitat" — a natural set of triggers. Hot dogs: barbecues, summer, baseball games. Ethiopian food: a much smaller habitat. You can expand a habitat by repeatedly pairing your product with prevalent stimuli.

- **Kit Kat and Coffee (Colleen Chorak, 2007).** Kit Kat sales had been falling about 5 percent a year. The classic "Give me a break" jingle had run out of gas. Colleen had no TV budget. Her research found people often ate Kit Kats during breaks, frequently with hot drinks. The new campaign positioned Kit Kat as "a break's best friend," pairing it relentlessly with coffee in radio spots. Sales lifted 8 percent within a year, up by a third in 12 months. The brand went from $300 million to $500 million. *Why coffee and not hot chocolate?* Frequency. Hot chocolate is mostly winter; coffee is year-round. Berger: alliteration ("Kit Kat and cantaloupe") would have been nice, but cantaloupe isn't a frequent cue.
- **Berger / BzzAgent / Boston Market.** A 6-week campaign. Some viewers saw "Thinking about *dinner*? Think about Boston Market." Others saw "Thinking about a *place to eat*? Think about Boston Market." Among people who'd previously associated Boston Market only with lunch, the dinner-specific message increased WOM by **20 percent**. Growing the habitat increased buzz.
- **Michelob's "Weekends are made for Michelob."** Originally "Holidays are made for Michelob." Holidays are infrequent. Anheuser-Busch switched to weekends — a recurring trigger — and the campaign succeeded.

#### C. Frequency vs. Strength tradeoff.

- **Frequency.** How often does the cue occur in the target environment?
- **Strength.** How exclusively is the cue tied to your product?

A trigger that's everywhere (e.g., the color red) is associated with too many things — roses, Coke, love, fast cars — so the association to any one of them is weak. "Peanut butter" → "jelly" is a strong link. The poking-holes-in-a-cup metaphor: more holes, weaker stream from each. **The ideal trigger is frequent in the audience's daily environment AND not already overloaded with other associations.**

#### D. Trigger location matters.

Position the trigger physically and temporally near the desired behavior. The bath-mat PSA fails because the trigger is in the bathroom, not the store. The Cialdini reusable-shopping-bag problem: most of us remember the bag only when we arrive at the grocery store — too late. The fix: link the bag to your shopping list, so seeing the list at home reminds you.

#### E. Use a competitor as a trigger (the Poison Parasite).

A California Department of Health Services anti-smoking campaign spoofed the Marlboro Man (00004.jpeg) with the caption "Bob, I've got emphysema." Every subsequent Marlboro ad seen in the wild became a trigger for the antismoking message. You inject your message into the rival's frequency.

#### F. Even negative WOM can help if it triggers.

Berger/Sorensen/Rasmussen analyzed hundreds of NYT book reviews. For established authors, negative reviews hurt sales. For new or unknown authors, **negative reviews increased sales by 45 percent** (book *Fierce People* sales quadrupled after a scathing review). Why? Reviews — even bad ones — remind the audience the book exists. Similar effects: a $60 wine reviewed as "redolent of stinky socks" saw 5 percent sales lift; the Shake Weight, widely ridiculed, did $50 million.

## 3. EMOTION — When We Care, We Share

**Definition.** Sharing is emotional behavior, not analytical behavior. Content that evokes high-arousal emotions (positive or negative) gets shared. Content that evokes low-arousal emotions (sadness, contentment) does not. The job is to focus on feelings, not features.

### Psychology / studies behind it

- **The Denise Grady cough article (NYT, October 27, 2008).** Grady, a science writer, published "The Mysterious Cough, Caught on Film" about schlieren photography of human coughs (used to study how TB, SARS, and influenza spread). The article had low scores on Social Currency and Practical Value. It rocketed up the Most E-Mailed list anyway. The reason: the accompanying schlieren image (00001.jpeg — a luminous, almost surreal photograph of a cough captured mid-air) was awe-inspiring.
- **The Berger / Milkman NYT study.** Built a web crawler that scraped every NYT article over six months (about 7,000 articles), tracked which made the Most E-Mailed list, and had research assistants score articles on interest, usefulness, emotion type, and positivity/negativity.
  - More **interesting** articles: 25 percent more likely to be highly shared.
  - More **useful** articles: 30 percent more likely.
  - More **awe-inspiring** articles: 30 percent more likely.
  - More **positive** articles: 13 percent more likely.
  - More **sad** articles: 16 percent **less** likely.
  - More **anger-** or **anxiety-evoking** articles: more likely to be shared.

The simple "positive emotion → share, negative emotion → don't share" rule didn't hold. Sadness suppressed sharing, but anger and anxiety boosted it. What unified the sharing emotions wasn't valence but **arousal**.

### High-Arousal vs. Low-Arousal — The Master Model

Berger's table (from p. ~3760 of the source):

|  | HIGH AROUSAL | LOW AROUSAL |
|---|---|---|
| POSITIVE | Awe, Excitement, Amusement (Humor) | Contentment |
| NEGATIVE | Anger, Anxiety, Disgust | Sadness |

- **Arousal** = a state of activation and readiness for action. Heart beats faster, blood pressure rises, palms sweat. Evolutionarily, it's the fight-or-flight response that helped our ancestors chase prey or flee predators. When we're aroused, **we do things**: we yell, pace, run, talk, share.
- **High-arousal emotions kindle the fire.** Awe makes us want to tell people what happened. Anger drives us to vent. Anxiety drives us to warn. Amusement makes us pass along the joke. Excitement gets us pumping.
- **Low-arousal emotions stifle action.** Sadness powers down. Contentment relaxes. Both feel like sitting still.

### The Awe Sub-Framework

Following Keltner and Haidt, awe = the sense of wonder and amazement triggered by encountering something greater than the self — knowledge, beauty, sublimity, might. Awe involves surprise, mystery, unexpectedness. Einstein: "The most beautiful emotion we can experience is the mysterious. It is the power of all true art and science."

- **Why science articles went viral in Berger's NYT study.** Awe was their dominant emotion. Articles about new AIDS treatments, gorillas grieving, a hockey goalie playing through brain cancer — high awe scores, high sharing.
- **Susan Boyle's "I Dreamed a Dream" (Britain's Got Talent).** A frumpy 47-year-old in a poorly fitting dress walks onstage. Judges and audience snicker. She opens her mouth. Stunning. Within 9 days, 100+ million views. Pure awe.

### Anger and Anxiety — The Productive Negatives

- **United Breaks Guitars (Dave Carroll, 2009).** Musician sees baggage handlers smash his $3,500 Taylor guitar at O'Hare. Nine months of fruitless customer service. He writes and posts a song. 500 comments in 24 hours; 1.3 million views in 4 days; 3 million views in 10 days; *Time*'s Top 10 Viral Videos of 2009. United's stock dropped 10 percent ($180M) within 4 days. Anger drove sharing.
- **The Babywearing Motrin debacle (2008).** McNeil/Motrin ad acknowledged that wearing your baby in a sling "totally makes me look like an official mom. And so if I look tired and crazy, people will understand why." Mommy bloggers, offended, took to Twitter. The story trended; NYT, Ad Age picked it up. Motrin pulled the ad and apologized. Lesson: monitor for high-arousal words ("pissed off," "angry," "mad") and address them before they cascade.
- **Disgust = high arousal.** The "Man Drinks Fat" PSA (white globs of fat sliding into a glass) drove sharing precisely because it grossed people out.
- **BMW *The Hire* (2001).** Short Internet films full of kidnappings, FBI raids, near-death moments — fear, not warm fuzzies. 11 million views in 4 months; BMW sales up 12 percent in the same period.

### Sadness Kills Sharing

Mother Teresa's principle (cited by Heath as well) maps here too. Sad / contemplative / low-arousal content reduces transmission even when valence is "noble." NYT articles paying tribute to a deceased grandmother — low sharing. ASPCA-style ads with mournful Sarah McLachlan are emotionally powerful but tend to be *received* rather than *passed on*. To make people *act*, you have to activate them.

### The Google "Parisian Love" Case

Anthony Cafaro, fresh out of graphic design school, joined Google's Creative Lab. He was told Google was "analytics, not emotion" — colors weren't picked aesthetically; they were "mathematical decisions." Asked to create content showcasing Google's new search features (flight queries, autocorrect, translation), Cafaro and team built "Parisian Love" — a wordless love story told entirely through search queries:

- "study abroad Paris France"
- "cafés near the Louvre"
- "translate tu es très mignon"
- "impress a French girl"
- "chocolate shops in Paris"
- "long-distance relationship advice"
- "jobs in Paris"
- "AA flight 38 arrival time"
- "churches in Paris"
- "how to assemble a crib"

Ends with "Search on." Even the CEO's wife loved it. Released to the public; viral. The product (search engine) was inherently un-emotional — yet by tying it to a love story, Cafaro made it feel awe-inspiring.

### Focus on Feelings — The Three Whys

Borrowed from Made to Stick: ask "Why is this important?" three times to drill down to the emotional core.

- Why is search important? → Find information quickly.
- Why find info quickly? → Get answers.
- Why those answers? → To connect with people, achieve goals, fulfill dreams.

By the third Why, you've reached emotion.

### Exercise Boosts Sharing

Berger's lab study: half of participants sat quietly for 60 seconds; half jogged in place. Both then read a school newspaper article and could optionally email it. **75 percent of joggers shared vs. ~33 percent of sitters.** Any physiological arousal — even non-emotional — boosts transmission. Implications:
- Ads at the gym, on game shows, during anxiety-inducing crime dramas — get boosted because the audience is already activated.
- Walking meetings get more idea sharing.
- Beware oversharing after stepping off a treadmill or surviving turbulence.

### The Arousal Diagnostic for Founders

If a piece of marketing content does not raise the audience's pulse, it will not be shared. The diagnostic question: "After reading / watching this, does the user feel like *doing* something, or like *sitting still*?" If sitting still, rewrite. Sad-but-touching brand stories, peaceful "lifestyle" footage, smooth-voiced narrators reading benefit lists — these all evoke low-arousal contentment and *suppress* sharing despite being emotionally pleasant.

This explains the apparent paradox that controversy and outrage drive social media. It's not that audiences are masochists. It's that anger and anxiety are high-arousal, and high-arousal anything spreads.

## 4. PUBLIC — Built to Show, Built to Grow

**Definition.** People imitate what they can see. Things that are observable spread; things that are private die. Two questions: (1) Can people see when others use your product? (2) Does using your product leave behavioral residue that lingers afterward?

### Psychology / studies behind it

- **Steve Jobs flipped the Apple logo.** Ken Segall's account: originally the Apple logo on PowerBook covers faced the user when closed (so the user could orient the laptop when pulling it from a bag). But when opened, the logo faced upside down to everyone else. Jobs reversed long-held design priorities and flipped the logo to face *the world*. The decision criterion was observability — others seeing the brand matters more than the owner's orientation moment.
- **Social proof in restaurants.** Berger's restaurant heuristic: when you don't know a city, pick the place with the crowd. Halal Chicken and Gyro (NYC) draws hour-long lines; the identical Halal Guys cart across the street draws none. Same food, same packaging. The line itself is the signal.
- **Kidney transplants and erroneous refusal.** MIT professor Juanjuan Zhang found that **97.1 percent of available kidney offers are refused**. Many are legitimately bad matches. But social proof induces errors: if many people earlier on the list have refused this kidney, those down the list infer it must be low-quality. One in ten of these refusals is mistaken. Thousands of patients turn down kidneys they should accept — because behavior is observable but reasoning is not.
- **The Wharton MBA career convergence.** Berger's annual exercise: ask half the class what they wanted to do when they entered; ask the other half what they want to do now. Entering ambitions are diverse (health reform, entertainment, politics, entrepreneurship, finance, consulting). After a year, more than two-thirds say investment banking or consulting. Social proof snowball: a 20 percent initial preference becomes a 60+ percent crowd.
- **Berger / McShane / Bradlow auto study.** 1.5 million car sales analyzed. Neighbors buying cars influenced others' purchases — about 1 in 8 cars sold attributable to social influence. The effect was significantly stronger in sunny cities (Miami, LA, where cars are visible) than in rainy or transit-heavy cities (Seattle, NYC, where they're not).
- **Television laugh tracks.** People laugh more when they hear others laugh. Baristas seed tip jars at the start of shifts — empty jars suggest no one tips.

### The College-Drinking Insight: "Behavior Is Public and Thoughts Are Private"

Koreen Johannessen, director of health promotion at University of Arizona, had tried everything to reduce binge drinking — flyers, ads, a coffin display. Nothing worked. She asked students how they felt. Surprised, she found most students were privately uncomfortable with the drinking culture. So why was it happening?

Because behavior is observable but discomfort is not. Every student sees the keg parties and tailgates; no student sees the private discomfort of the others. Each one assumes "I must be the outlier" — and has another drink.

Johannessen's intervention: publish ads in the school newspaper stating the *actual* norm — most students have only 1–2 drinks at parties; 69 percent have four or fewer. By making the private thoughts public, she **reduced heavy drinking by almost 30 percent**.

The same dynamic explains why no one asks questions after a confusing presentation — everyone is privately confused but no one signals it.

### How to Make Things More Public

#### A. Make the Private Public (Movember)

In 2003, friends in Melbourne grew moustaches as a joke about 1970s fashion. The next year (2004) they tied it to men's cancer awareness, formed the Movember Foundation, and 450 men raised $54,000 for the Prostate Cancer Foundation of Australia. By 2007, events ran globally. Total raised since: **more than $174 million worldwide.**

The mechanism: support for an abstract cause (men's cancer) is normally private. A moustache is unmissable, conversation-triggering, and brand-laden. Every November moustache-wearer is a walking, talking billboard. Berger noticed several of his Wharton MBAs growing them — and every conversation started "What's with the 'stache?" → cancer awareness → Movember.

#### B. Design Products That Advertise Themselves (Hotmail)

Sabeer Bhatia and Jack Smith launched Hotmail on July 4, 1996 (chosen symbolically as Independence Day, since web-based email freed users from one-machine dependence). Every outgoing email carried a tagline: **"Get Your Private, Free E-mail from Hotmail at www.hotmail.com."** Every existing user advertised the service every time they sent mail. Subscriber count: 8.5 million in just over a year. Microsoft acquired Hotmail for $400 million. Now 350+ million users.

The Apple/BlackBerry equivalent: "Sent from my iPhone." You can change it. Most don't, because the status (social currency) outweighs the friction. Every email passively endorses the brand.

#### C. The Distinctive Apple Headphones

When the iPod launched, the market had multiple competitors (Diamond Multimedia, Creative, Compaq, Archos). Most digital music players shipped with black headphones. Apple made theirs **white**. White headphones became instantly visible on subways, in coffee shops, on the street — social proof that everyone was switching. Combined with the flipped logo (above), Apple turned every customer into an ambulant ad.

#### D. Distinctive shapes, sounds, colors

- Pringles tubes
- Microsoft startup chime
- Christian Louboutin red soles (1992 — Louboutin noticed an employee's Chanel red nail polish, applied it to his shoes)
- Lululemon's heavy plastic reusable shopping bags (sturdier than paper; users carry them daily as ad surfaces)
- Tiffany blue, Victoria's Secret pink/striped bags

#### E. Behavioral Residue (Livestrong)

**Behavioral residue** = physical traces a behavior leaves behind after the behavior itself is over.

- **Scott MacEachern at Nike, 2003.** Lance Armstrong was at peak fame. MacEachern considered two campaigns: a cross-country bike ride or a yellow wristband. Lance's agent thought wristbands were "a stupid idea." MacEachern produced 5 million at $1 each, all proceeds to the Lance Armstrong Foundation. Sold out in 6 months. Eventually **85 million wristbands sold.** Yellow was chosen because it's the leader's jersey color in the Tour de France, unisex, and uncommon — making each wristband visible from far away. Even after the ride ends, the wristband lives on. MacEachern: "The bike ride doesn't [live on]. But the wristband does."
- **"I Voted" stickers (1980s).** Make a normally private behavior (voting) publicly visible, even after the voter has left the polling station. Functions as a trigger and social proof.
- **Reusable shopping bags from luxury retailers.** Tiffany and Bloomingdale's customers reuse the bags for lunches, gym clothes, errands. Each reuse is a paid-once, advertised-forever asset.
- **Conference giveaways.** A good giveaway is publicly visible (mug, gym bag, T-shirt) rather than privately used (makeup carrier).
- **Facebook Likes / Spotify-on-Facebook auto-posts.** Online behavioral residue. ABC News installed Facebook Like buttons; Facebook traffic up 250 percent.

### A Counter-Lesson: Don't Publicize the Wrong Behavior

Bob Hornik's anti-drug ads study (1998–2004 National Youth Anti-Drug Media Campaign, ~$1 billion). Tracked teen drug use against ad exposure. **Result: kids 12.5–18 who saw the ads were *more* likely to smoke marijuana.** Reason: the ads simultaneously said "drugs are bad" and "lots of kids are using drugs." The social-proof signal overrode the warning.

Music industry's claim: "30 billion songs were illegally downloaded in the past few years." Effect: normalizes piracy as commonplace.

Cialdini's Petrified Forest study. Signs reading "many past visitors have removed petrified wood" almost *doubled* theft. Signs reading "Please don't remove the petrified wood, in order to preserve the natural state" reduced it. Highlight what you want done — not what's commonly being done wrong.

**Rule of thumb:** to make a behavior catch on, make it visible. To suppress a behavior, make the *opposite* behavior visible — don't advertise the bad behavior's prevalence.

## 5. PRACTICAL VALUE — News You Can Use

**Definition.** People share useful information to help others. Sharing useful content is the modern barn raising — a quick, low-cost act of helpfulness. If Social Currency is about how the sender looks, Practical Value is mostly about how the receiver benefits.

### Two Anchoring Stories

- **Ken Craig and shucking corn.** 86 years old, no internet presence. His daughter-in-law filmed him in his Oklahoma kitchen demonstrating how to microwave unshucked corn for four minutes, cut a half-inch off the bottom, shake, and produce a silk-free ear. The video — "Clean Ears Everytime" — racked up more than 5 million views, skewing strongly toward viewers 55+ (the rare viral hit with an old-skewing audience).
- **Hikers discussing vacuum cleaners.** Berger overheard hikers in the Blue Ridge Mountains debating whether one vacuum model justified its premium price. In a forest. Vacuum cleaners are not socially currencied, not triggered there, not emotional. They were practically valuable. That alone drove the conversation.

### Psychology / studies behind it

#### The Anderson / Simester catalog experiment

Two versions of a clothing catalog mailed to 50,000+ homes. In one version, certain dresses were marked "Pre-Season SALE." In the other, they weren't. **Prices were identical.** The "sale"-labeled version increased demand by **more than 50 percent.** The label alone, attached to no actual discount, drove behavior.

#### Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky)

Kahneman won the 2002 Nobel in Economics (as a psychologist) for prospect theory with Tversky. Two relevant insights:

##### 1. Reference points govern perception of value.

Same item, two scenarios:
- **A.** Weber Q 320 grill, originally $350, marked down to $250.
- **B.** Weber Q 320 grill, originally $255, marked down to $240.

Same product. Scenario B is the lower price. In Berger's 100-person survey:
- 75 percent buy in Scenario A
- 22 percent buy in Scenario B

People aren't optimizing absolute price — they're optimizing perceived deal size relative to the reference. Infomercials exploit this: "You might expect to pay $200 for this knife set, but right now $39.99!" The $200 reference is invented but anchors perception.

##### 2. Diminishing sensitivity — the Rule of 100.

Two scenarios:
- **Clock radio:** $35 at one store, $25 at another 20 minutes away. **Most people drive** (87 percent decision rate).
- **TV:** $650 here, $640 at the other store, also 20 minutes away. **Most people don't drive** (17 percent decision rate).

Same $10 savings, same 20-minute drive. People treat the savings differently based on relative size to the base price. From this:

> **The Rule of 100:** If the price is under $100, percentage discounts (20% off) seem larger. If the price is over $100, dollar discounts ($200 off) seem larger.
>
> - A $30 T-shirt: present 20% off (not $6 off).
> - A $750 vacation: present $75 off (not 10% off).
> - A $2,000 laptop: present $200 off (not 10% off).

### What Makes Practical Value Worth Sharing

#### A. Highlight incredible value.
The deal must exceed expectations. Surplus over reference points is the share-trigger.

#### B. Restrict availability.
Time limits, quantity limits ("Limit 3 per customer" — research finds this **increases sales by more than 50 percent**), exclusive access (members-only rates, customized offers). All work the same way as Rue La La's scarcity model — restricting access makes the deal *feel* better, separately from whether the deal *is* better.

#### C. Don't always be on sale.
If a rug store is always at 70% off, the sale price becomes the reference price. Constant discounting collapses the reference window. Too many "sale" tags in a single store can actually *reduce* purchase.

#### D. Make the discount visible.
Grocery-store loyalty cards save money but the savings are hidden on a receipt. Imagine a store ringing a bell when a customer saved more than $25, or a sign at checkout broadcasting the savings. Now the practical value is observable — combine Practical Value with Public.

#### E. Package knowledge into shareable units.
**Vanguard's MoneyWhys newsletter.** Once a month, a one-page email with one main article and three or four links: homeowner's insurance, personal finance software, tax tips. Berger doesn't read every email but forwards many of them to relevant friends (the homeowner one to a friend who just bought a house; the personal-finance one to a friend learning to budget). Along the way Vanguard's expertise spreads. Format matters: short, focused, one-topic.

Same logic for the Heath cousin Lands' End story we'll cover under Stories — every story embeds practical info.

#### F. Audience size paradox.
Narrower content can be *more* shareable than broad content. If you read an article about football, you have many football-fan friends — but no specific friend feels uniquely right. If you read an article about water polo and you have one water-polo friend, you *have* to send it. Specificity creates obligation. Narrow can be more viral than broad.

#### G. False information warning.
The 1998 vaccines-cause-autism paper was fraudulent and the doctor lost his medical license. Childhood vaccination rates still dropped because the original "useful warning" had spread before the retraction. Practical Value is so powerful it carries falsehoods. Verify before sharing.

### Why Practical Value Is the Easiest Lever

Berger argues Practical Value is the easiest principle to apply: almost every product has *something* useful. The hard part is cutting through the clutter — making it stand out using the Rule of 100, scarcity, packaging, and the right framing.

### Sharing Practical Value as Modern Barn Raising

Berger frames this principle morally as well as mechanically: in the 18th and 19th centuries, communities would gather to build a barn together — direct, communal acts of helpfulness. Modern suburban life has eroded those direct opportunities. We live at the end of long driveways or high up in apartment buildings. We barely know the neighbors. Moving for work distances us from family and strong ties.

Sharing useful information has become the digital substitute. It's a quick, low-cost act of help that strengthens social bonds even at a distance. Parents send kids advice across continents. We forward articles to friends not because we're advertising the source — but because we care and the act of caring at scale is what the digital era provides.

This matters for the founder because it means Practical Value content has a *moral* dimension to the share decision. People aren't just sharing to look smart (Social Currency) — they're sharing to help. If your app's content can plausibly help the receiver, sharing it is an act of care, not vanity. That lowers the threshold for sharing dramatically compared to Social Currency content, which the sender has to feel slightly performative about.

## 6. STORIES — Information Travels Under the Guise of Idle Chatter

**Definition.** People don't think in information; they think in narratives. Stories are Trojan horses: the entertaining outer shell carries information, brand, lessons, and morals on the inside. Build a story that's *not just viral but valuable* — meaning the brand or message is **integral** to the narrative, not incidental to it.

### The Trojan Horse

A 10-year war; the Greeks build a giant wooden horse and hide warriors inside; the Trojans drag it inside their walls in victory; that night the Greeks emerge, open the gates, sack the city. The story's surface is action and drama; its core is the moral *"never trust your enemies, even when they seem friendly."* Homer could have stated the moral in one sentence. Instead he embedded it in an epic — and the lesson survived for 3,000 years.

### Psychology / studies behind it

- **Narratives are inherently engrossing.** They have beginning, middle, end; if the audience invests early they stay for the resolution. Information is easy to ignore; stories aren't.
- **People narrativize even unprompted.** Online product reviews are supposed to be feature evaluations. They become stories anyway: "My son just turned eight so we were planning our first trip to Disney World last July. We needed a digital camera..." The narrative scaffolding is the natural mode.
- **Stories serve as cultural learning shortcuts.** Trial and error is expensive (testing two dozen mechanics). Direct observation is impractical. Ads are not credible. Stories about specific people provide proof-by-analogy that's cheap to acquire and credible.
- **Stories disarm counterargument.** Hard to argue with what happened to a specific named person. We're cognitively absorbed in following the plot, so we don't expend energy questioning the message.
- **Allport / Postman telephone-game study (1940s).** Participants viewed a detailed image of a subway car with two arguing men, one holding a knife. They retold the image to others in sequence. About 70 percent of detail was lost within five to six transmissions, but the loss wasn't random — extraneous details (Eighth Avenue Express, Dyckman Street, advertisements) dropped out while *critical* details (the argument, the knife) survived. **Stories sharpen around their core as they're retold.** If your brand isn't core, it sharpens *out*.

### Stories Carry Information

- **Lands' End coat story.** Berger's cousin moved East, bought a Lands' End down coat, broke the zipper. He called expecting friction; instead the rep said "we'll send you a new one in the mail, free, two-day shipping — too cold this winter to go without a coat." Embedded info: (1) topcoats don't cut winter cold, (2) down coats work, (3) Lands' End makes warm coats, (4) excellent customer service, (5) they'll fix problems for free. Five marketing claims, transmitted through one personal anecdote.
- **Jared Fogle and Subway.** Subway had been pushing "7 Under 6" (seven subs under six grams of fat) — a low-impact, infrequent-mentioning fact. Jared lost 245 lbs eating Subway (foot-long veggie sub for lunch, six-inch turkey for dinner). His pants size dropped from 60 inches to 34. The story embeds: (a) Subway has healthy options, (b) so healthy you can lose weight on them, (c) a lot of weight, (d) the food remains tasty over months. People bring it up even outside conversations about weight loss because the surprise itself is a hook.

### Build a Trojan Horse — But Make Sure Your Message Is Inside It

The cautionary tale: **Ron Bensimhon at the Athens 2004 Olympics.** Self-proclaimed world's most famous streaker, jumps off the 3m springboard in a blue tutu and white polka-dot tights with "GoldenPalace.com" on his chest. Belly-flops. Olympic security swarms. Story goes global — discussed in news outlets, water-cooler talk for weeks.

But nobody talked about **GoldenPalace.com**. Discussion focused on whether his stunt threw off the Chinese divers (who flubbed and lost gold), Olympic security failings, his trial and potential jail time. The casino was an irrelevant detail; it dropped out (per the Allport / Postman pattern). Berger's verdict: "It's like building a magnificent Trojan Horse but forgetting to put anything inside."

Same problem with **Evian's "Roller Babies"** ad — diaper-wearing CGI-faced babies do roller-skate tricks to "Rapper's Delight." 50+ million views; Guinness "most viewed online advertisement." Evian sales **dropped almost 25 percent that year.** Babies aren't water. Babies aren't Evian. The story spread but the brand didn't ride along.

### Valuable Virality

The key concept: **virality is valuable only when the brand or benefit is integral to the story** — woven so deeply that the story doesn't make sense without it.

- **Panda Cheese (Egypt).** Commercial: father shopping with son. Son asks for Panda Cheese. Father says no. A giant panda (man in suit) appears, calmly walks to the shopping cart, flips it over, kicks the spilled groceries. Tagline: "Never say no to Panda." Other versions: panda destroys office workspace, hospital ward. You literally cannot retell the story without "Panda" — the brand IS the punchline.
- **Blendtec.** You can't tell the iPhone-pulverizing story without saying "Blendtec blender." The product's strength is the story's spine.
- **Dove "Evolution" (Tim Piper, 2006).** Stephanie, an average-looking woman, sits down. Makeup artists transform her over minutes. A photographer shoots. Then Photoshop: lips inflated, neck thinned and lengthened, eyes enlarged. Final image goes on a billboard. Tagline: "No wonder our perception of beauty is distorted." Cost: ~$100,000. Result: 16 million+ views, hundreds of millions of dollars in earned media, 3x the traffic of Dove's Super Bowl ad, double-digit sales growth. The brand is integral — the video could only exist as Dove's "Campaign for Real Beauty." The conversation about beauty norms is the Trojan horse; Dove rides inside.
- **Barclay Prime's $100 cheesesteak.** A stunt — but the stunt is the actual product on the menu, and it tells the audience exactly what kind of restaurant Barclay is (luxury, creative, premium). The brand isn't bolted on; it's the point.

### The Three (-Plot) Subset

Berger doesn't explicitly enumerate plot types (that's Heath's contribution in Made to Stick — Challenge, Connection, Creativity plots), but several Berger examples map cleanly:
- **Challenge plot:** Jared, Lance Armstrong, Susan Boyle (underdog wins).
- **Connection plot:** Dove "Evolution" (the woman / the viewer / the issue), "Parisian Love" (the boy / the girl).
- **Creativity plot:** Will It Blend?, Ken Craig's corn trick.

### What Survives Retelling (The Allport/Postman Deep Dive)

Worth restating because of its implication for founders: the 1940s Allport / Postman subway-image experiment is the empirical basis for "make your message integral." Subjects passed a description of the scene (Eighth Avenue Express, Dyckman Street, advertisements, a rabbi, a mother with baby, two arguing men, one with a knife) through six retellings. About 70 percent of detail was lost in the first five transmissions. But the loss wasn't random:

- Extraneous setting details (the subway line, the street, the ads) dropped first.
- Identifying characters dropped next.
- The *core dramatic detail* (the argument, the knife) survived all six transmissions.

Forecast for your app: when users tell each other about it, what survives? If the brand name and core value prop are *part of the dramatic core*, they survive. If they're setting detail ("...some app, I forget what it's called..."), they vanish. This is the operational definition of "valuable virality."

## The Vietnamese Manicurists Epilogue (Why It Matters)

Berger closes the book with the story of Thuan Le and 19 other Vietnamese refugee women at Hope Village outside Sacramento in 1975. Actress Tippi Hedren (star of Hitchcock's *The Birds*) visited regularly. The women admired her manicured nails. Hedren brought her manicurist in weekly for lessons. Twenty women learned to trim cuticles, wrap nails, remove calluses. Hedren got them free beauty-school classes and helped place them in salons in Santa Monica.

They worked long hours, took unwanted jobs. They saved money, opened their own salons, told other Vietnamese immigrants. Today **80 percent of manicurists in California are Vietnamese American; 40 percent nationwide.**

This isn't an isolated pattern: 80 percent of LA donut shops are owned by Cambodian Americans; 65 percent of NYC dry cleaners are Korean-owned; in the 1850s, 60 percent of Boston liquor stores were Irish-run; in the early 1900s, Jews produced 85 percent of US men's clothing.

The mechanism: new immigrants face language barriers and lack credentials. They look at what other recent immigrants are doing (Public). They talk to them (Triggers — employment is top of mind among job-seekers). Successful established immigrants tell exciting (Emotion) stories (Stories) about people they know, partly to look good (Social Currency) and partly to help (Practical Value). All six STEPPS, applied unintentionally, drive an entire occupational migration pattern.

Berger's point: **social epidemics are not driven by a handful of "influentials."** They're driven by ordinary people, each carrying a message through one of the six STEPPS. He invokes sociologist Duncan Watts's forest-fire analogy: a big fire doesn't need a big spark; it needs lots of individual trees willing to burn. Contagious products and ideas spread because hundreds or thousands of regular Janes and Joes choose to pass them along — and they do so for predictable reasons.

The takeaway for the founder: **don't chase Twitter followers with big audiences. Engineer the six principles into the product so any normal user becomes a propagator.**

## How the Six Principles Interact

The strongest campaigns hit multiple STEPPS at once.

- **Blendtec's *Will It Blend?* hits 4–5:** Social Currency (remarkable), Emotion (awe/surprise), Public (videos are observable), Practical Value (the product's strength is demonstrated), Stories (the demonstration *is* the story, brand integral).
- **The $100 cheesesteak hits all 6:** Social Currency (remarkable), Triggers (cheesesteaks everywhere in Philly), Emotion (surprise), Public (the item is on the menu — anyone can verify), Practical Value (quality steakhouse signal), Stories (memorable narrative, brand integral).
- **Movember hits all 6:** Social Currency (insider-cause), Triggers (every November, every facial-hair sighting), Emotion (high-arousal — both amusement and the seriousness of cancer), Public (literally a mustache on your face), Practical Value (cause-related info), Stories (the founder origin and personal stories of why a man is participating).
- **Susan Boyle hits Emotion (awe), Stories (challenge plot), Social Currency (remarkable), Public (TV broadcast + YouTube).**
- **Apple iPod / white headphones hits Public (white cords), Social Currency (premium status), Triggers (visible everywhere on commuters).**
- **Movember and Livestrong hit Public (visible artifact) plus Social Currency (signaling) plus Triggers (every sighting).**

Berger's metaphor: think of the principles less like a recipe (where you need all ingredients) and more like Cobb-salad toppings (any subset works; the more, the better). Some principles are easier to apply for some categories — Practical Value is everywhere; Public is easier with physical products; Emotion is easier with pets, kids, and causes.

## How to Apply to iOS Apps (Synthesis)

This is the section that matters for the founder. Map the STEPPS to the pipeline stages.

### Stage 1 — Ideation

1. **Choose concepts with a built-in inner-remarkability hook.** Before building, ask: "What is the one surprising thing this app does that nobody would believe?" If the answer is "tracks habits" or "helps you focus," kill the concept. If it's "scores how confident you sound" or "wakes you only at the exact stage of REM that won't ruin your day," keep going. Apps in saturated categories must violate the category's schema (JetBlue vs. low-cost-carrier expectations).

2. **Engineer scarcity into the concept itself.** Rue La La worked because the deals were ephemeral. PDT worked because the seats were finite. iOS apps can mirror this — invite-only beta, daily-limited interactions ("Be Real" model — one notification a day, you have two minutes), waitlists. If the app feels infinitely available, it loses signaling power.

3. **Design for behavioral residue at the concept level.** Before writing a line of code, ask: "What does this app leave in the world after the user closes it?" A screenshot? A wearable streak count? A shareable card? An on-feed post? An artifact in the physical world (printed photo, mailed gift)? Apps that disappear after closing don't grow.

4. **Match the Commander's Intent to a frequent environmental trigger.** Habit-tracking app → meals, mornings, evenings. Sleep app → bedtime. Anxiety app → commute, work hours. Avoid building apps tied to infrequent triggers (vacations, life events, weddings). The Boston Market dinner experiment: bind your app to the most frequent stimulus your audience encounters.

5. **Filter ideas against the Cheerios test, not the Disney test.** Ask: "Will the user think about this app once a day in the natural course of their life, without notification?" If no, the retention math will fail no matter how delightful the experience is.

### Stage 2 — Viral Growth (TikTok and Instagram organic)

6. **TikTok hooks are arousal devices.** The first 1.5 seconds must trigger awe, amusement, anger, anxiety, or disgust. NEVER sadness or contentment. "I tried sleeping with my phone in another room for 30 days and what happened scared me" = anxiety. "This app told me my voice sounds 8 years older than I am" = amusement + insecurity (arousal). "Look at what I made in 5 minutes" = awe.

7. **The Berger Exercise Effect applies to TikTok pacing.** Fast-cut, high-energy edits raise viewer arousal independent of content. Slow, contemplative, music-led edits suppress sharing. This is why "running through results" cuts outperform polished narrative ads.

8. **Use anger/anxiety as a productive negative.** "Your dating app is making you depressed and here's the data" = anger at the category, not at the founder. "You're wasting 4 hours a day in apps that don't pay you" = anxiety. Both share faster than "ours makes you happier."

9. **Find your Jared, not your "7 Under 6".** One transformation story (real user) outperforms ten feature carousels. Berger's framing: a guy losing 245 pounds eating fast food. Find the user who broke their expectations using your app. That single human anchors a Challenge plot.

10. **Build Trojan horses, not flexes.** Every piece of content should be a story you'd tell at dinner — *that the app is integral to*. Don't make a viral video about something cool happening to fictional roller babies; make a video about a real user transformation where your app is the cause. Test: if you removed the app from the story, would the story collapse? If yes, you have valuable virality.

11. **Open with a knowledge gap that the app resolves.** "Most people don't know this about their sleep" (then show your dashboard). Gap theory + practical value.

### Stage 4 — UI/UX Design

12. **Design for behavioral residue, not just usage.** Outputs from the app should be screenshot-bait, leave-the-app artifacts, shareable cards. Examples:
    - Spotify Wrapped (annual screenshot card)
    - Strava workout shares
    - Duolingo streak post
    - Calm sleep stories ending with "I just slept X hours" share card
    - BeReal posts (the residue *is* the product)
    Every meaningful interaction should end with "share this moment" as a one-tap option, branded.

13. **Add white-headphone equivalents.** What's the visual signal that someone is using your app that's recognizable to others? Sound? Color palette? Distinctive screenshot template? Apple's logo flip was a tiny detail; design for distinctive recognizability.

14. **Add Foursquare-style game mechanics.** Levels, badges, streaks. Display them in two places: (a) inside the app for intrinsic motivation, (b) outside the app (shareable card, Instagram Story export, web profile) for social currency. The Delta Platinum friend posts on Facebook — that's the goal.

15. **Make scoring legible to outsiders.** Olympic gold > sapphire/diamond/jade card. If your app has "Pro" and "Elite" and "Master" and "Grandmaster" levels, only insiders know what they mean. Use the food-traffic-light principle — make rank immediately decodable to non-users.

16. **Make the private public.** If the app deals with a behavior people are private about (mental health, dating, finance), find the moustache equivalent — a public-facing badge of participation that signals the *cause*, not the *user's individual struggle*. Movember model.

### Stage 5 — Onboarding

17. **Open with a gap, not a tutorial.** "Most people sleep 6 hours. Yours was 5.4 last week. Here's why." Trigger curiosity (Loewenstein gap theory) and use it to pull the user through.

18. **Use a Mother Teresa-style single-person framing.** "Sarah, age 31, lost 18 lbs in 11 weeks using these three habits." One named user > "Our users average X."

19. **Embed the trigger in onboarding.** Ask the user when they want to use the app, link it to a daily cue (alarm, meal, commute), and make that cue part of the notification copy.

### Stage 6 — Retention Loops

20. **Reward sharing with status, not money.** Foursquare didn't pay people to check in; airlines don't pay people to fly more. They paid in *status*. Referral programs should reward both sides with status artifacts (badges, leaderboards, exclusive features) more than cash. Cash kills intrinsic motivation (Lepper / Greene / Nisbett 1973).

21. **Build behavioral residue at the moment of value, not at signup.** The user just hit a milestone — that's the moment to push a one-tap share, not at install (when there's nothing to share yet).

22. **Make the streak the brand.** Streaks (Duolingo, Snapchat) function as game mechanics + behavioral residue + scarcity (lose it and it's gone). Build something a user can't easily replicate by quitting and restarting.

### Stage 8 — Paywall

23. **Use the Rule of 100 on pricing.** Subscriptions under $100 (annual at $79.99): frame as "65% off." Pricing over $100 (lifetime at $149): frame as "Save $200." Don't mismatch — "Save $5" on a $25/month sub is weaker than "20% off."

24. **Anchor with a high reference price.** "Most therapy apps charge $400/month. We're $9.99." Pure prospect-theory move.

25. **Use restrictive deals.** "First 100 users only" — boosts perceived value and creates scarcity-based social currency for the people who got in.

26. **WIIFY explicitly + the Three Whys.** Don't say "Premium unlocks analytics." Drill: Why do they want analytics? → To know what's costing them time. Why? → To get hours back. Why? → To live the life they meant to live. Premium copy: "Premium tells you the one habit eating 90 minutes a day so you get your evenings back."

### Stage 10 — App Store Listing

27. **Subtitle = high-concept pitch in a triggered phrase.** Pair the app with the most frequent daily stimulus its audience faces. "Sleep meditation for night owls." "Focus timer for ADHD founders." Schema + frequent cue.

28. **First screenshot = remarkable, not summary.** Open a gap. Not "Welcome to ___." Something like "Your sleep score is 73 — here's the one thing dragging it down."

29. **Embed a Jared in the screenshots.** Use a real user transformation flow — before → struggle → after. Specific people doing specific things (Heath: Velcro theory) carry more credibility than feature lists.

30. **One Sinatra-test credential + one human-scale stat + one story-shape.** "Featured in Apple's 'Apps We Love.' 24,000 users sleep 47 minutes more per night. Started by a founder who couldn't sleep after their startup failed." Three sentences, three principles.

### Universal — Don't Make Berger's Mistakes

31. **Don't make a Roller Babies video.** Don't build content unrelated to the app just for views. If you removed your app from the post, would the post make less sense? If no, the virality won't be valuable.

32. **Don't make a GoldenPalace.com stunt.** Don't sponsor unrelated chaos. The Olympic streaker won 24 hours of attention; nobody remembered the casino. App equivalents: random TikTok dances, unrelated controversy, paid celebrity posts. Brand has to be in the bloodstream of the story.

33. **Don't normalize the wrong behavior.** Hornik / anti-drug ads / Cialdini / petrified wood. If you tell users "most people don't stick with this kind of app" while trying to retain them, you've made the failure mode the social proof. Frame around the positive norm.

34. **Don't pay for word of mouth.** Once you pay, intrinsic motivation collapses. Let status, scarcity, and identity do the work.

35. **Build all six STEPPS into the launch checklist.** Before launch:
    - **Social Currency:** What's the inner remarkability? Is there a game mechanic? Insider feel?
    - **Triggers:** What daily cue triggers thoughts of the app? Is it frequent enough?
    - **Emotion:** What high-arousal emotion does first use produce? (Not contentment.)
    - **Public:** What's visible? What's the behavioral residue?
    - **Practical Value:** What's the news-you-can-use angle? Rule of 100?
    - **Stories:** What's the Trojan horse the user tells at dinner? Is the app integral?

If three or fewer answers are sharp, the launch will struggle no matter how good the product is.
