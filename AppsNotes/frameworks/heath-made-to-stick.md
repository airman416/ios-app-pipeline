# Made to Stick — Chip & Dan Heath (Key Extracts for iOS App System)

Source: Full book read. Apply across all stages — especially Stage 1 (Ideation), Stage 2 (Viral Growth), Stage 5 (Onboarding), Stage 8 (Paywall), and Stage 10 (App Store).

## Core Thesis

Some ideas survive and spread; others die. The difference is not luck, talent, or budget — it's design. A "sticky" idea is one that is **understood, remembered, and changes behavior or opinion**. The book argues that stickiness is engineerable. There are six traits sticky ideas share, and one villain that prevents most communicators from using them.

The Heaths' definition of sticky: an idea that the audience grasps quickly, retains over time, and acts on. The Kidney Heist urban legend sticks effortlessly with no marketing budget. Most corporate communication does not stick despite enormous investment. The difference is structural, not topical.

## The Villain: The Curse of Knowledge

Once you know something, you cannot un-know it, and you struggle to imagine what it's like to not know it. Experts cannot communicate to novices because they cannot reconstruct the novice's state of mind.

**The Tappers and Listeners experiment (Elizabeth Newton, Stanford, 1990):**
- Tappers were given a list of 25 well-known songs ("Happy Birthday," "Star-Spangled Banner") and asked to tap the rhythm on a table.
- Listeners had to guess the song from the tapping.
- Tappers predicted listeners would guess correctly 50% of the time.
- Actual hit rate: 2.5% (3 of 120).
- Tappers were astonished. To them, the song was obvious — they could *hear it* in their heads. To listeners, it was just disconnected taps, like bad Morse code.

This is the model for every failed communication. A CEO talking about "maximizing shareholder value" hears a rich tune in her head; her employees hear taps. A teacher explaining mitosis hears the elegance of cell division; students hear vocabulary.

**Two ways to beat the Curse:**
1. Don't learn anything (impossible/undesirable).
2. Transform your ideas using the SUCCESs principles.

The Curse of Knowledge is invoked throughout the book as the reason smart, experienced people produce dead, jargon-laden messages. It's why "It's the economy, stupid" works and "Our mission is to leverage cross-functional synergies" doesn't.

## The SUCCESs Framework

Six traits of sticky ideas. Not a formula — a checklist. Few sticky ideas have all six, but the more, the better.

- **S**imple
- **U**nexpected
- **C**oncrete
- **C**redible
- **E**motional
- **S**tories

Two steps to use it: **Step 1: Find the core. Step 2: Translate the core using the SUCCESs checklist.**

## 1. SIMPLE — Find the Core and Make It Compact

**Definition:** Simple = **Core + Compact**. Not "dumbed down." Not a sound bite. A *proverb*: a short sentence that captures a deep truth so well it can guide thousands of decisions.

> "A designer knows he has achieved perfection not when there is nothing left to add, but when there is nothing left to take away." — Saint-Exupéry

### Why it works (the psychology)

- **Decision paralysis.** Tversky & Shafir showed that uncertainty and choice abundance freeze people. In their famous study, students who'd just taken an exam were offered a Hawaii vacation. If told they passed, 57% bought. If told they failed, 54% bought. If they didn't know yet, 61% paid $5 to wait — even though they'd buy either way. **Conclusion: even irrelevant uncertainty paralyzes.** Another study: when students were offered a lecture or studying, 21% chose to study. When offered lecture, study, or a foreign film, 40% chose to study. Adding a second good option made study more likely because the choice between two good options paralyzed. **Cores rescue people from decision angst.**
- **Bandwidth.** We can remember only ~7 chunks at a time. JFKFBINATOUPSNASAIRS (raw letters) is unrememberable. JFK FBI NATO UPS NASA IRS (chunked) is trivial. **Cores let you pack more meaning per byte by tapping existing schemas.**

### Sub-frameworks and tactics

**A. Commander's Intent (CI).** The Army discovered that detailed battle plans become useless on contact with the enemy. So every order now starts with a Commander's Intent — a one-line statement of the operation's desired end-state. Officers are told to complete two sentences:
- "If we do nothing else during tomorrow's mission, we must _____."
- "The single, most important thing we must do tomorrow is _____."

**B. Forced prioritization / Burying the lead.** Journalism teaches you to put the most important thing first (the inverted pyramid). "Burying the lead" is the journalism crime of letting the most important fact slip too far down. Nora Ephron's high school journalism teacher gave students facts about a faculty trip to Sacramento with Margaret Mead etc. Students wrote leads listing all the speakers. The teacher said: "The lead is — *there will be no school next Thursday.*" Ephron realized journalism is not regurgitation; it's figuring out the point.

**C. Proverbs as the model.** "A bird in the hand is worth two in the bush" — 2,500 years old, exists in 50+ languages. Compact, profound, behavior-shaping. The Golden Rule is the ultimate sticky idea: one sentence that can guide a lifetime. **Aim for proverbs, not sound bites.**

**D. "Names, names, and names."** Hoover Adams, publisher of the Dunn, NC *Daily Record*, achieved 112% community penetration by ruthlessly focusing on local names in the paper. His Commander's Intent: "Names, names, and names." He'd hire more typesetters if reporters could fill more pages with names. He'd reprint the phone book if it would help. The proverb guided 20,000 issues of decisions he wasn't personally there for.

**E. "If you say three things, you don't say anything."** James Carville wrote three slogans on a whiteboard for Clinton's 1992 campaign. Only "It's the economy, stupid" was meant to be public — the others were reminders to focus. The point: even great ideas have to give way to the one most important idea.

**F. THE low-fare airline.** Herb Kelleher said he could teach you to run Southwest in 30 seconds: "We are THE low-fare airline." When marketing suggested adding a chicken Caesar salad, the answer is: "Does it make us THE low-fare airline on this route? Then no salad." The core guides all decisions, top to bottom.

**G. Palm Pilot / wooden block.** Jeff Hawkins fought feature creep by carrying a wooden block the size of the Palm Pilot. Anyone proposing a feature had to point at the block and show where it'd fit. The product became defined more by what it was *not* than what it was.

**H. Schemas (the Pomelo principle).** A pomelo is "the largest citrus fruit with a thick rind, light yellow to coral pink flesh, varying from juicy to dry, spicy-sweet to tart." vs. "A pomelo is basically a supersized grapefruit with a very thick soft rind." The second explanation works because it taps an existing schema (grapefruit) you already have, and modifies it. **Use what's there.**

**I. High-concept pitches.** Hollywood greenlights $100M movies based on a one-sentence analogy.
- *Speed* = "Die Hard on a bus."
- *Alien* = "Jaws on a spaceship."
- *13 Going on 30* = "Big for girls."
- *E.T.* = "Lost alien befriends lonely boy to get home."

These compress an enormous amount of decision-guidance (action director or indie? $10M or $100M? Big star or ensemble?) into five words. The trick is invoking an existing schema and modifying it.

**J. Generative analogies.** Some metaphors don't just clarify — they generate new thinking. Disney employees are "cast members." They don't interview, they audition. They don't wear uniforms, they wear costumes. Customers are guests. When in public, they're "onstage." This metaphor guides behavior across thousands of unscripted moments. **Contrast:** Subway calls workers "sandwich artists" — useless, because artists are defined by individual expression, which is exactly what Subway doesn't want.

**Key takeaway:** Accuracy is not the goal — usefulness is. "Maximize shareholder value" is more accurate than "THE low-fare airline" but useless to a flight attendant deciding whether to add chicken salad. **An accurate but useless idea is still useless.**

## 2. UNEXPECTED — Break Their Guessing Machines

**Definition:** Get attention by violating expectations (surprise). Hold attention by opening knowledge gaps (interest). Two questions: *How do I get attention?* and *How do I keep it?*

### Why it works

- Our brains are wired to ignore consistent patterns. Air conditioner hum, traffic noise, the smell of a candle — we notice only when they change. Smart product designers know this. Warning lights blink. Modern sirens wail in complex patterns.
- **Surprise has a biological function:** it acts as an "emergency override." When our guessing machine fails, surprise stops us, focuses attention, and forces us to repair our model of the world. Paul Ekman documented "the surprise brow" — eyebrows up, eyes widened (broader field of view), jaw drops, muscles slack. The body wants to take in more info.

### Sub-frameworks and tactics

**A. Break the schema.** The Enclave minivan ad: a perfect minivan family commercial — boy with football helmet, sliding doors, cup holders, 150 cable channels — and then a car broadsides the minivan. "Didn't see that coming? No one ever does. Buckle up." It violates two schemas at once: how minivan ads work, and how routine neighborhood trips end.

**B. Avoid gimmicky surprise. Surprise must produce insight (post-dictability).** PHRAUG and TAYBL raise eyebrows then click into FROG and TABLE — surprise + insight. HENSION and BARDLE just frustrate — surprise without insight. The Super Bowl 2000 wolves-attacking-marching-band ad: memorable but useless because no one remembers what it advertised. **Test:** does the surprise reinforce the core?

**C. The Nordstrom stories.** "The Nordie who ironed a customer's shirt for an afternoon meeting." "The Nordie who refunded money for tire chains — even though Nordstrom doesn't sell tire chains." "The Nordie who warmed customers' cars in winter." These attack the schema of "customer service = get them out the door fast" and replace it with the Nordstrom schema.

**D. Common sense is the enemy.** If your message sounds like common sense, it floats in one ear out the other. Your job is to find what's *uncommon* in your message. Process:
1. Identify the core message.
2. Figure out what's counterintuitive about it (the unexpected implication).
3. Communicate in a way that breaks the audience's guessing machine on that exact dimension.
4. Help them repair the machine afterward.

**E. Mystery — The Gap Theory of Curiosity (George Loewenstein).** Curiosity happens when we feel a gap between what we know and what we want to know. Gaps *cause pain*. We watch bad movies to the end because not knowing how they end is more painful than watching. **Practical implication: open the gap before you try to fill it.** Don't lead with the facts; lead with what they don't know.

Robert Cialdini found that the best scientific writing for general audiences started with a mystery. An astronomer's article on Saturn's rings: "How can we account for the most spectacular planetary feature in our solar system? How could three internationally acclaimed research teams come to totally different conclusions about what the rings are made of?" Answer (20 pages later): dust. Nobody cares about dust. But everyone reads to the end.

**F. McKee's Turning Points.** Robert McKee on screenwriting: *Curiosity is the intellectual need to answer questions and close open patterns.* Stories work by posing questions and opening situations. Every scene should be a Turning Point that makes the audience ask *What happens next? How will it turn out?*

**G. The news-teaser approach.** "There's an invisible chemical in your home — and it may be killing you right now." Shift the question from "what info do I need to convey?" to "what questions do I want my audience to ask?"

**H. Sequencing.** Treasure maps work because they're vague. They give you just enough to find the first landmark. If MapQuest produced treasure maps, adventure movies would die. Don't dump info — drop a clue, then another. **Communication should resemble flirting more than lecturing.**

**I. Battle overconfidence.** Gap theory only works if people realize they have gaps. People are overconfident (one study: people estimated they'd identified 75% of solutions to a problem; they'd actually gotten <30%). To break this: make them commit publicly to a prediction before revealing the answer (Eric Mazur's "concept testing"); create disagreement (students with disagreement gave up recess to watch a follow-up film, students with consensus didn't).

**J. Audacious goals.** Sony's "pocketable radio" (1953) when radios were furniture and the licensing body said it was impossible. JFK's "Man on the moon, by the end of this decade, returned safely." Big enough to surprise but not so big as to paralyze. Engineers immediately started thinking "well, first we'd need…"

## 3. CONCRETE — Help Them See It

**Definition:** Use language and imagery that can be examined with the senses. The opposite of "high-performance," "synergy," "diversity," "world-class." V8 engines, Darth Vader toothbrushes, BBs in a bucket — these are concrete.

> Most of the time, concreteness boils down to **specific people doing specific things**.

### Why it works

**A. The Velcro Theory of Memory.** Memory is not one filing cabinet — it's Velcro. One side is hooks, the other side is loops. Every concrete sensory detail you add gives the idea another hook to snag in the listener's mind. "Capital of Kansas" has few hooks. "Childhood home" has thousands.

**B. Concrete sticks across cultures and centuries.** Aesop's "Fox and the Grapes" → "sour grapes" exists in dozens of languages 2,500 years later. The Iliad and Odyssey survived oral transmission because abstractions evaporated and concrete details stayed.

**C. Cognitive research:** people remember concrete nouns ("bicycle," "avocado") far better than abstract ones ("justice," "personality").

**D. Concreteness coordinates action.** Abstract messages mean different things to different people. "World-class customer service" can mean anything. "A Nordie who irons a shirt for a customer" cannot be misinterpreted.

### Sub-frameworks and tactics

**A. Specific people doing specific things.** Don't say "Nature Conservancy protects ecologically important areas." Say "We're protecting the Mount Hamilton Wilderness." TNC realized that "2 million acres" was unbuyable as a concept. So they reframed: 50 *landscapes* over 10 years, each one given a name. "The Mount Hamilton Wilderness" is an eco-celebrity. A name made it real.

**B. Computing in Context.** Japanese math teachers explaining subtraction: "You had 100 yen, you bought a notebook for 70 yen. How much is left?" or laying out 50 tiles, removing 30, asking how many are left. Asian schools use concrete computing in 61% of math lessons vs. 31% in US schools. Asian kids outperform.

**C. Case-study learning.** Two Georgia State professors taught an entire intro accounting course through the soap opera of "Kris and Sandy" — two imaginary sophomores starting a teen-driver-monitoring product called Safe Night Out. Students became their accountant. They learned fixed vs. variable costs not as a definition but through *Kris and Sandy bouncing checks despite record sales*. Two years later, the case-study students outscored peers by 12 points on a follow-up course exam — biggest gains for C students.

**D. Concrete = useful for novices.** Abstraction is the luxury of the expert. "Cook until it reaches a hearty consistency" is meaningless until you've cooked it a few times. Then it makes sense. **Always provide concrete examples before abstractions.**

**E. The Velcro takeaway for app builders:** every concrete detail = another hook. A name, an image, a story, a specific moment — each one increases retention.

## 4. CREDIBLE — Help Them Believe

**Definition:** Sticky ideas need to be believable. There are external sources of credibility (authorities, antiauthorities) and internal sources (details, statistics, the Sinatra Test). The best is to outsource credibility to the audience itself (testable credentials).

### External credibility

**A. Authorities.** A surgeon general's statement is believed without question.

**B. Antiauthorities.** Sometimes a non-expert with personal experience is more credible than an authority. Pam Laffin — a young, dying, smoking mother — was the centerpiece of the Massachusetts antismoking campaign. The Doe Fund sends a formerly homeless driver, Dennis, to pick up potential donors. Forty-five minutes in the car with Dennis beats any director's pitch. **Honesty and trustworthiness > status.**

### Internal credibility (the message vouches for itself)

**A. Vivid details.** The Boyfriend's Death urban legend always specifies a particular road. Specifics make stories feel real.

The **Darth Vader Toothbrush experiment** (Shedler & Manis, 1986): jurors evaluated a fictional custody case. All heard 8 arguments for and 8 against the mother. One group heard pro-mother arguments with vivid irrelevant details ("the boy uses a Darth Vader toothbrush"); the anti-mother arguments were pallid. The other group got the reverse. Even though the details were irrelevant to the case, jurors rated the mother as significantly more fit when *her* side had the details. **Concrete details make claims more credible even when the details don't matter.**

**The Liz Lerman Dance Exchange.** When skeptics challenged the dance company's claim that "diversity" was a core value ("you're just a bunch of 25-year-old dancers in different colors"), the artistic director responded with a detail: their longest-serving company member is Thomas Dwyer, a 73-year-old former government employee who joined in 1988 with no dance background. Silence in the room.

**B. Statistics.** Use them sparingly. Statistics are credible only in **relationship**, and you should make the relationship vivid.

**Beyond War and the BBs.** Geoff Ainscow demonstrated the nuclear arsenal by dropping one BB into a metal bucket ("the Hiroshima bomb"), then 10 BBs ("the missiles on one US/Soviet submarine"), then 5,000 BBs all at once ("the world's nuclear arsenal"). The roar was terrifying. **No one remembers the number 5,000. Everyone remembers the bucket.**

**C. The Human-Scale Principle.** "Scientists calculated to an accuracy of throwing a rock from the sun to earth and hitting within 1/3 of a mile of dead center" — 58% said impressive. "Throwing a rock from New York to LA and hitting within 2/3 of an inch" — 83% said impressive. Same accuracy. Human-scale wins.

**Stephen Covey's poll soccer analogy:** raw stats said only 37% of employees understood the goal, only 20% trusted the company. Reframed: "If a soccer team had these scores, only 4 of 11 players would know which goal is theirs, only 2 of 11 would care, and most would be competing against their own team."

**D. The Sinatra Test.** "If I can make it there, I can make it anywhere." One credential, properly chosen, establishes credibility for an entire domain.

Safexpress (Indian shipping firm) pitched a Bollywood studio that worried about piracy. The pitch: "We handled the Indian release of the fifth Harry Potter book — every store, by 8am, no leaks. And we transported your brother's high school board exams." Deal signed.

Bill McDonough convincing chemical execs his "safe textile" process was possible: tested 8,000 chemicals, kept 38, fabrics "safe enough to eat," Swiss inspectors thought their water-quality instruments were broken because the factory effluent was cleaner than the intake. Production costs dropped 20%.

**E. Shark vs. Deer.** Don't say "shark attacks are rare (0.4 deaths/year)." Say: "Which is more likely to kill you, a shark or a deer? Answer: the deer — 300 times more likely (via car collisions)." Audience-validated, unexpected, vivid.

### Outsourcing credibility to the audience

**F. Testable credentials — Where's the Beef?** The Wendy's 1984 campaign didn't say "11% more beef." It showed three old women squinting at a tiny patty on a huge bun. The implicit challenge: *go look for yourself*. Customer belief that Wendy's burger was bigger than Whopper/Big Mac jumped 47% in two months. Revenue +31% in a year.

**G. Reagan's "Are you better off than you were four years ago?"** He could have cited inflation, unemployment, interest rates. Instead he asked the audience to test for themselves.

**H. Emotional Tank exercise.** Positive Coaching Alliance trainers ask coaches to imagine someone just flubbed a play, then say something to *drain* their emotional tank. Coaches do this with creativity and laughter. Then they're asked to *fill* it. The room goes silent. The coaches learn the lesson by experiencing it.

## 5. EMOTIONAL — Make Them Care

**Definition:** Belief is not enough. People have to **feel something** to act. The goal is not manipulation — it's making them care.

### Why it works

**The Mother Teresa Principle.** "If I look at the one, I will act. If I look at the mass, I will not." Researchers gave two groups a Save the Children letter. Group A got statistics about food shortages in Malawi, Zambia, etc. Group B got a story about one girl: "Rokia, 7 years old, lives in Mali." Group B gave more than twice as much. **Then the killer follow-up:** they gave a third group *both* the stats and Rokia's story. Those people gave $1.43 — *less than the Rokia-only group*. Statistics shift the brain into analytical mode, which *suppresses* emotional response. Even worse: when researchers primed people to think analytically *before* showing them the Rokia letter, donations dropped. **The mere act of calculation reduces charity.**

> We are wired to feel things for *people*, not for abstractions.

### Sub-frameworks and tactics

**A. Tap into existing emotions (Association).** The most basic way to make people care is to associate something they don't care about with something they already do.

**The Truth campaign vs. "Think. Don't Smoke."** Two antismoking campaigns ran simultaneously. The Truth campaign: teenagers pile 1,800 body bags outside a tobacco company HQ. Philip Morris's "Think. Don't Smoke" was the opposite — analytical, abstract. Result: 22% spontaneous recall of Truth, 3% of Think. Teens exposed to Truth were 66% *less* likely to smoke. Teens exposed to Think were 36% *more* likely. Truth tapped teenage anti-authority rebellion, redirected at Big Tobacco. **Don't tell teens smoking is dangerous — tell them they're being manipulated by The Man.**

**B. Semantic Stretch — the danger of overused associations.** Words lose power as they're stretched. "Unique" used to mean one-of-a-kind. Newspaper usage of "unique" up 73% over 20 years; "unusual" simultaneously down. Same with "relativity" used as a synonym for "everything is relative" (which is the opposite of what Einstein meant). Same with "sportsmanship" — degraded to mean "didn't punch the ref."

**Honoring the Game.** Jim Thompson of the Positive Coaching Alliance gave up on "sportsmanship" — too weak. He reframed it: "Honoring the Game." Sports patriotism. The game is bigger than the participant. After PCA training, a Dallas basketball league reduced technical fouls from one every 15 games to one every 52. A Northern California baseball league saw 90% fewer ejections, 20% more sign-ups. **When the word is exhausted, find a new frame.**

**C. WIIFY — What's In It For You.** Jerry Weissman: every speech should have an explicit WIIFY (pronounced *whiff-y*). John Caples, the greatest mail-order copywriter ever, said: "Get self-interest into every headline." Famous headlines:
- "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano — But When I Started to Play!"
- "Retire at 55."
- "How You Can Improve Your Memory in One Evening."
- "The Secret of How to Be Taller."

**Spell out the benefit of the benefit.** People don't buy quarter-inch drill bits. They buy quarter-inch holes so they can hang their kids' pictures.

Keep the "self" in self-interest: "**You** enjoy a sense of security when you use Goodyear Tires" — not "people enjoy."

**D. Cable TV in Tempe — visualization beats listing.** Researchers visited homeowners about cable. Group 1 got an abstract description ("CATV will provide a broader entertainment service"). Group 2 was asked to imagine themselves using it ("Take a moment and imagine how *you* will plan in advance, instead of spending money on babysitters and gas…"). A month later, cable arrived. Group 1 subscribed at 20%. Group 2 at 47%. **Tangibility, not magnitude, drives action. Help people see themselves using your thing.**

**E. Don't live in Maslow's basement.** Maslow's hierarchy:
1. Transcendence
2. Self-actualization
3. Aesthetic
4. Learning
5. Esteem
6. Belonging
7. Security
8. Physical

Subsequent research showed there's no hierarchy — people pursue all levels simultaneously. But marketers and managers default to the bottom: security, esteem, physical. **And here's the kicker:** when asked what motivates *us*, we pick higher levels (learning, transcendence, self-actualization). When asked what motivates *others*, we pick lower levels (money, security). We assume everyone else lives in the basement.

**Floyd Lee, mess hall manager in Iraq (Pegasus chow hall).** Same Army food, same Army menu as everyone else. But the prime rib is marinated for two days. Soldiers drive across Baghdad through dangerous roads to eat there. Lee: "I'm not in charge of food service. I'm in charge of morale." That's Transcendence-level motivation.

**F. Identity beats consequence.** James March's two models of decision-making:
1. *Consequence model:* What's in it for me? What's the cost/benefit?
2. *Identity model:* Who am I? What kind of situation is this? What do people like me do in this situation?

**The firemen and the popcorn popper.** A marketer offered firefighters a free popcorn popper just for reviewing a fire-safety film. From a self-interest view: free popper, no downside, just take it. Firefighters' actual response: "Do you think we'd review a fire-safety program for some popcorn popper?!" They didn't see themselves as the kind of people who need bribes to do safety work. **Identity overrode self-interest.**

Political research confirms: self-interest is a "trifling" predictor of voting. Group interest (race, class, ideology, profession) matters more. People ask not "what's in it for me?" but "what's in it for people like me?"

## 6. STORIES — Get Them to Act

**Definition:** Stories are flight simulators for the brain. They prepare us to act in situations we haven't yet faced. They also inspire by association — when we see someone overcome, we believe we can too.

### Why it works

**Stories are mental flight simulators.** UCLA study: students with current stressors were split into three groups.
1. Control group — just got problem-solving advice.
2. Outcome-simulation group — visualized the relief and pride of solving the problem.
3. Event-simulation group — visualized step by step *how the problem arose and was being worked on*.

A week later, the event-simulation group did vastly better — more action taken, more advice sought, more learning reported. **Visualizing the process beats visualizing the outcome.**

Why? Because mental simulation activates the same brain regions as actual action. Imagining the Eiffel Tower moves your eyes upward. Imagining lemon juice makes you salivate. Mental practice produces about **two-thirds the gains of physical practice** (across 35 studies, 3,214 participants — from dart throwing to trombone playing to figure skating).

> A story is powerful because it provides the context missing from abstract prose. It's back to the Velcro theory — the more hooks, the more it sticks. Stories provide emotional, historical, sensory, and procedural hooks all at once.

### The opening story — The Heart Monitor Lies

A nurse is checking on a newborn whose heart monitor reads green. Instead of trusting it, she notices the baby's color is off. She calls the team in; the baby is dying of pneumococcal sepsis. The monitor was misleading. Why does this story stick with new nurses? Because it teaches not a rule ("don't trust monitors blindly") but a *simulation* of the moment when you have to choose between data and your senses.

### The four kinds of stories

**A. Springboard stories (Stephen Denning, World Bank).** Denning was assigned to "look into information" — corporate Siberia. He had to convince senior management to invest in knowledge management. Instead of giving a deck on KM best practices, he told one story: a health worker in Kamana, Zambia, trying to fight malaria, went online (in 1996!) and got the data he needed from the CDC website in Atlanta. *That worker should have been able to tap the World Bank's knowledge.* Within minutes, executives were telling Denning what *they* should do to implement his (now their) idea. **A springboard story lets people see how an existing problem could change. It doesn't push the conclusion — it invites them to discover it.**

### The three inspiration plots (after analyzing hundreds of Chicken Soup stories)

**B. The Challenge Plot.** Protagonist overcomes a formidable obstacle. David and Goliath. Underdog. Rags-to-riches. Triumph of willpower. **Examples:** Jared (Subway), Rose Blumkin (started a furniture business with $500, did $100M/year fifty years later, still worked seven days a week at 100), Lance Armstrong, Rosa Parks, Seabiscuit. **What they inspire:** perseverance. After hearing them you want to work harder, take on more.

**C. The Connection Plot.** People develop a relationship that bridges a gap (racial, class, religious, demographic). The Good Samaritan. Romeo and Juliet. *Titanic.* The Mean Joe Greene Coke commercial (white kid + Black football star + Coke). **What they inspire:** social action. Tolerance. Helping. Love.

**D. The Creativity Plot.** Someone makes a mental breakthrough, solves a puzzle, attacks a problem in a new way. The MacGyver plot. Newton's apple. **Ingersoll-Rand's Drag Test:** a team developing a grinder needed to know if a plastic casing would hold up vs. metal. The old company method would have been months of tensile testing. Instead, on a customer visit, the team tied samples to the rental car bumper and dragged them around the parking lot until the police told them to stop. Both materials held up. Decision made. The story became a permanent symbol of the team's new culture. **What they inspire:** experimentation, fresh approaches.

### Jared and the SUCCESs scorecard

The Heaths show the Subway "Jared" campaign hits all six:
- **Simple:** Eat subs, lose weight.
- **Unexpected:** Lost 245 lbs eating *fast food*.
- **Concrete:** 60-inch waist pants. Foot-long veggie sub for lunch, six-inch turkey for dinner.
- **Credible:** The guy who wore 60-inch pants is the antiauthority — testable credentials (eat at Subway yourself).
- **Emotional:** Individual story (Mother Teresa principle), reaching potential (Maslow).
- **Story:** Challenge plot.

Compare with the previous Subway campaign, "7 Under 6" (seven subs under six grams of fat) — simple but uncompelling, no story, no emotion, no concrete person. The Heaths argue: you could have predicted in advance which would win.

### Spotting beats creating

Most great stories already exist. You don't have to make them up — you have to spot them. The Subway franchisee who recognized Jared. The Nordstrom employee who told the tire-chain story. **Put on Core Idea Glasses** — keep your core message active in the back of your mind so you spot examples when they cross your desk. The barrier is that we file anecdotes as "interesting trivia" instead of as ammunition for our core message.

## What Sticks (Epilogue) — The Audience Has a Vote

Sometimes ideas mutate in the marketplace. Leo Durocher said "Nice guys finish in seventh place" — over decades it became "Nice guys finish last." Sherlock Holmes never said "Elementary, my dear Watson" — but he said both phrases and the audience welded them. James Carville wrote *three* slogans on a whiteboard — only "It's the economy, stupid" stuck.

**The test isn't whether they quote you verbatim. The test is whether the mutated version still hits your core.** If it does, embrace the change.

## How to Apply to iOS Apps (Synthesis)

This is the section that matters for the founder. Each principle mapped to specific decisions in the app pipeline.

### Stage 1 — Ideation: Naming and concept

1. **The high-concept pitch is the test of an app concept.** If you can't write *"X for Y"* or *"X but Z"* — "Calm for kids," "Duolingo for cooking," "Tinder for jobs" — you don't have a concept yet. The pitch must invoke a schema the audience already has and modify it cleanly. If the schema you're invoking is obscure, the app is dead before launch.

2. **Find the Commander's Intent of the app.** Complete: "If our app does nothing else, it must _____." This is the equivalent of "THE low-fare airline." Every screen, feature, paywall copy, and TikTok hook must serve this one sentence. If the answer is "make users sleep through the night" then a chicken-Caesar-salad feature (e.g., a community forum, a leaderboard) doesn't make the cut.

3. **If you say three things, you say nothing.** Most failed first-time apps try to do too much. Pick the one outcome that wins, kill the rest, ship.

4. **Compete on uncommon sense, not common sense.** "Helps you sleep" is common sense for a sleep app. "Pays you to fall asleep" or "uses your last text from your ex as the trigger" is uncommon sense. Find the angle that violates the genre's schema.

### Stage 2 — Viral Growth: TikTok and Instagram hooks

5. **TikTok hooks are gap-theory machines.** The first 1.5 seconds must open a knowledge gap painful enough that the viewer cannot scroll. "I tried sleeping with my phone in my mouth for 30 days" is gap. "Our app helps you sleep better" is filler. Use the news-teaser approach: *"There's one thing your sleep tracker isn't telling you — and it's keeping you tired."*

6. **Schema violation in 1.5 seconds.** The Enclave minivan ad opens like every other minivan ad — then breaks the schema. Same principle: open with what the viewer expects (another fitness ad, another productivity hack), then violate. Post-dictable surprise — they have to *get it* after the twist, not be confused.

7. **One concrete person, not a feature list.** The Mother Teresa principle. A real before/after of a user (or the founder) hits 10x harder than a feature carousel. Jared > "7 Under 6." Find your Jared.

8. **Don't list — simulate.** Show the user using the app inside their life. Cable TV in Tempe: ask the viewer to imagine themselves using it. *"Imagine waking up at 7 without an alarm, on day 14."* Better than *"Helps you sleep deeper."*

9. **Testable credentials beat claims.** Don't say "the best meditation app." Say "Try this for 60 seconds — if you don't feel calmer, delete it." Wendy's "Where's the Beef?" outsourced credibility to customers. Invite the viewer to verify with their own senses.

10. **Use antiauthorities.** A real user who quit smoking with your app, or who fixed their anxiety, or who hit their goal — more credible than a polished CEO testimonial. Pam Laffin > the Surgeon General.

### Stage 5 — Onboarding (first 30 seconds)

11. **Onboarding must do one job: communicate the Commander's Intent.** Every onboarding screen that doesn't move the user toward "I get what this app does for me, and I see myself using it" is friction. Burying the lead = users churn before paywall.

12. **Open the gap before filling it.** "Most people sleep 6 hours. Yours is 5.4. Here's why." Pose the question; let curiosity pull them through onboarding.

13. **Use schemas the user already has.** Don't teach a new mental model — anchor on a known one. *"Like a habit tracker, but the streak resets on lies."* > *"Welcome to our 7-pillar accountability system."*

14. **Concrete first, abstract later.** Show the user *doing one specific thing* in the first 10 seconds, before explaining the philosophy. Computing in Context — Asian math classrooms started with "you have 100 yen…" not "subtraction is the inverse operation."

15. **Make the user simulate using the app, not just see screenshots.** "Tap to log your first meal." "Type the name of someone you want to call this week." Action beats description.

### Stage 8 — Paywall copy

16. **WIIFY explicitly.** Don't say "Premium unlocks advanced analytics." Say "Premium tells you the one habit costing you 90 minutes of sleep per night." Benefit of the benefit.

17. **Sell the dream outcome, not features.** Concrete future state of the user. "Imagine three months from now, sleeping 8 hours, waking without alarms, no caffeine after 2pm." Tempe study: imagining the outcome doubled conversions.

18. **Stay out of Maslow's basement.** Don't pitch only on Security ("don't lose your data") or Esteem ("look successful"). Pitch on Learning, Transcendence, Self-Actualization where appropriate. "Become the kind of person who…"

19. **Identity over consequence.** "Subscribers are the kind of people who don't settle for half-tracked goals." Triggers the identity model, not the calculator model.

20. **Use testable credentials.** Free trial = the ultimate "try before you buy." Don't argue value — let them feel it. But also build *internal* credibility before the trial offer: one concrete user story, one Sinatra-level credential ("Featured in X," "Used by Y professional teams").

### Stage 10 — App Store listing

21. **App name = the proverb.** A short phrase that captures the core. "Calm." "Headspace." "Streaks." "Forest." These are proverbs disguised as names — they tell you the dream outcome or the mental model in one or two words.

22. **Subtitle = high-concept pitch.** "Sleep meditations & sounds." Six words. Schema (sleep meditation), modification (sounds). Don't bury the lead.

23. **Screenshots tell a Challenge plot.** Use a real user as the through-line. Before → struggle → after. The Subway/Jared formula, condensed to 5 screenshots. Each screenshot is a Turning Point that opens a small gap propelling the eye to the next.

24. **First screenshot opens a gap, doesn't summarize.** Most apps' first screenshot says "Welcome to ___." Wrong. Should say "What's stealing your sleep? →" — the news-teaser approach.

25. **Description: one Sinatra credential, one human-scale stat, one story, one WIIFY.** Not a feature list. Skip "synergy" — write like Wendy's, like John Caples, like Aesop.

### Universal app-system principles

26. **Curse of Knowledge is the founder's biggest enemy.** You have lived inside the app for months. The user has 30 seconds. Get a non-technical friend to use the app cold. If they can't articulate the Commander's Intent after 60 seconds, you're tapping a song no one else hears.

27. **Spot more than you create.** When a real user posts a transformation, screenshots a milestone, or sends a thank-you email — that's a Jared. Most founders treat these as morale boosters; instead, treat them as marketing ammo. Maintain Core Idea Glasses.

28. **Every feature is either core or chicken Caesar salad.** When in doubt, cut. The Palm Pilot wooden block test: where does this fit in the smallest viable product?

29. **The numerator vs. the SUCCESs:** Hormozi tells you to increase dream outcome and reduce friction. The Heaths tell you to make people *understand*, *believe*, and *care* — so they'll stick around long enough to experience that value. Both are necessary. Stickiness keeps them in the funnel; value keeps them paying.

30. **Audience has a vote.** If users start describing your app in their own words on TikTok, listen. If their version still hits the core, embrace the mutation. Don't fight the audience — they're testing your idea in the wild.
