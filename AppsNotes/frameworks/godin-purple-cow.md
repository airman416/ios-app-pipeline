## 1. Core Thesis

The central argument of Purple Cow is expressed in a single experience: driving through the French countryside, Godin's family was enchanted by cows in the fields for the first twenty minutes. Then they started ignoring them. The cows were perfect cows, beautiful cows — but they were boring. A purple cow would be different. And that difference is the entire point.

"Something **remarkable** is worth talking about. Worth noticing. Exceptional. New. Interesting. It's a Purple Cow. Boring stuff is invisible. It's a brown cow."

The book's argument in one sentence: in a world where consumers can ignore almost everything, the only products that spread are the ones that are genuinely remarkable — meaning, literally, worth remarking about.

**The old rule:** Create safe, ordinary products and combine them with great marketing.

**The new rule:** Create remarkable products that the right people seek out.

**The central inversion:** Safe is risky. The riskiest thing you can do is be boring. This is not rhetoric — Godin argues it as a business fact. Boring products are invisible. Invisible products fail. Therefore being boring guarantees failure. The only real risk worth fearing is the risk of being ordinary.

**The marketing department is the wrong frame.** Godin explicitly defines "remarkable marketing" not as better advertising, but as "the art of building things worth noticing right into your product or service. Not slapping on marketing as a last-minute add-on, but understanding that if your offering itself isn't remarkable, it's invisible."

**The opposite of remarkable is not bad.** This is a critical distinction Godin draws explicitly: "The opposite of remarkable is 'very good.'" Companies that make very good products confuse quality with remarkability. Getting you there safely does not make an airline worth talking about. That's what's supposed to happen.

## 2. The Death of the TV-Industrial Complex

**What the TV-Industrial Complex was:** A self-reinforcing economic engine built on a simple loop — find a large, growing, undominated market niche; build a factory; buy massive TV advertising; the ads generate retail distribution and sales; sales fund more ads; the cycle compounds. Brands like Revlon, Cap'n Crunch, and the entire Procter & Gamble lineup (Tide, Bounce, Crest, Pampers, etc.) were built this way. Godin calls out the P&G brand list specifically: can you read "Bold, Bounce, Bounty, Cascade, Charmin, Cheer..." without filling your head with jingles? That's the TV-Industrial Complex at work.

The system was brilliant because it was self-reinforcing. TV ads built brand value. Brand value commanded premium pricing. Premium pricing funded more TV ads. And crucially: consumers were trained to believe "as seen on TV" was proof of product quality. Non-advertised brands lost distribution. The whole economy organized itself around this loop for roughly fifty years.

**Why it worked:** Two conditions made it possible — (1) consumers had disposable income and genuine unmet desires, and (2) people were actually paying attention to TV. Both conditions are now gone.

**Why it stopped:** Three simultaneous collapses:

- Consumers stopped paying attention. Jaded by decades of advertising, buyers now ignore interruption by default. They are too busy, have too many choices, and have solved most of their basic needs already.
- The supply of unsatisfied markets dried up. Aspirin, yoga books, laundry detergent — every obvious category is over-served. There are more than a hundred aspirin variants and more than five hundred yoga books. There is no more empty shelf to fill.
- The advertising pipeline broke. Mass media fragmented. Cable, internet, and digital media gave people the ability to opt out of ads entirely. The average consumer now encounters far more messages and processes far fewer of them.

**The "squeeze play" diagram (index-24_1.png):** A simple two-arrow chart showing "Available choices" trending sharply up and "Available time" trending sharply down. More options, less time — the structural reason why mass marketing fails. This is the most important diagram in the book: consumers cannot pay attention to your ad because they have too many things competing for their attention and not enough time to evaluate them.

**What replaced the TV-Industrial Complex:** Word of mouth through passionate early adopters — but at a different speed and scale than the old word of mouth. "Instead of products succeeding by slow and awkward word of mouth, the power of our new networks allows remarkable ideas to diffuse through segments of the population at rocket speed."

**The Before/After table Godin draws explicitly:**

| TV-Industrial Age | Post-TV Age |
|---|---|
| Average products | Remarkable products |
| Advertise to anyone | Advertise to the early adopter |
| Fear of failure | Fear of fear |
| Long cycles | Short cycles |
| Small changes | Big changes |

**The empirical proof from Interbrand's top 100 brands (2002):** Godin analyzes the list carefully. Of the top 100 global brands by value, seventy were built more than twenty-five years ago when TV advertising was cheap and effective. Of the remaining thirty, half were built almost entirely through word of mouth (HP, Oracle, Nintendo, SAP, Canon, IKEA, Sun, Yahoo, Ericsson, Motorola, Amazon, Prada, Starbucks, Polo Ralph Lauren, Armani). Only a tiny handful — perhaps seven — built their brands the traditional old-fashioned way during the modern era. "Just one of the companies on the list is the product of the last ten years of media excess. Only AOL was able to spend hundreds of millions of dollars and convert that investment into a valuable brand." The implication: if 94 percent of valuable brands either pre-date the TV-Industrial Complex or were built through word of mouth, why do you believe running ads will work for you?

## 3. The Diffusion of Ideas (Rogers' Curve)

Godin builds on Geoff Moore's *Crossing the Chasm* and Rogers' diffusion of innovation curve throughout the book. This is the structural framework for understanding how any idea reaches a market.

**The five groups, left to right on the curve:**

1. **Innovators** — people who like having something first, regardless of whether they need it. They sit in the front row at fashion shows, go to every trade conference, read edgy journals. They adopt for the experience of adopting.

2. **Early adopters** — people who can actually benefit from a new product and actively seek to maintain their edge. They have real needs the new product solves. They are sizable, willing to spend money, and crucially — they are watching for new things and willing to take a risk on them.

3. **Early majority** — followers. They will try something if enough of their trusted peers have already vetted it. They are not looking for new; they need social proof before moving.

4. **Late majority** — even more conservative. They wait until a product is fully established and often don't move until the old thing becomes unavailable.

5. **Laggards** — they get around to buying a cassette deck when the rest of us have moved on to CDs. These people are the true adapters (not adopters). They resist until the old way becomes impossible.

**The critical insight Godin returns to repeatedly:** Mass marketing aims for the center of the curve — the early and late majority — because that is the largest group by volume. But this strategy is now broken for two reasons:

First, the early and late majority ignore advertising. They won't listen to you. They only listen to their peers. And their peers are the early adopters. So the path to the early majority is always through the early adopters. You cannot shortcut it.

Second, the early adopters — the only people willing to try something new — are specifically repelled by products designed for the mass market. Products rounded and smoothed and compromised to appeal to everybody appeal to nobody who matters. "By targeting the center of the market and designing the product accordingly, these marketers waste their marketing dollars."

**Godin's precise statement of the sequence:** "You must design a product that is remarkable enough to attract the early adopters — but is flexible enough and attractive enough that those early adopters can then sell it to the early majority." And then critically: "After the early adopters embrace what you're selling, they are the ones who will sell it to the early majority — not you. And they will sell it poorly."

That last clause is important. The early adopters will spread the word imperfectly, in their own language, through their own channels. You do not control how the idea spreads. You can only control whether it's worth spreading in the first place.

**The chasm:** Godin references Moore's chasm — the gap between early adopters and early majority — as the graveyard of most products. Ideas that don't cross this gap die there. The only thing that gets an idea across the chasm is a remarkable product that early adopters are passionate enough about to actively sell to their peers.

**Designing for the left side of the curve is not niche thinking — it's the only path to mass.** There is no direct path from you to the mass market. The mass market is only reachable through the sneezers who live on the left side of the curve.

## 4. Otaku

**Definition:** Otaku (Japanese) describes something that is more than a hobby but a little less than an obsession. "Otaku is the overwhelming desire that gets someone to drive across town to try a new ramen-noodle shop that got a great review. Otaku is the desire to find out everything about Lionel's new digital locomotive — and to tell your fellow hobbyists about it."

**Why otaku matters:** Consumers with otaku are the sneezers you seek. They will take the time to learn about your product, take the risk to try it, and take their friends' time to tell them about it. Crucially: "some markets have more otaku-stricken consumers than others." The task of the remarkable marketer is to identify these markets and focus on them, regardless of their relative size.

**The hot sauce example:** There is a healthy vein of hot-sauce otaku in the US. Dozens of entrepreneurs have created successful businesses — Dave's Insanity, Blair's After Death Hot Sauce, Mad Dog 357, Pain 100%, etc. — with no advertising, no mass marketing. At the same time, nobody has made any impact selling mustard. Mustard is consumed by more people than hot sauce. Yet hot sauce is a business and mustard is not. "Because very few people will order mustard by mail or request a different brand at a restaurant. They don't have the otaku."

**The insight:** Smart businesses target markets where there's already otaku — where consumers are passionately invested in the category and will actively seek out and talk about new entries. This is the precondition for a Purple Cow to work.

**Otaku as the originating passion:** "Where does remarkable come from? Often, it comes from passionate people who are making something for themselves." Godin cites Howard Schultz of Starbucks (genuine coffee otaku — he spent months in Italy learning to drink coffee, refers to everyone who hasn't had their first cup as "pre-caffeinated"). The Apple iPod, the Burton snowboard, the Vanguard mutual fund, the Learjet — all came from people with an otaku. Starbucks' chocolate, by contrast, is merely adequate: "Obviously, Howard doesn't know chocolate the way he knows coffee." You can feel the absence of otaku in a product.

**The practical question:** "The number-one question about the Purple Cow is, 'How do I know it's remarkable?' This question almost always comes from the people who don't have the otaku." John Scharffenberger of Scharffen Berger Chocolate has no trouble telling great chocolate from ordinary chocolate. He gets it. If you have to ask whether your product is remarkable, you may not be the right person to build it — or you may need to go deeper into the category until you genuinely develop the obsession.

**Hiring for otaku:** When Godin was building his first company, he always asked potential employees how often they went to the bookstore. "People who don't love shopping for books obviously don't have the book otaku, and they're going to have a harder time inventing books for people who do." Everyone at Patagonia is an outdoor nut. When the surf is up, the offices empty. This chaos is the price of having a team that knows remarkable when they see it.

**The Dineh Mohajer example (index-114_1.png):** The photo shows Dineh Mohajer, founder of Hard Candy cosmetics, with more than $10 million in annual sales. The caption: "She knows what young women who love nail polish want because she is a young woman who loves nail polish." The product grew entirely from her own otaku, built into every detail.

## 5. Sneezers

**Definition:** Sneezers are the people who spread the word. They are the transmitters in the ideavirus. They are specifically the early adopters with otaku who take the time to tell their peers about what they've found.

**The critical distinction from ordinary consumers:** A Consumer experiences your product and moves on. A Sneezer experiences your product and tells others. The difference is not demographic — it's motivational. Sneezers feel compelled to share because your product is remarkable enough to be worth sharing, and because they have the social energy and network to do so.

**Godin does not formally subdivide sneezers as much in Purple Cow as in Unleashing the Ideavirus**, but the implicit distinction runs throughout: some sneezers are "powerful" — they are influential in a specific community, and their recommendation carries weight (the early adopter who tech enthusiasts trust); others are "promiscuous" — they tell everyone but carry less credibility per transmission. The powerful sneezers are worth more to you. Design for them first.

**What makes someone a sneezer vs. a consumer:**
- They have otaku in the category — they care deeply enough to form and share opinions
- They have social capital in a community of buyers — their word carries weight
- Your product gave them something genuinely worth saying — a story, an experience, a surprise
- The product makes it easy to share — there's a natural script for talking about it

**Why you cannot manufacture sneezing:** You cannot turn a Consumer into a Sneezer through incentives. Sneezers sneeze because they want to, not because you paid them. If the product isn't remarkable, no incentive program will make people spread it authentically.

**Designing for Sneezers specifically:** Godin argues that the slogan, the name, and the story of the product are all "scripts" for the Sneezer. "A slogan that accurately conveys the essence of your Purple Cow is a script. A script for the sneezer to use when she talks with her friends. The slogan reminds the user, 'Here's why it's worth recommending us; here's why your friends and colleagues will be glad you told them about us.' And best of all, the script guarantees that the word of mouth is passed on properly."

**The Leaning Tower of Pisa test:** The Tower sees millions of visitors. The Pantheon, which is more beautiful, more important, and more significant architecturally, sees 1 percent as many visitors. Why? "The purity of the message makes it even more remarkable. It's easy to tell someone about the Leaning Tower. Much harder to tell them about the Pantheon." If a Sneezer can't summarize your product in ten seconds in a way that makes the listener want to know more, the idea won't travel.

**The Curad case:** Curad challenged Band-Aid by printing characters on bandages. When the first kid with Curad bandages showed up at school, every other kid wanted them. That's sneezing — one kid with a remarkable product becomes the vector for spreading it through the entire peer group. The sneezer didn't need to be convinced to spread the word. The product was the script.

**Sneezers and the "permission" connection:** Godin links sneezers explicitly to permission marketing. Once you have a sneezer who loves you, you need to get their permission to communicate with them again — not to spam them, but "to alert them the next time you might have another Cow." The sneezer becomes your permission asset and your launching pad for the next Purple Cow.

## 6. The Idea Virus (referenced from Unleashing the Ideavirus)

Godin does not detail the full Ideavirus framework in Purple Cow, but he references it repeatedly. The core principle is that ideas spread like viruses — from person to person — and the most effective business ideas are the ones that spread.

**Key mechanics referenced in Purple Cow:**

- **Sneezers are the vectors.** Without someone actively transmitting the idea from person to person, it dies. The idea needs a host who will infect others.
- **The smoothness of the transmission.** Some ideas are easy to share; others are sticky and hard to transmit. The more complex your idea, the less likely it is to spread. The Leaning Tower has one attribute: it leans. It travels perfectly.
- **The velocity of spread.** How fast does the idea move through the population? Remarkable products that live in communities with high otaku spread fast because sneezers are already talking to each other constantly.
- **The persistence.** Does the idea continue to spread after the initial burst? Products with strong permission assets (email lists, loyal communities) have persistence. Products without them fade.

**Pearl Jam as the ideavirus model:** Pearl Jam broke through via traditional channels (working hard, getting lucky, recording hits). Then instead of trying to repeat the mass-market hit model, they rallied their core audience and built a permission asset. Between 2001 and 2002, they released seventy-two live albums on their website — selling to the converted rather than interrupting strangers. The economics: cost of selling to the existing audience is nearly zero, profit on all seventy-two albums. The growth mechanism: a portion of those core fans are so delighted that they take time to induct their friends. "Big fans bring in new fans, and old fans stick around because they're catered to."

## 7. Remarkability — What It Actually Means

**The definition Godin insists on:** Remarkable = "worth remarking about." This is the literal etymology and the precise meaning. A product is remarkable if, upon encountering it, a person feels compelled to remark on it to someone else. Nothing more, nothing less.

**What remarkable is NOT:**

- Not "quality." Quality is a minimum bar, not a differentiator. Airlines that get you there safely are not remarkable.
- Not "very good." "The opposite of remarkable is 'very good.'" The opposite of a Purple Cow is not a bad product — it's a solid, competent, forgettable one.
- Not "popular." Popularity is the result of remarkability, not the definition of it.
- Not necessarily "expensive" or "cheap." Motel 6 and the Four Seasons are both remarkable — for opposite reasons.
- Not necessarily "outrageous." Outrageous for its own sake is just annoying. "Outrageousness by itself won't work because the conversations sneezers have about you aren't positive."

**Case studies in remarkability:**

**Starbucks:** Remarkable not because of the coffee per se, but because of the entire experience it created. In a market dominated by bad diner coffee and percolated office swill, Starbucks redefined what a cup of coffee meant — the experience, the language (venti, grande), the store as a third place. That was the Purple Cow. As Godin notes: "Starbucks was remarkable a few years ago. Now they're boring." But the benefits of that first Cow compounded into thousands of stores worldwide.

**JetBlue:** Remarkably affordable and remarkably pleasant at the same time. They managed to get to the edge of both service and pricing — with a profitable business model. The CEO made one critical decision on day one: got the head of Marketing involved in product design and training, not just advertising. Everything JetBlue does that adds value is marketing. Leather seats on a budget airline. Satellite TV. A culture of genuine service. "Poland Spring starts with worthless water. JetBlue sells a commodity just like American Airlines does, but manages to make a profit doing it. These companies are marketers at their core."

**The Aeron Chair (Herman Miller):** Before the Aeron, desk chairs were invisible. Purchasing departments made the decision. No one cared. Herman Miller introduced a $750 chair in 1994 that looked radically different, felt different, and cost a huge amount. "Everyone who saw it wanted to sit in it, and everyone who sat in it wanted to talk about it." Seth Goldstein, founder of SiteSpecific, spent his very first venture capital check on more than a dozen Aeron chairs — and got on the front page of the Wall Street Journal. The chair is now in the permanent collection of MoMA. "Millions of Aeron chairs have been sold since its introduction in 1994." The lesson from Herman Miller's own designer: "The best design solves problems, but if you can weld that to the cool factor, then you have a home run."

**Dutch Boy Paint:** They changed the can. "Paint cans are heavy, hard to carry, hard to close, hard to open, hard to pour from, and no fun." Dutch Boy realized there was no good reason for this and introduced an easier-to-carry, easier-to-pour-from, easier-to-close paint jug. Sales went up. Distribution increased. Retail price went up. No ad campaign. One remarkably simple product change. The caption on the image (index-95_1.png shows Dutch Boy's new jugs side-by-side): "A few obvious changes in the can meant a huge surge in sales for Dutch Boy. The obvious question: Why did it take so long?"

**Krispy Kreme:** Makes a good donut. But is it worth driving an hour for? Donut maniacs believe it is. When Krispy Kreme opens in a new town, they begin by giving away thousands of donuts to the people who've already heard the legend. Those sneezers immediately bring their friends. Then Krispy Kreme rapidly expands into gas stations and delis — moving from people who will drive twenty miles to people too lazy to cross the street. Crucially: "It's worth noting that this probably wouldn't work with bagels or brownies. There's something very visceral about the obsession that donut fans feel about Krispy Kreme." They found the market niche first, then made the remarkable product — not the other way around.

**Dr. Bronner's:** No advertising. Eccentric packaging covered in thousands of words. You discover it at a friend's house while snooping in the guest bathroom. You start reading. The packaging is so remarkable that people seek it out and proselytize. Its uniqueness is aimed at a specific audience — early adopters who are eager to share it. "In a world without effective ads, it has an unfair advantage against anything the big guys can develop." The Dr. Bronner's bottle (index-82_1.png) is shown in full — the actual product, with dense text covering every surface. Decorated vs. bare: decoration is the product.

**Krispy Kreme vs. Bagels:** This contrast is deliberate. There is a donut otaku but not a bagel otaku. The existence of the otaku precedes the success of the product. Market selection matters as much as product design.

**Sam Adams:** Remarkable in a category (mass-market beer) where the dominant players (Budweiser) had been mass-marketed into blandness. Captured a huge slice of business by being obviously, dramatically different. The craft beer consumer has otaku. Budweiser drinkers do not.

**Logitech:** In the fashion business, not the technology business. The guts of the device don't change often — the functionality and style change constantly. "Logitech doesn't crave more advertising. They crave more remarkable products. That's what their customers want to buy."

**Zara:** Changes its clothing line every three to four weeks. Measures what's working and kills what isn't at a speed that competitors cannot match. Remarkability as a process, not just a one-time event.

**Vanguard:** Remarkably low-cost mutual funds. Continues to "whale away at Fidelity's market dominance." Cost is a form of remarkability if it is dramatically lower — not incrementally cheaper.

**Google vs. Yahoo:** Yahoo had hundreds of words on its homepage. Google obsessively kept it to two buttons ("and one of them was 'I feel lucky'"). Someone emailed Google regularly with only a two-digit number — the current word count on the home page. "Every time he writes, the email contains only a two-digit number... Turns out he's counting the number of words on the home page." That discipline — remarkable restraint in a category full of clutter — was Google's Purple Cow.

**Altoids:** Found young adults who weren't taking up smoking and were looking for something to do with their fingers and mouths. Created a tin that "just begged to be shared." By advertising in urban centers with cutting-edge imagery (not on TV, but where sneezers congregate), Altoids made it easy for early adopters to spread the product. "The result: one of the most profitable candy introductions ever."

**Silk soy milk:** Steve Demos put Silk in a milk-like carton and placed it in the refrigerator at mainstream grocery stores — not in the health food section. Sales tripled almost overnight. The remarkability was not the product itself — it was the positioning decision that changed behavior.

**Enterprise Rental Car:** Hertz and Avis own the airport. Enterprise didn't try to compete there. Instead: they'll pick you up when your car is in the shop or totaled. A completely different Purple Cow in the same category. "Safe, it seems, is risky."

**The Italian Butcher (Dario Cecchini):** Thousands of butchers in Italy. One is famous. One is rich. Cecchini recites Dante, rhapsodizes about beefsteak, and when the EU banned bones in steaks, held a mock funeral and buried a steak in front of his shop with a casket. Is his meat better? "Probably not. But by turning the process of buying meat into an intellectual and political exercise, Dario has figured out more than one way to make money from a cow — this time, a purple one."

**The Häagen-Dazs in Bronxville:** The difference: a stack of business cards on the counter listing the owner's home phone number. "If you have any comments at all about the store, please call me at home." Customers notice. They mention it to each other. Staff is on alert. "Stand in the store for twenty minutes, and you're sure to hear one customer mention the cards to another." A tiny, costless gesture that is remarkable because nobody else does it.

**J. Peterman Catalog (index-84_1.png):** A tiny ad in The New Yorker featuring an illustration of a Western duster coat. The writing was so over-the-top, so idiosyncratic, that it became remarkable enough to be parodied on Seinfeld. "The same word of mouth that can make your product a huge hit can also lead to someone's snickering at you." L.L. Bean and Lands' End are safe and steady and boring — no one parodies them.

**VW Beetle, old vs. new (index-32_1.png and index-32_2.png):**
- index-32_1.png: The classic "Think small" ad — the original Beetle saved by brilliant advertising. A small car in a big-car era, with a campaign that turned the size into a virtue. This was the TV-Industrial Complex at its finest — advertising transformed an ordinary car into an icon.
- index-32_2.png: The new Beetle — a success because of its shape, not its ads. Every time the distinctively round new Beetle drove down a street full of boxy SUVs, it marketed itself. The Purple Cow model. "After marketing the new Beetle for just three years, VW is now offering incentives, new designs, and other features to make the car exciting again. The Purple Cow works, but, alas, it doesn't last as long as good old TV domination did."

## 8. Safe Is Risky — The Inversion

This is the central rhetorical move of the book. Godin states it directly: "The lesson of the Cow is worth repeating: Safe is risky."

**Why playing it safe guarantees failure:**

1. The "safe" choice — an average product for the mass market — already exists. Every category has an entrenched, adequate product that already owns the middle. You cannot displace it by being slightly more adequate.

2. The people you need to reach (early adopters and sneezers) are specifically repelled by safe, average offerings. Safety is the thing that makes them not notice you.

3. Advertising a safe product doesn't help. "These markets waste their marketing dollars. Exhibit A: The dozens of consumer-focused dot-com companies who wasted more than a billion dollars advertising watered-down products to the mass market."

4. Safe products cannot get early adopters to sneeze. And without sneezing, nothing reaches the majority.

**The Buick as the archetype of safe failure:** "The Buick is a boring car. It's been boring for almost fifty years. Few people aspire to own a Buick. The Buick isn't easy to criticize, but it's also not very successful, is it?"

**The footnote Godin adds:** "Except, of course, when being boring is, in and of itself, remarkable." This is a genuine exception — if every competitor is exciting and outrageous, being aggressively restrained is its own form of Purple Cow.

**Companies that played it safe and lost:**
- Maxwell House: had all the brand value in coffee ten years before Starbucks. Played it safe. Now stuck exactly where they were, while Starbucks grew to thousands of stores.
- Motorola and Nokia: Cell phones became boring when everybody had one. "The Purple Cow has left the room, and there's not a lot the cell phone companies can do about it."
- Palm: Made a remarkable product in the early days (single-minded, cheap, exactly right). "Only when Palm tried to play it safe did they start to stumble. Three years in a row of incremental feature creep has cost them market share and profit."
- The record industry: Labels all have "similar pricing, merchant policies, contracts, and packaging. Each label avoids criticism by sticking with the pack." When digital reshuffled the deck, no label had practiced leading.

**The KPMG Consulting full-page Wall Street Journal ad:** Godin reproduces it verbatim. "We have chartered a new beginning. An era of empowerment." No one at the breakfast table could name a single company after reading the entire first section of the Journal. "A committee wrote this ad. A committee approved it. No one will remember it." That is what safe looks like in practice.

**The important nuance:** You cannot know in advance exactly which edge will work. "You can't know if your Purple Cow is guaranteed to work. You can't know if it's remarkable enough or too risky. That's the point. It's the very unpredictability of the outcome that makes it work." The goal is not to eliminate risk — it's to embrace the right kind of risk.

## 9. The Fashion Cycle and the Cow — Remarkability Ages

**The half-life of a Purple Cow:** "Even better, these benefits have a half-life. You don't have to be remarkable all the time to enjoy the upside." Starbucks was remarkable a few years ago. Now they're boring. But that first burst of remarkability compounded into thousands of stores. "Starbucks is unlikely to keep up their blistering growth rate unless they find another Cow."

**The paradox of success:** Once you create a remarkable product and it succeeds, two contradictory pressures emerge:
1. Milk the Cow — extract maximum profit from the existing success.
2. Build the next Cow — invest now in the next remarkable thing, before the current one fades.

These are fundamentally at odds. The profits from a successful Cow create the temptation to coast. The accolades create the illusion that what worked once will work again. "The tempting thing to do is to coast. Take profits. Fail to reinvest. Take no chances because those 'chances' seem to be opportunities to blow the very benefits you worked so hard to earn."

**The failure pattern:** Palm, Yahoo, AOL, Marriott, Marvel Comics — each had a breakthrough, built an empire, and failed to take another risk. Disney coasted for decades. Milton Berle did too. Once you have a hit, the organizational incentives all point toward protecting it, not risking it.

**The Magic Cycle of the Cow — Godin's four-step process:**

1. Get permission from the people you impressed the first time. Not to spam them — "permission to alert them the next time you might have another Cow."

2. Work with the sneezers in that audience to make it easier for them to help the idea cross the chasm. "Give them the tools (and the story) they'll need to sell your idea to a wider audience."

3. Once you've crossed from remarkable to profitable, let a different team milk it. "Productize your services, servicize your products, let a thousand variations bloom. But don't believe your own press releases. This is the inevitable downward slide to commodity. Milk it for all it's worth, and fast."

4. Reinvest. "Do it again. With a vengeance. Launch another Purple Cow (to the same audience). Fail and fail and fail again. Assume that what was remarkable last time won't be remarkable this time."

**The process, not the plan:** "There is no plan. The eventual slowdown of almost every Purple Cow company indicates that there's no rule book." What there is, is a process: "Go for the edges. Challenge yourself and your team to describe what those edges are (not that you'd actually go there), and then test which edge is most likely to deliver the marketing and financial results you seek."

**What "do nothing" teaches:** Ben & Jerry's deliberately did nothing if they didn't have a super-cool flavor or great promotional idea. No 5 percent off sale this week. McIntosh launches a few amplifiers per decade, not per year. "Doing nothing is not as good as doing something (great). But marketing just to keep busy is worse than nothing at all." Mediocre messaging and mediocre products actively corrupt your sneezer network by giving them nothing worth sneezing.

## 10. Questions and Exercises Godin Poses

Godin marks his "top takeaway points" with a symbol throughout. Collecting every question and exercise he explicitly poses:

**On seeing your product's future:**
- "If a product's future is unlikely to be remarkable — if you can't imagine a future in which people are once again fascinated by your product — it's time to realize that the game has changed. Instead of investing in a dying product, take profits and reinvest them in building something new."

**On changing behavior, not just product:**
- "Instead of trying to use your technology and expertise to make a better product for your users' standard behavior, experiment with inviting the users to change their behavior to make the product work dramatically better."

**On measurement:**
- "What could you measure? What would that cost? How fast could you get the results? If you can afford it, try it. 'If you measure it, it will improve.'"

**On the opposite of remarkable:**
- "Are you making very good stuff? How fast can you stop?"

**On packaging as product:**
- "Buy a bottle of Dr. Bronner's. Now, working with your factory and your designers, Bronnify a variation of one of your products."
- "Where does your product end and marketing hype begin? The Dutch Boy can is clearly product, not hype. Can you redefine what you sell in a similar way?"

**On the collectible / parody test:**
- "Could you make a collectible version of your product?"
- "How could you modify your product or service so that you'd show up on the next episode of Saturday Night Live or in a spoof of your industry's trade journal?"

**On following leaders:**
- "What tactics does your firm use that involve following the leader? What if you abandoned them and did something very different instead? If you acknowledge that you'll never catch up by being the same, make a list of ways you can catch up by being different."

**On the marketing budget reallocation:**
- "What would happen if you gave the marketing budget for your next three products to the designers? Could you afford a world-class architect/designer/sculptor/director/author?"

**On the design budget:**
- "Go take a design course. Send your designers to a marketing course. And both of you should spend a week in the factory."

**On finding your otaku audience:**
- "Go to a science fiction convention. These are pretty odd folks. Do you appeal to an audience as wacky and wonderful as this one? How could you create one?"

**On slogan as sneezer script:**
- "Do you have a slogan or positioning statement or remarkable boast that's actually true? Is it consistent? Is it worth passing on?"

**On permission and the email list:**
- "Do you have the email addresses of the 20 percent of your customer base that loves what you do? If not, start getting them. If you do, what could you make for these customers that would be super-special?"
- "All of a sudden, it's obvious why you need a permission asset. If your company doesn't have one yet, you can start today, for free, by using Outlook on your PC. Give people an email address to write to. Write back. You're on your way."

**On the maverick:**
- "If someone in your organization is charged with creating a new Purple Cow, leave them alone! Don't use internal reviews and usability testing to figure out if the new product is as good as what you've got now. Instead, pick the right maverick and get out of the way."

**On modeling remarkable behavior:**
- "Make a list of all the remarkable products in your industry. Who made them? How did they happen? Model the behavior (not mimic the product) and you're more than halfway to making your own."

**On prototyping:**
- "Can you create a culture of aggressively prototyping new products and policies?"

**On honest marketing:**
- "What would happen if you told the truth?"

**On the edges:**
- "Explore the limits. What if you're the cheapest, the fastest, the slowest, the hottest, the coldest, the easiest, the most efficient, the loudest, the most hated, the copycat, the outsider, the hardest, the oldest, the newest, the most! If there's a limit, you should (must) test it."

**The eight "Salt is not boring" exercises:**
1. Think of ten ways to change the product (not the hype) to make it appeal to a sliver of your audience.
2. Think small. Describe a product that overwhelms the smallest conceivable market with its remarkability.
3. Outsource. If the factory resists, go elsewhere. After it works, the factory will be happy to take it back.
4. Build and use a permission asset. Communicate directly with your most loyal customers.
5. Copy — not from your industry, but from any other industry. Find who's remarkable there and do what they did.
6. Go one more. Identify a competitor who is generally regarded as at the edges, and outdo them. Or do the opposite.
7. Find things that are "just not done" in your industry, and do them. JetBlue almost instituted a dress code for passengers. A plastic surgeon could offer gift certificates. Stew Leonard's let customers pick their own strawberries — and sales doubled.
8. Ask "Why not?" Almost everything you don't do has no good reason for it. Almost everything you don't do is the result of fear, inertia, or a historical lack of someone asking "Why not?"

## 11. Case Studies in Full

**Otis Elevator — Going Up?**
Problem: elevators are inefficient — they stop at every floor, creating waits and wasting energy. Otis's solution: a pre-sort panel in the lobby. You key in your floor; the panel tells you which elevator is coming. Every elevator becomes an express. Buildings can be taller, need fewer elevators, wait times shrink. "Is there a significant real estate developer in the world who is unaware of this breakthrough? Not likely." The product change redefined what an elevator company sells. Lesson: invite the user to change their behavior to make the product work dramatically better.

**Tide / P&G — What Should Tide Do?**
Tide is arguably the best laundry detergent in history. But the TV-Industrial Complex is dead, and one chain (Wal-Mart) now accounts for a third of Tide's sales. Are the incremental chemical improvements likely to generate a noteworthy payoff? Godin's verdict: No. "Orthodox Purple Cow thinking would have P&G take the profits while they're still there. Cut research spending, raise the price as much as is practical, and put the incremental profits into the creation of ever more radical and interesting new products."

**Lionel Poilane — The Best Baker in the World**
Inherited a French bakery. Instead of maintaining the status quo, became obsessed with remarkable: interviewed 8,000+ bakers, pioneered organic flour in France, refused to bake baguettes (they're actually a fairly recent import from Vienna), acquired the world's largest collection of bread cookbooks. Refused to hire bakers — hired young men willing to apprentice for years. The French establishment initially rejected him. Then virtually every fancy restaurant in Paris served Poilane bread, and people traveled from all over the world to wait in line at his tiny shop. He sold more than $10 million worth of bread per year.

**Logitech — Fashion, Not Technology**
Logitech succeeds as the fastest-growing technology company by understanding they're in the fashion business. The guts don't change often — the style and user experience change constantly. "Logitech doesn't crave more advertising. They crave more remarkable products."

**Zespri — A New Kind of Kiwi**
New golden kiwi with an edible peel. Rather than mass-market advertising, Zespri targeted Latino foodies specifically — an audience with otaku about exotic produce and who were underserved. No advertising. In-store tasting. Over $100 million in golden kiwi sold per year. "Unless you're Latino, you've probably never even seen it."

**Dario Cecchini — The Italian Butcher**
Thousands of butchers in Italy; one is famous. Cecchini recites Dante, rhapsodizes about beefsteak, held a mock funeral for the Fiorentina steak when the EU banned it. People come from all over the world. Is his meat better? Probably not. The experience is the Purple Cow.

**Dutch Boy Paint**
Changed the can. Introduced an easier-to-carry, easier-to-pour-from, easier-to-close paint jug. Sales rose. Distribution increased. Retail price went up. No advertising needed. "This is marketing done right. Marketing where the marketer changes the product, not the ads."

**Krispy Kreme**
Starts new markets by giving away thousands of donuts to the people who've heard the legend. Sneezers drag their friends. Then rapidly expands into gas stations, coffee shops, delis. "They start with people who will drive twenty miles, and finish with people too lazy to cross the street." The remarkability is the donuts themselves — but the model is seeding with the passionate first.

**Dr. Bronner's**
No advertising. Eccentric, text-covered packaging. Discovery happens in the guest bathroom of a friend who already uses it. The packaging is "a very much part of the experience of using the product." Its uniqueness is aimed specifically at an audience of early adopters who will proselytize.

**Herman Miller / Aeron Chair**
A $750 chair in 1994 when chairs were invisible commodities. Looked different. Worked differently. Cost a lot. "Everyone who saw it wanted to sit in it, and everyone who sat in it wanted to talk about it." Now in the MoMA permanent collection. Millions sold. The Purple Cow investment went into the product, not into media.

**Curad Bandages**
Added character prints to bandages. Kids loved them. When the first kid with Curad bandages showed up at school, every other kid wanted them. Grabbed market share from Band-Aid, one of the strongest household brand institutions.

**Pearl Jam — 72 Live Albums**
Instead of trying to manufacture another mass-market hit, rallied core audience and sold seventy-two live albums on their website — to the converted, not to strangers. Low cost, profitable on all seventy-two. Core fans so delighted they inducted friends. "Big fans bring in new fans, and old fans stick around because they're catered to."

**US Postal Service — ZIP+4**
In one of the most resistant, conservative markets imaginable, ZIP+4 succeeded by: (1) being a genuinely game-changing innovation — not incremental, but completely transforming how bulk mailers targeted and delivered; (2) wisely targeting a few technically savvy early adopters who were sensitive to pricing and speed; those early adopters then sneezed to the rest of the market. "The more intransigent your market, the more crowded the marketplace, the busier your customers, the more you need the Purple Cow."

**McDonald's France**
Subsidized and publicized a report urging French consumers not to visit fast food more than once a week. The US parent professed shock. Godin asks: is this a bad strategy? Perhaps by being honest when talking to customers, the French subsidiary is building a long-term relationship that the American model — "more, more, more, which ultimately leads to a flameout" — cannot sustain. What would happen if you told the truth?

**Best Buy**
Under CEO Brad Anderson: "Instead of selling what we wanted to sell, we sold what people wanted us to sell, and then figured out how to make money doing it. Every time we talked to our customers, they wanted us to follow the path that turned out to be the hardest possible path we could follow. And every time, that path was the right path." Got rid of commissions. Grew from $250 million to more than $23 billion in revenue. "That set the stage for a growth spurt." The store itself was the product change.

**Hallmark.com**
Godin uses this as a brainstorm exercise: three forces at work — permission (people voluntarily come to send e-cards), problem to solve (Gold Crown Club members want more points), and sneezers (Gold Crown members send a ton of cards and their recommendations carry credibility). His proposed idea: when a Gold Crown member is about to send an e-card, offer them a chance to win randomly between 100 and 1 million points by also sending a gift certificate. "Isn't a million points a lot to give away? Exactly. It's Purple."

**Tracey the Publicist**
A publicist who spent years doing excellent but unremarkable work. When she finally decided to specialize — to be the best in the world at doing PR for a specific kind of company — the phone started ringing. Specialization is a form of remarkability in professional services: "I don't care about famous clients from this firm. But if they were known for a specific thing that I need, I'd seek them out." Lesson: in a career, being remarkable works the same way as in products.

**Robyn Waters at Target**
Target's VP of trend, who pushed the organization to make cheap products beautiful. Brought in designers like Michael Graves for housewares. Target's "cheap chic" became a Purple Cow in retail — "neither as cheap as Kmart nor as expensive as a design store, but more stylish than either." Target became worth talking about. Waters understood that design is not luxury — it's a differentiator available at every price point.

**Chip Conley's Phoenix Hotel (San Francisco)**
A small boutique hotel that took a different tack from the Four Seasons and Motel 6 — aimed at musicians and rock bands, decorated accordingly, attracted a specific audience. Every "segment" of the market — even a small one — can be served remarkably. The size of the segment doesn't determine whether it's worth pursuing; the passion of the segment does.

**CDBaby.com / Oasis CD Duplication / Airshow Mastering**
Three companies succeeding in a "dying" industry (the record business) by being remarkably focused on their niche: independent musicians. CDBaby sells the work of thousands of independent acts "with such success (and treating his partners with such respect) that word of mouth is the only advertising he needs to attract new musicians and new customers." Lesson: targeting a thriving niche in a slow-moving industry can work — "if you're prepared to invest what it takes to be remarkable."

**The Bloomberg Terminal (index-138_1.png)**
The image shows the Bloomberg terminal interface — a complex, dense, command-line-style screen full of menus, codes, and financial data. Should have been replaced by the internet (free, easy, comprehensive). Instead, it's on the desk of just about every important player on Wall Street — at more than $1,000 per month. Why? Because it's very expensive and very difficult to use. "Traders and other investors insist on it. They've gone through the pain of learning how to use it, and they're not prepared to give up that expertise." The learning curve itself is the moat. Complexity, in a market of experts, is the Purple Cow.

## 12. Permission Marketing Connection

Godin is explicit about the relationship between Purple Cow and his earlier book, Permission Marketing: the Purple Cow is the entry point; permission is the asset that enables the cycle to repeat.

**How they connect:** The first Purple Cow impresses a set of early adopters. Those early adopters sneeze to the early majority. But the lasting business value comes from capturing the permission of the people who were impressed — their consent to hear from you again. "Get permission from people you impressed the first time. Not permission to spam them or sell them leftovers or squeeze extra margins from them. Permission to alert them the next time you might have another Cow."

**Why you need a permission asset:** Without it, you have to start from zero every time. With it, you have a launching pad for the next Purple Cow — an audience that already trusts you, is already open to hearing from you, and has already demonstrated they're the kind of sneezer who responds to remarkable things.

**Pearl Jam as the model:** The email list, the website, the direct-to-fan relationship. They can release seventy-two albums because they have permission to talk to their core fans. The fans have opted in. The cost of reaching them is nearly zero.

**The practical implementation Godin suggests:** "If your company doesn't have a permission asset yet, you can start today, for free, by using Outlook on your PC. Give people an email address to write to. Write back. You're on your way."

**The deeper point:** The TV-Industrial Complex was built on interruption — reaching people who hadn't asked to be reached. The post-TV world is built on permission — reaching people who asked to hear from you. The Purple Cow is what earns permission in the first place. "Companies like JetBlue, Starbucks, Hasbro, and Poland Spring have to be run by marketers." Marketing is not communication — it's the product itself.

**The cycle:**
Purple Cow (remarkable product) → earns attention of early adopters → sneezers spread to early majority → capture permission of fans → use permission to launch next Purple Cow → repeat

The "Magic Cycle" is not a one-time event. It's a discipline.

## 13. How to Apply to iOS Apps

This section is synthesis — applying Godin directly to the founder's pipeline.

### Stage 1: Ideation — Is This a Purple Cow?

**1. The remarkability filter is mandatory.** Before committing to any app concept, the founder must be able to answer: "Would a person who discovered this app tell their friends about it unprompted?" If the honest answer is no, the concept is not ready. "Good enough" is a category killer — in the App Store, good enough is invisible.

**2. The "copy + differentiate" rule needs a Purple Cow test.** The system's core philosophy is to copy a successful app and change one or two things. That's right, but the Purple Cow constraint clarifies what to change: change the one thing that makes it worth remarking about. Not the UI polish. Not the onboarding flow. The thing that makes a user DM a friend: "Have you seen this app? It does this."

**3. Who is the Otaku for your category? Find them before you build.** Every app category has a group with otaku and a group without. Sleep trackers have an audience of people mildly interested in sleep — but they also have a hardcore biohacker audience with genuine otaku. Anxiety apps have casual users, but also an audience of therapists, coaches, and people in active CBT treatment with real obsessiveness about their condition. The app must be designed for the otaku audience first. The casual users come later, via the sneezers with otaku.

**4. The Krispy Kreme principle: find the niche first, then make the remarkable product.** Godin says it explicitly: "find the market niche first, and then make the remarkable product — not the other way around." Don't start with "I want to build a meditation app." Start with "who has otaku around meditation, and what does that audience desperately need that no current app provides?"

**5. The mustard test: does your category have otaku?** Some app categories have deeply passionate users (fitness tracking, language learning, anything with streaks, journaling for mental health, specific diet systems like keto or fasting). Others are mustard categories — everyone uses them but nobody cares deeply enough to seek out and evangelise the best one. If you're in a mustard category, you need to create an artificial otaku by being so dramatically different that a new community forms around your product.

**6. Remarkable is not "better" on the dimensions that already exist.** An app with a slightly better sleep tracking algorithm is not a Purple Cow. An app that phones your partner if your sleep drops below 6 hours is. An app that locks your TikTok at bedtime based on your heart rate is. The question is: what is the one thing this app does that no other app does, and is that thing surprising enough to make someone pull out their phone and show their friend?

**7. The "old Beetle vs. new Beetle" test.** The old Beetle (original concept) needed advertising to succeed. The new Beetle (redesigned shape) marketed itself every time it drove down the road. Ask: does your app market itself? Does the experience of using it create a story worth telling? If someone screenshots your app, does that screenshot tell a story worth posting?

### Stage 2: Viral Growth — TikTok and the Sneezer Strategy

**8. TikTok is not an ad platform — it's a sneezer platform.** The founder's growth model (TikTok + Instagram organic) is precisely the post-TV-Industrial-Complex strategy Godin describes. The difference: in the Purple Cow model, you're not trying to reach everyone on TikTok — you're trying to reach the 1,000 people with otaku in your category who will then tell the next 100,000. Seed with passion, not volume.

**9. Identify the otaku communities on TikTok before posting.** Where do the sneezers in your category live? For a sleep app: #sleeptok, #biohacking, #circadianrhythm. For an anxiety app: #therapytok, #anxietytips, #CBT. For a fitness app: the specific subculture (powerlifting, running, HIIT). Build the first 100 users from those communities specifically — not from generic health content.

**10. Design the app's ONE most remarkable feature and lead with it.** Every TikTok video the founder makes should be about the one thing that is most worth remarking about. Not a feature list. Not "5 reasons to use our app." The one thing a person would immediately want to show a friend. If you don't have that one thing, you don't have a Purple Cow.

**11. The sneezer script must be pre-built.** Godin says explicitly that your slogan is a script for the sneezer. In the TikTok context, this means: the app itself must create a moment so concrete, so surprising, or so funny that it naturally becomes shareable video content. The Dutch Boy paint jug was a script: "They changed the can." Your app needs its Dutch Boy moment. What did you change that was so obvious in retrospect, users will film themselves reacting to it?

**12. 100 passionate users beats 10,000 passive ones.** The early adopter funnel: get the people with otaku first, convert them to sneezers, then let them bring in the early majority. Passive installs from broad paid campaigns do not generate sneezing. Passionate installs from the right community generate sneezing even without being asked.

**13. The remarkability test before every TikTok video:** "Would someone DM this to a friend?" If no — it's content, not a Purple Cow. Good content builds followers slowly. Purple Cow content creates sneezers who actively recruit new users.

### Stage 4: UI/UX — Designing Remarkability In

**14. Marketing is design; design is marketing.** Godin's clearest line on this: "Marketing is the act of inventing the product. The effort of designing it. The craft of producing it. The art of pricing it." The UI/UX decisions made in Stage 4 are marketing decisions. Every screen either contributes to or undermines the app's remarkability.

**15. The Dr. Bronner principle: the experience itself should be the thing people remark about.** Dr. Bronner's is not a better shampoo. It's a shampoo that people discover in a friend's bathroom and immediately show to whoever walks in next. The app's experience should create that same moment: "Have you used this? Come look at this." What is the moment in your app's UX that produces that response?

**16. Compromise is the enemy of the Purple Cow in design.** "A camel is a horse designed by a committee." When features are added to please everyone, the result pleases nobody enough to remark about. Cut to the one remarkable thing. Build everything else around making that one thing more remarkable.

**17. The slogan / tagline is a sneezer script.** Every app needs a short, true, remarkable statement that a user can repeat in ten seconds and make someone else want to install it. Not "the best sleep app." Something like the Leaning Tower — one thing, pure, easy to transmit. Design this before designing screens.

**18. The Curad insight for app design: collectibility.** Curad put characters on bandages and kids showed them off. What in your app is a "collectible" — a badge, a streak, a visual, a score — that users are proud to show off? Design the sharing moment intentionally.

### Stage 10: App Store — Positioning as a Purple Cow

**19. Remarkable apps create their own categories.** The most powerful App Store positioning is a new category you define, not the existing category you compete in. If you're in "Sleep Tracking," you're competing with Sleep Cycle, Oura, and dozens of others. If you're in "Sleep Accountability" or "Partner Sleep Sync," you're in a category of one. Dutch Boy didn't sell better paint — they sold a fundamentally different container experience.

**20. The subtitle is the sneezer script.** The App Store subtitle (30 characters) is the Leaning Tower. One thing, pure, remarkable, impossible to misunderstand. Not "Your wellness companion." Something specific and surprising.

**21. Screenshot 1 must be remarkable, not descriptive.** Most apps use the first screenshot to show the home screen with a caption. That is the boring strategy. The remarkable strategy is to show the one thing that makes the app worth downloading — the Dutch Boy moment — in a way that makes someone stop scrolling.

**22. True Facts + App Store: the word-of-mouth brands win.** Godin's analysis of the Interbrand top 100 shows that the brands with lasting value were built through word of mouth, not advertising. The App Store equivalents are the apps people recommend to their friends: Duolingo, Calm, Headspace, Strava. Each of these was built on a remarkable core feature that early adopters talked about — the streak mechanic, the sleep stories, the guided runs. Not on the advertising campaigns that followed.

### Universal — The Lifecycle of the Purple Cow

**23. Plan for the Cow to age.** The new Beetle was remarkable for three years before VW started offering incentives. Starbucks was remarkable before it became ubiquitous. Your app's Purple Cow feature has a half-life. Build the permission asset (email list, push notification consent, community) during the peak of the Cow so you have a launching pad for the next one.

**24. The Magic Cycle applied to app development:**
- Launch the Purple Cow feature (the one remarkable thing).
- Get permission from the early adopters who love it (notification opt-in, newsletter, community).
- Let the sneezers spread it; help them with the script.
- Once it's profitable, let the team optimize it (A/B test the paywall, improve the onboarding). Don't let "optimization" dilute the remarkability.
- Reinvest in the next Purple Cow feature. Assume what was remarkable at launch will be expected in six months.

**25. The "sit there, don't just do something" rule for updates.** Ben & Jerry's only released a new flavor when they had something genuinely remarkable. McIntosh launches amplifiers per decade, not per year. Before pushing an update, ask: does this update add to the app's remarkability, or does it just add more features? Mediocre updates corrupt the sneezer network by giving users nothing worth talking about — and potentially diluting what they were already talking about.

**26. The otaku question is the same as the Hormozi pain question.** The system's current core philosophy — "Pain is the pitch" — aligns precisely with finding the otaku. The people with otaku are the people whose pain is acute enough that they are actively seeking a solution. The difference: Hormozi tells you to articulate the pain; Godin tells you to make the solution so remarkable that the articulation becomes word-of-mouth.

**27. Designing for the left side of the diffusion curve vs. designing for the middle.** In Stage 4, every design decision creates a product that either appeals to the early adopters (who will sneeze) or to the early majority (who won't listen until their peers sneeze). Features designed to be maximally accessible — smooth, bland, safe — are features designed for the early majority, who won't encounter the product until the early adopters have already told them about it. Feature design should prioritize the Sneezer's experience first.

**28. The permission asset is the app's retention mechanism and its marketing.** Godin's four-step Magic Cycle starts with getting permission from the people impressed by the first Cow. In the app world, this means: push notifications, email capture, community. Not to spam — to alert them when the next remarkable feature ships. The "permission asset" is not separate from retention strategy; it is the retention strategy.

**29. The "very good is bad" test for every stage.** At each stage gate, before proceeding, ask: is what we're building merely very good, or is it genuinely remarkable? If the honest answer is "very good" — stop and find the remarkable angle before building. The App Store is full of "very good." It has almost no Purple Cows.

**30. The one framework-to-framework connection:** Purple Cow tells you what to build and who to build it for. Made to Stick (Heath) tells you how to communicate it once you've built it. Contagious (Berger) tells you the social mechanics by which it spreads. Hooked (Eyal) tells you how to keep users once they arrive. The pipeline stages map these books to stages: Ideation (Purple Cow + Contagious), Viral Growth (Purple Cow + Contagious), UI/UX (Made to Stick + Hooked), App Store (Made to Stick). A concept that fails the Purple Cow filter fails all the downstream stages automatically. No hook, no retention mechanic, no App Store optimization rescues an unremarkable idea from invisibility.
